## Electromagnetic Induction

> When a conductor **moves through a magnetic field** (or a magnetic field changes around a conductor), an **EMF (electromotive force) is induced** in the conductor. If the circuit is complete, a **current flows**.

This is **Faraday's Law** of electromagnetic induction.

---

## Conditions for Induction

An EMF is induced when:
- A conductor **cuts through** magnetic field lines (relative motion between conductor and magnet)
- The **magnetic flux** through a coil **changes**

> ⚠️ If the conductor is stationary and the field is constant → NO induction

---

## Factors Affecting the Size of Induced EMF

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Speed of movement | Faster → larger EMF |
| Strength of magnet | Stronger → larger EMF |
| Number of turns in coil | More turns → larger EMF |
| Area of coil | Larger area → larger EMF |

---

## Direction of Induced Current — Lenz's Law

> The induced current always flows in a direction that **opposes the change that caused it** (Lenz's Law).

### Fleming's Right-Hand Rule (Generator Rule)

Used to find the direction of induced current:
- **F**irst finger → **F**ield (N to S)
- Thu**M**b → **M**otion (direction of movement)
- Se**C**ond finger → **C**urrent (direction of induced conventional current)

> 💡 Right Hand = Generator (uses motion to produce electricity)

## The AC Generator (Alternator)

Converts **mechanical energy → electrical energy** (AC).

### Construction
- Rectangular coil rotating in a magnetic field
- Connected to external circuit via **slip rings** and **carbon brushes**

### How it Works
1. Coil rotates in magnetic field
2. Sides of coil cut field lines → EMF induced
3. As coil rotates, rate of cutting changes → sinusoidal AC output
4. Slip rings allow continuous rotation (no reversal of current direction)

### Output Waveform
- **Sinusoidal** (sine wave)
- EMF is maximum when coil is **horizontal** (parallel to field — cutting most lines)
- EMF is zero when coil is **vertical** (perpendicular to field — not cutting lines)

### Increasing Output of AC Generator
- Rotate coil faster → higher frequency AND higher peak EMF
- Stronger magnets → higher peak EMF
- More turns in coil → higher peak EMF

---

## The DC Generator

- Same as AC generator but uses a **split-ring commutator** instead of slip rings
- Reverses connections every half turn → output always same direction (DC)
- Output is "bumpy" DC (varies but doesn't reverse)

---

## Transformers

> A **transformer** changes the **voltage** of an alternating current.

### Construction
- **Primary coil** (input) and **secondary coil** (output)
- Both wound on a **soft iron core**
- Core channels magnetic flux between the coils

### How it Works
1. AC in primary coil → changing magnetic field in iron core
2. Changing magnetic field reaches secondary coil → induces EMF
3. **Only works with AC** (DC produces constant field → no change → no induction)

### Transformer Equation

$$\frac{V_p}{V_s} = \frac{N_p}{N_s}$$

- $V_p$ = primary voltage (V)
- $V_s$ = secondary voltage (V)
- $N_p$ = number of turns in primary coil
- $N_s$ = number of turns in secondary coil

### Types of Transformers

| Type | Turns | Voltage | Current |
|------|-------|---------|---------|
| **Step-up** | $N_s > N_p$ | $V_s > V_p$ | $I_s < I_p$ |
| **Step-down** | $N_s < N_p$ | $V_s < V_p$ | $I_s > I_p$ |

### Power in an Ideal Transformer

$$P_{input} = P_{output}$$
$$V_p I_p = V_s I_s$$

$$\frac{I_p}{I_s} = \frac{V_s}{V_p} = \frac{N_s}{N_p}$$

> In a **real** transformer, some energy is lost as heat in the coils and core.

### Transformer Efficiency
$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{V_s I_s}{V_p I_p} \times 100\%$$

**Ways to improve transformer efficiency:**
- Use thicker wire (lower resistance → less heat loss)
- Laminate the iron core (reduces eddy current losses)
- Better core material

## The National Grid

The **National Grid** transmits electricity from power stations to homes and industry across the country.

### Why High Voltage for Transmission?

$$P_{loss} = I^2 R$$

- High voltage → low current (since $P = IV$ and P is constant)
- Low current → less power lost as heat in cables ($P = I^2 R$)

### National Grid Process

```
Power station → Step-up transformer → High voltage cables
(~25,000 V)     (→ ~400,000 V)       (low current, low loss)

→ Step-down transformer → Homes (230 V) / Industry (higher V)
```

| Stage | Voltage | Why |
|-------|---------|-----|
| Power station output | ~25 kV | Generated here |
| Transmission lines | ~400 kV | Minimise energy loss |
| Industrial users | ~33 kV | Stepped down |
| Domestic homes | 230 V | Safe for home use |
