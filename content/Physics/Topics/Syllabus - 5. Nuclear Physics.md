## 5.1 The Nuclear Atom

### Structure of the Atom

| Particle | Location | Relative Charge | Relative Mass |
|----------|----------|----------------|--------------|
| Proton | Nucleus | +1 | 1 |
| Neutron | Nucleus | 0 | 1 |
| Electron | Shells/orbits | −1 | 1/1836 (≈ 0) |

- The **nucleus** is tiny compared to the atom
- Most of the atom is **empty space**
- The nucleus contains almost all the **mass**

### Key Numbers

$$^A_Z X$$

- $A$ = **mass number** (protons + neutrons)
- $Z$ = **atomic number** (number of protons)
- Number of neutrons = $A - Z$

**Example:** $^{12}_6C$ = Carbon-12 → 6 protons, 6 neutrons, 6 electrons

### Isotopes
- Atoms of the **same element** with the same number of protons but **different numbers of neutrons**
- Same chemical properties (same electrons)
- Different physical properties (different masses)

**Examples:**
- $^1_1H$, $^2_1H$ (deuterium), $^3_1H$ (tritium)
- $^{235}_{92}U$, $^{238}_{92}U$

### Nuclide Notation
- A **nuclide** is a specific type of nucleus defined by its proton and neutron numbers
- **Radioactive nuclides** are unstable and decay

## 5.2 Radioactivity

### What is Radioactivity?
- The **spontaneous** and **random** emission of radiation from unstable nuclei
- **Spontaneous** = not triggered by external factors
- **Random** = cannot predict when a particular nucleus will decay

### The Three Types of Radiation

| Property | Alpha (α) | Beta (β) | Gamma (γ) |
|----------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Nature | Helium nucleus $^4_2He$ | Fast electron $^0_{-1}e$ | EM radiation (photon) |
| Charge | +2 | −1 | 0 |
| Mass | 4 | ~0 | 0 |
| Speed | Slow (~5% c) | Fast (~90% c) | Speed of light (c) |
| Penetrating power | Low | Medium | High |
| Range in air | ~5 cm | ~1 m | Kilometres |
| Stopped by | Paper / skin | 3mm aluminium | Thick lead / concrete |
| Ionising ability | **High** | Medium | **Low** |

### Deflection in Fields
- **Alpha:** Deflected by electric and magnetic fields (positive charge)
- **Beta:** Deflected in opposite direction to alpha (negative charge)
- **Gamma:** Not deflected (no charge)

### Nuclear Decay Equations

**Alpha decay:**
$$^A_Z X \rightarrow ^{A-4}_{Z-2} Y + ^4_2\alpha$$
- Mass number decreases by 4
- Atomic number decreases by 2

**Beta decay:**
$$^A_Z X \rightarrow ^A_{Z+1} Y + ^0_{-1}\beta$$
- Mass number unchanged
- Atomic number increases by 1
- A neutron converts to a proton + electron

**Gamma emission:**
- No change in mass number or atomic number
- Nucleus just loses energy as gamma radiation

### Radioactive Decay Series
- A nucleus may undergo several decays before becoming stable
- e.g. Uranium-238 → eventually → Lead-206

## Half-Life

### Definition
> The **half-life** is the time taken for **half** the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay.

- It is **constant** for a given isotope
- It is **independent** of temperature, pressure, chemical state

### Half-Life Calculations
| Time elapsed | Fraction remaining | Amount remaining (if started with N) |
|-------------|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| 0 | 1 | N |
| 1 half-life | 1/2 | N/2 |
| 2 half-lives | 1/4 | N/4 |
| 3 half-lives | 1/8 | N/8 |
| n half-lives | $(1/2)^n$ | $N \times (1/2)^n$ |

**Example:** A sample has 80g of a radioactive isotope with half-life of 10 years.
- After 10 years → 40g
- After 20 years → 20g
- After 30 years → 10g

### Activity
- **Activity** = number of decays per second
- Unit: **Becquerel (Bq)** = 1 decay per second
- Activity also halves every half-life

## Background Radiation

### Sources of Background Radiation
- **Natural:** Radon gas, rocks (granite), cosmic rays, food/drink, living things
- **Artificial:** Medical X-rays, nuclear power stations, nuclear weapons testing

> Background radiation must always be subtracted from measurements to get the true count rate.

## Uses of Radioactivity

| Use | Type used | Reason |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Medical tracers | Gamma (short half-life) | Penetrates body, decays quickly |
| Cancer treatment | Gamma | Kills cancer cells |
| Thickness gauges | Beta | Absorbed proportionally by material |
| Smoke detectors | Alpha | Ionises air to detect smoke |
| Carbon dating | Beta (C-14) | Known half-life (~5730 years) |
| Sterilising food/equipment | Gamma | Kills bacteria without heating |

## Dangers of Radiation

### Health Risks
- Ionising radiation can damage or kill cells
- Can cause **mutations** in DNA → cancer
- High doses → radiation sickness, death

### Safety Precautions
- Keep distance from source (intensity ∝ 1/distance²)
- Minimise exposure time
- Use shielding (lead, concrete)
- Use tongs/gloves, never handle directly
- Store in lead-lined containers

### Nuclear Fission
- Heavy nucleus (e.g. U-235) splits into two smaller nuclei when hit by a neutron
- Releases **large amounts of energy** and more neutrons
- More neutrons → more fissions → **chain reaction**
- Controlled in **nuclear reactors** → electricity generation
- Uncontrolled → **nuclear bomb**

### Nuclear Fusion
- Two light nuclei (e.g. hydrogen) combine to form a heavier nucleus
- Releases **even more energy** than fission
- Requires **extremely high temperature** (~10⁷ K)
- Powers the Sun and stars
- Not yet practical for power generation on Earth (being researched — ITER project)
