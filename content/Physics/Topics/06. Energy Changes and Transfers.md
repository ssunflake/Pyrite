## Forms of Energy

| Energy Store | Description | Example |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **Kinetic** | Energy of moving objects | Moving car, flowing water |
| **Gravitational potential** | Energy due to height in a gravitational field | Book on a shelf |
| **Elastic potential** | Energy stored in stretched/compressed objects | Compressed spring, drawn bow |
| **Chemical** | Energy stored in chemical bonds | Fuel, food, batteries |
| **Thermal (heat)** | Energy of particles due to their temperature | Hot object |
| **Nuclear** | Energy stored in atomic nuclei | Radioactive materials, nuclear fuel |
| **Electrical** | Energy of moving charges | Current in a circuit |
| **Light (radiant)** | Energy carried by electromagnetic radiation | Sunlight, laser |
| **Sound** | Energy carried by mechanical waves | Music, vibrations |

---

## Conservation of Energy

> **Energy cannot be created or destroyed.** It can only be transferred from one store to another.

$$\text{Total energy in} = \text{Total energy out}$$

- Every energy transfer has some energy "wasted" as **thermal energy**
- The total energy is always conserved

### Energy Transfer Diagrams
- Use **Sankey diagrams** to show energy transfers
- Width of arrow represents amount of energy
- Useful energy goes forward; wasted energy usually goes downward

---

## Kinetic Energy

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

- $E_k$ = kinetic energy (J)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $v$ = speed (m/s)

> Doubling speed → quadruples kinetic energy (v² relationship!)

---

## Gravitational Potential Energy

$$E_p = mgh$$

- $E_p$ = gravitational potential energy (J)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $g$ = gravitational field strength (N/kg) ≈ 10 N/kg
- $h$ = height above reference point (m)

---

## Conservation of Energy in Mechanics

When an object falls (ignoring air resistance):

$$\text{GPE lost} = \text{KE gained}$$

$$mgh = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

This gives: $v = \sqrt{2gh}$

### Example — roller coaster
- At top of hill: maximum GPE, minimum KE
- At bottom of hill: minimum GPE, maximum KE
- In reality, some energy is lost to friction/air resistance as heat

---

## Work Done

$$W = Fd$$

- $W$ = work done (J)
- $F$ = force applied in the direction of motion (N)
- $d$ = distance moved in the direction of the force (m)

> **Work is only done when a force causes motion in the direction of the force.**

If force and motion are not parallel:
$$W = Fd\cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between force and displacement.

**1 joule = 1 newton × 1 metre**

### Work and Energy
- Work done ON an object = energy transferred TO it
- Work done BY an object = energy transferred FROM it

---

## Elastic Potential Energy

Energy stored in a stretched/compressed spring:

$$E_{elastic} = \frac{1}{2}ke^2$$

- $k$ = spring constant (N/m)
- $e$ = extension (m)

> This is also equal to the area under a force-extension graph.

---

## Efficiency

$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful energy output}}{\text{total energy input}} \times 100\%$$

Or with power:
$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful power output}}{\text{total power input}} \times 100\%$$

- Efficiency is always < 100% (some energy always wasted)
- Wasted energy is usually heat or sound
- Efficiency has no units (it's a ratio / percentage)

### Improving Efficiency
- Better insulation
- Lubrication to reduce friction
- Streamlining to reduce air resistance
- LED bulbs instead of incandescent bulbs

---

## Sankey Diagrams

- Arrows show energy flows
- Width proportional to energy amount
- Useful output: main arrow going forward
- Wasted output: smaller arrows going sideways/down

**Example — light bulb (20% efficient):**
```
100 J electrical energy →  [BULB]  → 20 J light energy
                                   → 80 J heat energy
```
