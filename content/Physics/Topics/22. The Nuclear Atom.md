## Structure of the Atom

| Particle | Location | Charge | Relative Mass |
|----------|----------|--------|--------------|
| **Proton** | Nucleus | +1 | 1 |
| **Neutron** | Nucleus | 0 | 1 |
| **Electron** | Shells/orbitals (outside nucleus) | −1 | ~1/1836 (≈ 0) |

- The **nucleus** is tiny compared to the atom (~10⁻¹⁵ m vs ~10⁻¹⁰ m for atom)
- Most of the atom is **empty space**
- The nucleus contains virtually all the **mass**
- The atom is electrically **neutral** — equal numbers of protons and electrons

## Nuclide Notation

$$^A_Z X$$

| Symbol | Name | Meaning |
|--------|------|---------|
| $A$ | Mass number (nucleon number) | Total number of protons + neutrons |
| $Z$ | Atomic number (proton number) | Number of protons |
| $X$ | Chemical symbol | Element |

**Number of neutrons** = $A - Z$

### Examples

| Nuclide | Protons | Neutrons | Electrons |
|---------|---------|----------|-----------|
| $^{12}_6\text{C}$ | 6 | 6 | 6 |
| $^{14}_6\text{C}$ | 6 | 8 | 6 |
| $^{238}_{92}\text{U}$ | 92 | 146 | 92 |
| $^{1}_1\text{H}$ | 1 | 0 | 1 |

## Isotopes

> **Isotopes** are atoms of the **same element** (same atomic number Z) with **different numbers of neutrons** (different mass numbers A).

- Same number of **protons** → same **element**, same **chemical properties**
- Different number of **neutrons** → different **mass**, different **nuclear stability**
- Some isotopes are **stable**; others are **radioactive** (unstable → decay)

### Examples of Isotopes

| Element | Isotope 1 | Isotope 2 | Isotope 3 |
|---------|----------|----------|----------|
| Hydrogen | $^1_1\text{H}$ | $^2_1\text{H}$ (deuterium) | $^3_1\text{H}$ (tritium) |
| Carbon | $^{12}_6\text{C}$ (stable) | $^{14}_6\text{C}$ (radioactive) | |
| Uranium | $^{235}_{92}\text{U}$ | $^{238}_{92}\text{U}$ | |

## The Rutherford Scattering Experiment

### The Experiment (1909 — Geiger and Marsden, supervised by Rutherford)
- Fired **alpha particles** at a thin **gold foil**
- Detected where alpha particles went using a fluorescent screen

### Observations
| Observation | Proportion |
|-------------|-----------|
| Most alpha particles passed straight through | ~99.9% |
| Some deflected by small angles | Small fraction |
| A few bounced almost straight back | Very small fraction (~1 in 8000) |

### Conclusions
| Observation | Conclusion |
|-------------|-----------|
| Most passed straight through | Atom is mostly empty space |
| Some deflected | A positive nucleus exists |
| A few bounced back | Nucleus is very small, dense and positively charged |

> Before this experiment, the accepted model was Thomson's **'plum pudding' model** — positive charge spread throughout the atom with electrons embedded in it.

### The Nuclear Model (Rutherford Model)
- Tiny, dense, positively charged **nucleus** at the centre
- Electrons orbit the nucleus at relatively large distances
- Atom is mostly empty space

## Electron Shells

- Electrons occupy **shells** (energy levels) around the nucleus
- Shells closer to nucleus have **lower energy**
- Electrons fill lowest available energy level first
- Maximum electrons per shell: 2, 8, 8, 18, ...

### Shell Notation (first 20 elements)

| Element | Atomic no. | Shell structure |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| Hydrogen | 1 | 1 |
| Helium | 2 | 2 |
| Carbon | 6 | 2, 4 |
| Sodium | 11 | 2, 8, 1 |
| Chlorine | 17 | 2, 8, 7 |
| Calcium | 20 | 2, 8, 8, 2 |

## Nuclear Forces

- **Electrostatic repulsion:** protons repel each other (like charges)
- **Strong nuclear force:** very short-range force that holds protons and neutrons together in nucleus; stronger than electrostatic repulsion at very short range
- The balance between these forces determines nuclear stability

### Stability
- Nuclei with too many or too few neutrons relative to protons are **unstable**
- Unstable nuclei undergo **radioactive decay** to become more stable
