## Electric Current

> **Current** is the rate of flow of electric charge.

$$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$

- $I$ = current (A, amperes)
- $Q$ = charge (C, coulombs)
- $t$ = time (s)

- Current is carried by **electrons** in metals (electron flow)
- **Conventional current** flows from **positive to negative** (opposite to actual electron flow)
- Measured with an **ammeter** (connected in **series**)

### Direct Current (DC) vs Alternating Current (AC)

| Feature | DC | AC |
|---------|----|----|
| Current direction | One way | Alternates back and forth |
| Source | Batteries, solar cells | Mains electricity, generators |
| Frequency (AC) | N/A | 50 Hz (UK/India), 60 Hz (USA) |
| Voltage (UK mains) | N/A | ~230 V |

## Potential Difference (Voltage)

> **Potential difference (p.d.)** is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points.

$$V = \frac{W}{Q}$$

- $V$ = potential difference (V, volts)
- $W$ = energy/work done (J)
- $Q$ = charge (C)

- 1 volt = 1 joule per coulomb
- Measured with a **voltmeter** (connected in **parallel**)

### EMF vs Terminal Voltage
- **EMF (electromotive force):** energy given to each coulomb by the source (battery/cell)
- **Terminal voltage:** voltage measured across battery terminals when current flows
- Terminal voltage < EMF because of **internal resistance**

$$\text{EMF} = V_{terminal} + Ir$$

- $r$ = internal resistance of battery

## Resistance

> **Resistance** is the opposition to the flow of electric current.

$$R = \frac{V}{I}$$

- $R$ = resistance (Ω, ohms)
- $V$ = potential difference (V)
- $I$ = current (A)

### Ohm's Law

> For a metallic conductor at **constant temperature**, the current through it is directly proportional to the potential difference across it.

$$V = IR \quad (V \propto I)$$

- A component that obeys Ohm's Law is called an **ohmic conductor**

---

## I–V Characteristics

### Ohmic Conductor (Resistor)
- Straight line through origin
- Constant resistance at all voltages

### Filament Lamp (Bulb)
- Curved line — slope decreases at higher V
- Resistance **increases** as temperature increases (wire heats up)
- Not ohmic

### Diode
- Current only flows in **one direction** (forward bias)
- Reverse bias → very high resistance → almost no current
- Used to convert AC to DC (rectification)

### Thermistor (NTC)
- Resistance **decreases** as temperature **increases**
- NTC = Negative Temperature Coefficient
- Used in temperature sensors, thermostats

### LDR (Light Dependent Resistor)
- Resistance **decreases** as light intensity **increases**
- Used in light sensors, automatic lights, burglar alarms

---

## Factors Affecting Resistance of a Wire

$$R = \frac{\rho L}{A}$$

- $\rho$ = resistivity of material (Ω m)
- $L$ = length of wire (m)
- $A$ = cross-sectional area (m²)

| Factor | Effect on resistance |
|--------|---------------------|
| Length increases | Resistance increases |
| Cross-sectional area increases | Resistance decreases |
| Temperature increases (metals) | Resistance increases |
| Temperature increases (thermistor) | Resistance decreases |
| Material | Depends on resistivity |

## Electrical Power

$$P = IV = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$

- $P$ = power (W)
- $I$ = current (A)
- $V$ = voltage (V)
- $R$ = resistance (Ω)

---

## Electrical Energy

$$E = Pt = IVt = I^2Rt$$

- $E$ = energy (J)
- $P$ = power (W)
- $t$ = time (s)

### Kilowatt-Hour
- Practical unit for electrical energy
- $1 \text{ kWh} = 3.6 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$
- Used for electricity billing

**Cost calculation:**
$$\text{Cost} = \text{Power (kW)} \times \text{Time (h)} \times \text{Price per kWh}$$

## Household Electricity Safety

### The Three-Pin Plug (UK/India standard)
| Wire | Colour | Function |
|------|--------|---------|
| Live | **Brown** | Carries current at high voltage (~230 V) |
| Neutral | **Blue** | Return path at ~0 V |
| Earth | **Green/Yellow** | Safety — connected to metal casing |

### Fuses
- A **fuse** is a thin wire that **melts** if current exceeds its rating → breaks circuit
- Protects against short circuits and overloading
- Always in the **live** wire
- Ratings: 1 A, 3 A, 5 A, 13 A
- Choose fuse slightly **above** normal operating current

**Which fuse to choose?**
$$I = \frac{P}{V}$$
Choose next fuse rating above this calculated current.

### Circuit Breakers (MCBs)
- Electromagnetic switch that trips when current too high
- Can be **reset** (unlike fuse which must be replaced)
- Faster response than a fuse

### Earth Wire
- Connects metal casing of appliances to earth
- If live wire touches casing → large current flows to earth → fuse blows → circuit breaks

### Double Insulation
- Appliances with **no earth wire**
- Two layers of electrical insulation on all live parts
- Marked with symbol ⬜⬜ (square within a square)
