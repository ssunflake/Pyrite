## What is Sound?

- Sound is a **mechanical, longitudinal wave**
- It requires a **medium** (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel — **cannot travel through a vacuum**
- Produced by vibrating objects
- Travels as a series of **compressions** (regions of high pressure) and **rarefactions** (regions of low pressure)

---

## Longitudinal Waves

- Particles **vibrate parallel** to the direction of wave travel
- Energy is transmitted through the medium without the medium itself moving overall
- Contrast with **transverse waves** (e.g. light) where particles vibrate perpendicular to wave direction

### Compressions and Rarefactions
- **Compression:** particles pushed together → high pressure region
- **Rarefaction:** particles spread apart → low pressure region
- Wavelength = distance between successive compressions (or rarefactions)

---

## Speed of Sound

| Medium | Speed of Sound |
|--------|---------------|
| Air (20°C) | ~340 m/s |
| Water | ~1500 m/s |
| Steel | ~5000 m/s |
| Vacuum | 0 (cannot travel) |

> Sound travels **faster** in denser/stiffer media:
> Solids > Liquids > Gases

### Speed, Distance, Time
$$v = \frac{d}{t}$$

---

## Pitch and Frequency

- **Frequency** = number of waves per second (Hz)
- **Higher frequency → higher pitch**
- **Lower frequency → lower pitch**

### Human Hearing Range
- Normal: **20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz)**
- Below 20 Hz: **infrasound** (too low to hear)
- Above 20 kHz: **ultrasound** (too high to hear)

| Sound Type | Frequency Range |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Infrasound | < 20 Hz |
| Audible sound | 20 Hz – 20 kHz |
| Ultrasound | > 20 kHz |

- Human hearing deteriorates with age (upper limit decreases)
- Some animals can hear ultrasound (dogs, bats, dolphins)

---

## Loudness and Amplitude

- **Amplitude** = maximum displacement of particles from rest position
- **Higher amplitude → louder sound**
- Loudness is measured in **decibels (dB)**

| Sound | Approximate dB level |
|-------|---------------------|
| Threshold of hearing | 0 dB |
| Whisper | 30 dB |
| Normal conversation | 60 dB |
| Busy street | 80 dB |
| Concert | 110 dB |
| Jet engine | 140 dB |

> Prolonged exposure to sounds above 85 dB can cause hearing damage.

---

## Reflection of Sound — Echo

$$d = \frac{v \times t}{2}$$

- $d$ = distance to reflecting surface (m)
- $v$ = speed of sound (m/s)
- $t$ = total time for sound to travel there and back (s)
- Divide by 2 because sound travels to object AND back

### SONAR (Sound Navigation and Ranging)
- Sends pulses of sound (usually ultrasound) into water
- Measures time for echo to return
- Calculates depth or distance to objects

$$\text{depth} = \frac{v \times t}{2}$$

**Applications of SONAR:**
- Measuring depth of sea
- Detecting submarines
- Fish-finding equipment
- Mapping ocean floor

---

## Ultrasound

> Sound with frequency **above 20,000 Hz** (above human hearing range)

### Properties
- Short wavelength (high frequency)
- Absorbed and reflected at boundaries between different materials
- Non-ionising (safe for medical use)

### Applications of Ultrasound

| Application | How it works |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Medical scanning** | Ultrasound reflects off organs/tissue boundaries → image formed |
| **Prenatal scanning** | Images of unborn baby — safer than X-rays |
| **SONAR** | Depth measurement, submarine detection |
| **Industrial flaw detection** | Finds cracks/defects inside solid materials |
| **Cleaning** | High-frequency vibrations clean delicate items (e.g. jewellery, surgical instruments) |
| **Breaking kidney stones** | Focused ultrasound breaks up stones without surgery |

### Ultrasound vs X-rays for Medical Imaging

| Feature | Ultrasound | X-rays |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Ionising? | No | Yes |
| Safe for unborn baby? | Yes | No |
| Shows soft tissue? | Yes | Poor |
| Shows bones? | Poor | Yes |

## Oscilloscope Traces

An oscilloscope displays sound waves as transverse waves on screen:

- **Amplitude** of trace → loudness (bigger = louder)
- **Frequency** of trace → pitch (more waves per unit time = higher pitch)
- **Wavelength** of trace → visual representation of pitch

| Trace Change | Sound Change |
|-------------|-------------|
| Taller waves | Louder |
| Shorter waves | Quieter |
| More waves in same space | Higher pitch |
| Fewer waves in same space | Lower pitch |
