## Electric Charge

- There are two types of charge: **positive (+)** and **negative (−)**
- **Like charges repel; unlike charges attract**
- Charge is measured in **coulombs (C)**
- Protons carry positive charge; electrons carry negative charge
- Atoms are normally neutral (equal protons and electrons)

---

## Charging by Friction

- When two insulators are rubbed together, **electrons are transferred** from one to the other
- The material that **gains electrons** becomes **negatively charged**
- The material that **loses electrons** becomes **positively charged**
- Total charge is always **conserved** (what one gains, the other loses)

### Examples

| Materials rubbed | Result |
|----------------|--------|
| Polythene rod + cloth | Polythene: negative; cloth: positive |
| Perspex/acetate rod + cloth | Perspex: positive; cloth: negative |
| Balloons rubbed on hair | Balloon: negative; hair: positive |

> **Only electrons move** during charging — protons are fixed in the nucleus.

---

## Conductors and Insulators

| Conductors | Insulators |
|-----------|-----------|
| Allow charge to flow through easily | Do not allow charge to flow |
| Have **free electrons** | No free electrons |
| Examples: metals, graphite | Examples: rubber, plastic, glass, wood |

---

## Electric Fields

> An **electric field** is a region where a charged object experiences a force.

- Represented by **electric field lines**
- Direction: the direction a **positive** test charge would move
- Lines go from **positive** to **negative**
- Closer lines = stronger field

### Electric Field Patterns

| Setup | Field shape |
|-------|------------|
| Point positive charge | Radial, outward |
| Point negative charge | Radial, inward |
| Two opposite charges | Curves from + to − |
| Two parallel plates (+ and −) | Uniform, straight lines from + to − |

### Uniform Electric Field (Parallel Plates)
- Between two oppositely charged parallel plates
- Field is uniform (same everywhere between plates)
- Used in cathode ray tubes, inkjet printers

---

## Induced Charge

- A charged object can attract a neutral object by **electrostatic induction**
- The charged object repels same-sign charges in the neutral object away, and attracts opposite-sign charges to the near surface
- Results in a net attractive force even though the neutral object has no overall charge

**Example:** A charged comb picking up small pieces of paper.

---

## Dangers of Static Electricity

| Danger | Explanation |
|--------|-------------|
| **Fuel filling (aircraft/trucks)** | Friction builds up charge → spark → fire/explosion |
| **Flour mills / grain silos** | Dust particles charged by movement → spark → explosion |
| **Lightning** | Build-up of charge in clouds → massive discharge to ground |
| **Electrostatic discharge (ESD)** | Damages sensitive electronics (computer chips) |

---

## Uses of Static Electricity

### Electrostatic Precipitators (Smoke Filters)
- Used in chimneys of power stations and factories
- Particles inside chimney given a charge
- Oppositely charged plates attract and collect the particles
- Reduces air pollution

### Inkjet Printers
- Tiny ink droplets given a charge
- Deflected by electric fields between charged plates
- Steered to correct position on paper to form letters/images

### Photocopiers / Laser Printers
- Drum given a positive charge
- Light reflects off document → discharges lit areas → leaves pattern on drum
- Toner (negatively charged powder) sticks to positively charged areas
- Transferred to paper and fixed with heat

### Spray Painting
- Paint droplets given a charge (e.g. positive)
- Object to be painted given opposite charge (e.g. negative)
- Droplets attracted to object → even coating
- Reduces waste; even coverage including edges (wraps around)

### Defibrillators
- Capacitors charged up to high voltage
- Discharged through patient's chest → electric shock restarts heart

---

## Lightning Conductors

- Tall buildings fitted with a thick copper strip connected from a metal spike at the top down to the ground
- If lightning strikes → charge safely conducted to Earth through the copper
- Prevents charge building up in building → prevents dangerous sparks

---

## Earthing

- Connecting a charged object to the **Earth** (ground) via a conducting wire
- The Earth acts as an infinite reservoir of charge
- Excess charge flows to or from Earth → object becomes neutral
- Used to safely discharge objects and in electrical safety (earth wire in plugs)
