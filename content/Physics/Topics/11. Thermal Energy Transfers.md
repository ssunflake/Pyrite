## Three Methods of Thermal Energy Transfer

| Method | Medium needed? | Particles move? | Best in |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Conduction** | Yes (solid) | No | Solids |
| **Convection** | Yes (fluid) | Yes | Fluids (liquids/gases) |
| **Radiation** | No | No | Vacuum / any medium |

---

## Conduction

> Transfer of thermal energy through a material **without the material itself moving**, by particle collisions and (in metals) free electron movement.

### Mechanism
- Particles near heat source vibrate more vigorously
- They collide with neighbouring particles → pass on energy
- Energy transferred through material without particles moving from place to place

### In Metals (Extra Mechanism)
- **Free electrons** absorb energy and move quickly through the metal
- Much faster than particle collisions alone
- This is why **metals are much better conductors** than non-metals

### Conductors vs Insulators

| Good Conductors | Good Insulators (Poor Conductors) |
|----------------|----------------------------------|
| Copper, aluminium, iron, steel | Wood, plastic, glass, rubber |
| All metals | Air, wool, fibreglass, foam |
| Diamond (non-metal exception) | Cork, ceramic |

### Applications
| Application | Principle |
|-------------|-----------|
| Metal cooking pans | Good conductor — transfers heat to food |
| Wooden/plastic handles | Poor conductor — stays cool to touch |
| Double-glazed windows | Air gap between panes — poor conductor |
| Loft insulation (fibreglass) | Traps air — reduces heat loss by conduction |
| Wearing layers of clothing | Traps air between layers |

---

## Convection

> Transfer of thermal energy by the **bulk movement of a fluid** (liquid or gas) carrying energy with it.

### Mechanism
1. Fluid near heat source is heated
2. Heated fluid **expands** → becomes **less dense**
3. Less dense fluid **rises**
4. Cooler, denser fluid **sinks** to replace it
5. A **convection current** is established

> Convection does NOT occur in solids (particles cannot move)

### Convection Currents

**Natural (Free) Convection:**
- No fan or pump
- Driven purely by density differences

**Forced Convection:**
- Uses a pump or fan to circulate fluid
- e.g. fan-assisted oven, car radiator fan

### Applications

| Application | Explanation |
|-------------|-------------|
| Heating a room with a radiator | Hot air rises from radiator → circulates room |
| Sea breeze (daytime) | Land heats faster → hot air rises → cool air from sea flows in |
| Land breeze (night) | Sea stays warm → hot air rises → cool air from land flows out |
| Hot water systems in homes | Hot water rises from boiler → distributed to radiators |
| Thunderclouds | Strong upward convection currents in atmosphere |
| Lava lamps | Wax rises when hot, sinks when cool |

## Radiation (Infrared Radiation)

> Transfer of energy as **electromagnetic waves** (infrared radiation), which does not require a medium.

- All objects **emit** and **absorb** infrared radiation
- Does not require particles → works in a **vacuum**
- Travels at the **speed of light** (3 × 10⁸ m/s)

### Emission and Absorption

| Surface Type | Emission | Absorption |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| **Dull, black, dark, rough** | Best emitter | Best absorber |
| **Shiny, white, silver, smooth** | Worst emitter | Worst absorber (best reflector) |

> A good absorber is also a good emitter (at the same temperature).

### Factors Affecting Rate of Radiation
- **Temperature** — higher temperature → much more radiation emitted (Stefan-Boltzmann: ∝ T⁴)
- **Surface colour/texture** — dark/matt surfaces emit and absorb more
- **Surface area** — larger area → more radiation

### Applications

| Application | Principle |
|-------------|-----------|
| Solar panels (black surface) | Absorbs maximum radiation |
| Survival (emergency) blankets | Silver/shiny surface reflects radiation back to body |
| White buildings in hot countries | Reflects solar radiation, stays cooler |
| Infrared thermometers | Detect emitted infrared from objects |
| Greenhouses | Glass allows visible light in but traps infrared |
| Vacuum flask (thermos) | Silvered walls reflect radiation; vacuum prevents conduction/convection |

## The Vacuum Flask (Thermos)

Designed to minimise ALL three types of heat transfer:

| Feature | Reduces |
|---------|---------|
| Double-walled glass with vacuum between | Prevents conduction and convection |
| Silvered inner walls | Reduces radiation |
| Plastic stopper/cap | Poor conductor — reduces conduction through top |
| Outer casing | Shock protection |

---

## Greenhouse Effect

- **Natural greenhouse effect:** CO₂, H₂O vapour and CH₄ in atmosphere absorb outgoing infrared radiation → keeps Earth warm (~15°C average instead of −18°C)
- **Enhanced greenhouse effect:** increased CO₂ from fossil fuels → more heat trapped → global warming

| Method | Key Mechanism | Medium? |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| Conduction | Particle vibrations + free electrons | Yes |
| Convection | Density-driven fluid movement | Yes (fluid) |
| Radiation | Infrared electromagnetic waves | No |
