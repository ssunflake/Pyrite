## What is a Force?

- A **force** is a push or pull on an object
- Forces are **vectors** — they have magnitude and direction
- Unit: **newton (N)**
- Forces can: change an object's speed, direction, or shape

### Types of Forces
| Force | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Gravitational** | Attraction between any two masses |
| **Normal (contact) force** | Perpendicular to a surface, prevents objects passing through |
| **Friction** | Opposes relative motion between surfaces |
| **Tension** | Force through stretched strings/ropes/springs |
| **Air resistance / drag** | Fluid force opposing motion |
| **Upthrust / buoyancy** | Upward force from fluids on submerged objects |
| **Magnetic force** | Force between magnets or on current-carrying conductors |
| **Electrostatic force** | Force between charged objects |

## Resultant Force

- The **resultant force** is the single force that has the same effect as all forces combined
- If forces are in the same line: add or subtract
- For perpendicular forces: use Pythagoras

$$F_{resultant} = \sqrt{F_1^2 + F_2^2}$$

### Free Body Diagrams
- Draw the object as a box or dot
- Draw arrows representing all forces
- Arrow length = relative magnitude
- Arrow direction = direction of force
- Label each force

## Newton's Laws of Motion

### Newton's First Law
> An object remains **at rest** or continues in a **straight line at constant velocity** unless acted on by a **resultant force**.

- This is also called **inertia** — the tendency to resist change in motion
- More mass = more inertia

### Newton's Second Law
> The resultant force on an object equals its mass multiplied by its acceleration.

$$F = ma$$

- $F$ = resultant force (N)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $a$ = acceleration (m/s²)

> Doubling force → doubles acceleration  
> Doubling mass → halves acceleration

### Newton's Third Law
> For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force.

- Forces act on **different objects**
- Same magnitude, opposite direction, same type of force

**Examples:**
- You push ground down → ground pushes you up (walking)
- Earth pulls moon → moon pulls Earth
- Rocket expels gas backwards → gas pushes rocket forwards

## Mass and Weight

| Property | Mass | Weight |
|----------|------|--------|
| Definition | Amount of matter | Gravitational force on object |
| Type | Scalar | Vector |
| Unit | kg | N |
| Changes with location? | No | Yes |
| Measured with | Balance | Newton meter / spring balance |

$$W = mg$$

- $W$ = weight (N)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $g$ = gravitational field strength (N/kg) = 9.8 ≈ 10 N/kg on Earth

| Location | g (N/kg) |
|----------|---------|
| Earth surface | 9.8 |

## Friction

- **Static friction:** keeps stationary object from sliding; up to a maximum value
- **Kinetic (dynamic) friction:** opposes motion of a sliding object; roughly constant
- Friction always acts **opposite to motion** (or tendency of motion)

### Factors affecting friction:
- Nature of surfaces (rougher = more friction)
- Normal force (heavier = more friction)
- Does NOT depend on speed or contact area (for simple cases)

### Useful and Harmful Friction
| Useful | Harmful |
|--------|---------|
| Brakes, tyres gripping road | Engine parts wearing out |
| Walking (feet grip floor) | Heat generated in machinery |
| Pencil writing on paper | Slowing down moving vehicles |

### Reducing Friction
- Lubrication (oil, grease)
- Smooth, polished surfaces
- Ball bearings
- Streamlining (reduce air resistance)

## Drag and Terminal Velocity

- **Drag** is friction from a fluid (air or liquid)
- Increases with speed
- Depends on: shape, size, speed, fluid density

### Falling Object — Terminal Velocity
1. Object released → weight > drag → accelerates
2. Speed increases → drag increases
3. Weight = drag → **terminal velocity** (constant speed, zero acceleration)
4. (If parachute opens) → drag suddenly >> weight → decelerates → new, lower terminal velocity

## Momentum

$$p = mv$$

- $p$ = momentum (kg m/s)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $v$ = velocity (m/s)
- Momentum is a **vector**

### Conservation of Momentum
> In a closed system (no external forces), total momentum **before** = total momentum **after**.

$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$$

**Applies to:**
- Collisions (objects bounce off or stick together)
- Explosions (e.g. gun firing, rocket launching)

### Impulse
$$\text{Impulse} = F \times t = \Delta p = mv - mu$$

- Unit: Ns (same as kg m/s)
- Larger contact time → smaller force for same change in momentum

**Safety applications of impulse:**
- Car airbags (increase time → reduce force on body)
- Crumple zones
- Helmets
- Catching a cricket ball by pulling hands back
