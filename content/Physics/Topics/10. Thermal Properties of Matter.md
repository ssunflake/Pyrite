## Temperature

- Temperature measures how **hot or cold** something is
- It is a measure of the **average kinetic energy** of particles
- Unit: **degrees Celsius (°C)** or **Kelvin (K)**

$$T(K) = T(°C) + 273$$

### Fixed Points (Celsius Scale)
- **Lower fixed point:** 0°C — melting point of pure ice
- **Upper fixed point:** 100°C — boiling point of pure water at standard pressure

## Thermometers

| Type | Thermometric Property | Range | Notes |
|------|----------------------|-------|-------|
| Liquid-in-glass (mercury) | Expansion of mercury | −38°C to 356°C | Accurate, fragile |
| Liquid-in-glass (alcohol) | Expansion of alcohol | −115°C to 78°C | Used for low temps |
| Thermocouple | Voltage between junctions | Very wide range | Fast response, for extremes |
| Resistance thermometer (RTD) | Electrical resistance | Wide range | Very accurate |
| Clinical thermometer | Expansion of mercury | 35–43°C | Has a constriction to retain reading |

### Properties of a Good Thermometer
- Sensitive (responds to small changes)
- Accurate (gives true temperature)
- Wide range
- Quick to respond
- Easy to read
- Safe (non-toxic)

## Specific Heat Capacity (SHC)

> The **specific heat capacity** of a substance is the heat energy needed to raise the temperature of **1 kg** of that substance by **1°C (or 1 K)**.

$$Q = mc\Delta T$$

- $Q$ = heat energy added or removed (J)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $c$ = specific heat capacity (J/kg°C or J/kg K)
- $\Delta T$ = change in temperature (°C or K)

### Specific Heat Capacities of Common Substances

| Substance | SHC (J/kg°C) |
|-----------|-------------|
| Water | 4200 |
| Ice | 2100 |
| Aluminium | 900 |
| Iron | 500 |
| Copper | 390 |
| Lead | 130 |
| Air | 1000 |

> Water has a **very high SHC** — it heats and cools slowly. This makes it useful in:
> - Cooling systems (car radiators)
> - Hot water bottles
> - Climate regulation near seas/oceans

### Practical: Measuring SHC
1. Heat known mass of substance with immersion heater
2. Record temperature change and energy input ($Q = Pt$)
3. Calculate: $c = Q / (m \Delta T)$

## Specific Latent Heat (SLH)

> The **specific latent heat** is the heat energy needed to change the state of **1 kg** of a substance at **constant temperature**.

$$Q = mL$$

- $Q$ = heat energy (J)
- $m$ = mass (kg)
- $L$ = specific latent heat (J/kg)

### Two Types of Latent Heat

| Type | Change of State | Typical values |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| **Specific latent heat of fusion ($L_f$)** | Solid ↔ Liquid | Water: 340,000 J/kg |
| **Specific latent heat of vaporisation ($L_v$)** | Liquid ↔ Gas | Water: 2,260,000 J/kg |

> Latent heat of vaporisation is always **much larger** than latent heat of fusion (more bonds broken).

### Why Temperature Stays Constant During Change of State
- Energy being supplied is used to **break intermolecular bonds**
- Not used to increase kinetic energy of particles
- Therefore temperature does not change

## Thermal Expansion

Most materials **expand** when heated and **contract** when cooled, because particles vibrate more and take up more space.

### Applications and Problems

| Application | Explanation |
|-------------|-------------|
| Gaps in railway lines | Allow expansion on hot days without buckling |
| Expansion joints in bridges | Allow safe expansion |
| Bimetallic strip thermostats | Two metals expand at different rates → strip bends |
| Liquid-in-glass thermometer | Liquid expands measurably |

### Water — Anomalous Expansion
- Water is unusual — it **expands** as it cools from 4°C to 0°C
- **Maximum density at 4°C**
- Ice is less dense than liquid water → ice floats
- This protects aquatic life (ice layer insulates water below)
