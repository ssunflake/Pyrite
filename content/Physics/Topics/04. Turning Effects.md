## The Moment of a Force

A **moment** (or torque) is the turning effect of a force about a pivot.

$$M = F \times d$$

- $M$ = moment (Nm)
- $F$ = force (N)
- $d$ = **perpendicular** distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force (m)

> ⚠️ The distance must be **perpendicular** to the force — not just any distance to the pivot.

### Direction of Moments
- **Clockwise** moments
- **Anticlockwise** moments

---

## The Principle of Moments

> For an object in **equilibrium**, the sum of **clockwise moments** about any pivot equals the sum of **anticlockwise moments** about the same pivot.

$$\sum \text{clockwise moments} = \sum \text{anticlockwise moments}$$

$$F_1 \times d_1 = F_2 \times d_2$$

### Example
A see-saw: person A (600 N) sits 2 m from pivot.
Where must person B (400 N) sit to balance?

$$600 \times 2 = 400 \times d$$
$$d = \frac{1200}{400} = 3 \text{ m}$$

---

## Equilibrium

An object is in **equilibrium** when:
1. The **resultant force** is zero (no linear acceleration)
2. The **resultant moment** is zero (no rotational acceleration)

### Three Forces in Equilibrium
- If three forces act on an object in equilibrium, their lines of action pass through a **single point** (they are concurrent).

---

## Centre of Gravity (Centre of Mass)

- The **centre of gravity** is the point at which the entire weight of an object appears to act.
- For a **uniform** object (regular shape, evenly distributed mass) → CoG is at the geometric centre.
- For an **irregular** object → found by suspending it from different points; CoG is where vertical lines cross.

### Finding CoG of an Irregular Lamina (flat plate)
1. Suspend the lamina from a pin at point A
2. Hang a plumb line from the same pin
3. Draw a vertical line along the plumb line
4. Repeat from point B
5. CoG = intersection of the two lines

---

## Stability

The stability of an object depends on:
1. **Height of centre of gravity** — lower CoG = more stable
2. **Width of base** — wider base = more stable

### Types of Equilibrium

| Type | What happens when tilted slightly | Example |
|------|----------------------------------|---------|
| **Stable** | Returns to original position | A cone resting on its base |
| **Unstable** | Falls further away from original | A cone balanced on its tip |
| **Neutral** | Stays in new position | A ball on a flat surface |

### The Tipping Condition
- An object **topples** when the vertical line through its CoG falls **outside its base**.
- Double-decker buses, racing cars, and cranes are designed with a low CoG and wide base for stability.

### Practical Examples
| Design feature | Purpose |
|----------------|---------|
| Racing car — wide wheelbase, low body | Lower CoG for cornering stability |
| Lorry loaded at the bottom | Keeps CoG low |
| Ballast in ship's hull | Lowers CoG in water |
| Wide base of a Bunsen burner | Prevents toppling |

## Couples

A **couple** is a pair of **equal and opposite forces** that act on an object at different points.

- A couple produces **pure rotation** (no resultant linear force)
- **Torque of a couple** = Force × perpendicular distance between forces

$$\tau = F \times d$$

**Examples:** turning a steering wheel, using a screwdriver, opening a bottle cap
