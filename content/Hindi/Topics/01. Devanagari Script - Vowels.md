## 1.1 Introduction to Devanagari (देवनागरी लिपि)

**Devanagari** (देवनागरी) is the script used to write Hindi, Sanskrit, Marathi, Nepali, and several other South Asian languages. It is one of the most systematic scripts in the world.

**Key facts:**

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Direction | Written **left to right** (→) |
| Type | **Abugida** — each character is a consonant with an inherent vowel /a/ |
| Vowels | 13 independent vowel forms |
| Consonants | 33 main consonants |
| Head-line | All letters hang from a horizontal bar called **शिरोरेखा** (śirorekha) |
| Phonetic nature | Nearly perfectly phonetic — you read exactly what is written |

> 💡 **Tip:** Unlike English spelling, Devanagari is almost entirely phonetic. Once you learn the sounds of each character, you can read *any* Hindi word correctly. This makes learning the script an excellent investment of time.

## 1.2 Independent Vowels (स्वर — Svar)

Independent vowels are written as full characters and used:
1. At the **beginning of words**: अब (now), आम (mango)
2. After **another vowel** within a word
3. When a vowel stands **alone** as a syllable

When a vowel follows a **consonant**, it is written as a **mātrā** (vowel diacritic) — covered in [[03. Matras and Writing Rules|Chapter 3]].

### Complete Independent Vowel Chart

| Devanagari | Name | Translit. | Pronunciation Guide | Example Word | Meaning |
|------------|------|-----------|---------------------|--------------|---------|
| अ | a | a | Short, like 'u' in *but* or 'a' in *about* | अब (ab) | now |
| आ | ā | ā | Long, like 'a' in *father* — held twice as long as अ | आम (ām) | mango |
| इ | i | i | Short, like 'i' in *bit* | इधर (idhar) | this way / here |
| ई | ī | ī | Long, like 'ee' in *feet* — held twice as long as इ | ईमान (īmān) | honesty / faith |
| उ | u | u | Short, like 'u' in *put* | उम्र (umr) | age |
| ऊ | ū | ū | Long, like 'oo' in *food* — held twice as long as उ | ऊँट (ūṃṭ) | camel |
| ए | e | e | Like 'e' in *they* — pure, no glide | एक (ek) | one |
| ऐ | ai | ai | Like 'a' in *hat* or 'e' in *bed* | ऐनक (ainak) | spectacles |
| ओ | o | o | Like 'o' in *go* — pure, no glide | ओस (os) | dew |
| औ | au | au | Like 'ou' in *caught* | औरत (aurat) | woman |
| ऋ | ṛi | ṛ | A retroflex 'ri' — tongue curls back slightly | ऋतु (ṛtu) | season |
| अं | aṃ | ṃ | Nasalized vowel — the anusvara (see §1.4) | अंदर (aṃdar) | inside |
| अः | aḥ | ḥ | Vowel followed by soft 'h' breath — the visarga (see §1.5) | अतः (ataḥ) | therefore |

## 1.3 Short vs Long Vowels (ह्रस्व और दीर्घ स्वर)

Hindi has **three short-long pairs** where length is phonemically significant — it **changes the meaning** of a word:

| Short Vowel | Long Vowel | Difference | Short Example | Long Example |
|-------------|------------|------------|---------------|--------------|
| अ (a) | आ (ā) | Duration doubled | कल (kal) = yesterday/tomorrow | काल (kāl) = time / death |
| इ (i) | ई (ī) | Duration doubled | दिन (din) = day | दीन (dīn) = poor / humble |
| उ (u) | ऊ (ū) | Duration doubled | सुना (sunā) = heard | सूना (sūnā) = deserted / empty |

> [!note] In Roman transliteration, a macron (ā, ī, ū) always marks a **long vowel**. Short vowels have no mark (a, i, u). You must never confuse these — they represent genuinely different phonemes.

### Further Minimal Pairs — Vowel Length Changes Meaning

| Word | Transliteration | Meaning | Word | Transliteration | Meaning |
|------|-----------------|---------|------|-----------------|---------|
| पल | pal | moment | पाल | pāl | sail; to rear/raise |
| बिल | bil | burrow / hole | बील | bīl | type of berry |
| पुल | pul | bridge | पूल | pūl | pool (loanword) |
| मिल | mil | mill / to meet | मील | mīl | mile |
| चल | cal | walk! / move! | चाल | cāl | gait / trick |

## 1.4 Anusvara (अनुस्वार — ं)

The **anusvara** is a dot written above a vowel character (◌ं). It represents **nasalisation** — the vowel is pronounced through the nose.

The exact nasal consonant sound the anusvara represents depends on the consonant that **follows** it:

| Following Consonant Group | Nasal Sound | Example | Meaning |
|---------------------------|-------------|---------|---------|
| क, ख, ग, घ (velar) | ṅ (velar nasal, like *sing*) | संकट (saṃkaṭ) | crisis |
| च, छ, ज, झ (palatal) | ñ (palatal nasal) | पंजा (pañjā) | paw / grip |
| ट, ठ, ड, ढ (retroflex) | ṇ (retroflex nasal) | ठंडा (ṭhaṃḍā) | cold |
| त, थ, द, ध (dental) | n (dental nasal) | अंत (aṃt) | end |
| प, फ, ब, भ (labial) | m (labial nasal) | संभव (saṃbhav) | possible |
| Before vowel / at word end | pure nasalisation | यहाँ (yahāṃ) | here |

**Common words with anusvara:**

| Hindi | Transliteration | English |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| अंदर | aṃdar | inside |
| अंगूर | aṃgūr | grapes |
| हिंदी | hiṃdī | Hindi |
| बंद | baṃd | closed |
| संस्कृत | saṃskṛt | Sanskrit |
| रंग | raṃg | colour |
| मंदिर | maṃdir | temple |

## 1.5 Visarga (विसर्ग — ः)

The **visarga** (ः) represents a voiceless glottal fricative — a short, aspirated 'h' sound following a vowel. It occurs mainly in:
- Sanskrit loanwords used in formal Hindi
- Traditional/literary texts

| Hindi | Transliteration | English |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| दुःख | duḥkh | sorrow, pain |
| अतः | ataḥ | therefore (formal) |
| पुनः | punaḥ | again (formal) |
| प्रायः | prāyaḥ | usually, generally |

> [!note] The visarga is rare in everyday spoken Hindi but appears in formal and literary contexts. Recognise it in reading; you will not need it often in writing.

## 1.6 Chandrabindu (चंद्रबिंदु — ँ)

The **chandrabindu** (ँ — literally "moon-dot") indicates **gentle nasalisation of a vowel**, softer than the anusvara. It is used above vowels with ascenders (tall mātrās).

| Hindi | Transliteration | English |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| यहाँ | yahāṃ | here |
| वहाँ | vahāṃ | there |
| कहाँ | kahāṃ | where |
| माँ | māṃ | mother |
| हाँ | hāṃ | yes |
| मुँह | muṃh | mouth / face |

> 💡 **Tip:** In practice, chandrabindu and anusvara are used interchangeably in many common words. You will see both **यहाँ** and **यहां** in printed texts. Do not worry about this variation — both are understood.

## 1.8 Tips for Remembering the Vowel Sequence

The traditional order **अ आ इ ई उ ऊ ए ऐ ओ औ ऋ** follows a phonetic logic:

1. **अ / आ** — open vowels, mouth wide open (like 'aah' at the doctor)
2. **इ / ई** — front high vowels, tongue forward and high (like 'ee')
3. **उ / ऊ** — back rounded vowels, lips rounded (like 'oo')
4. **ए / ऐ** — mid front vowels (like 'e')
5. **ओ / औ** — mid back rounded vowels (like 'o')
6. **ऋ** — special retroflex vowel, Sanskrit origin

> 💡 **Tip:** Write each vowel on a flashcard with its transliteration, pronunciation guide, and one example word. Drill the cards every day in the first two weeks. Once you can read all vowels instantly, you have mastered 30% of the script.
