## 7.1 Physical and Chemical Changes

| Feature | Physical Change | Chemical Change |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| New substances formed? | No | Yes |
| Reversible? | Usually yes | Usually no (or difficult) |
| Energy change | Small | Often large |
| Examples | Melting, dissolving, boiling | Burning, rusting, neutralisation |

### Signs of a Chemical Change
- Temperature change (heat released or absorbed)
- Colour change
- Gas produced (effervescence)
- Precipitate formed
- Smell produced
- Light produced (luminescence)
- **Irreversible** under normal conditions

## 7.2 Exothermic and Endothermic Reactions

> **Exothermic reaction**: releases heat energy to surroundings; temperature of surroundings **rises**; products have less energy than reactants.

> **Endothermic reaction**: absorbs heat energy from surroundings; temperature of surroundings **falls**; products have more energy than reactants.

### Energy Level (Profile) Diagrams

**Exothermic:**
- Reactants at higher energy level than products
- őĒH = negative (energy released)
- Activation energy (E‚āź) = energy needed to start reaction (energy to peak from reactants)

**Endothermic:**
- Products at higher energy level than reactants
- őĒH = positive (energy absorbed)
- Activation energy = energy to peak from reactants

### Activation Energy
> The **activation energy** (E‚āź) is the minimum energy that reactant particles must have in order to react when they collide.

A **catalyst** provides an **alternative pathway** with **lower activation energy** ‚ÄĒ same reactants and products, same őĒH, but faster rate.

- Combustion: CH‚āĄ + 2O‚āā ‚Üí CO‚āā + 2H‚āāO; őĒH = ‚ąí890 kJ/mol
- Neutralisation: HCl + NaOH ‚Üí NaCl + H‚āāO; őĒH ‚Čą ‚ąí57 kJ/mol
- Respiration, oxidation of metals, displacement reactions
