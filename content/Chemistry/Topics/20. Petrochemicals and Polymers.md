## 20.1 Petroleum and its Products

> **Petroleum (crude oil)** is a complex mixture of hydrocarbons (mainly alkanes) formed from ancient marine organisms over millions of years.

### Fractional Distillation of Petroleum

**Principle:** Different hydrocarbons have different boiling points depending on chain length. The mixture is separated by heating and collecting fractions at different temperatures.

**In the fractionating column:**
- Hot crude oil vapour enters at the bottom
- Temperature decreases from bottom (high) to top (low)
- Fractions condense at different levels and are collected

### Petroleum Fractions

| Fraction | Carbon chain length | Approx. boiling point | State at r.t.p. | Uses |
|----------|--------------------|-----------------------|-----------------|------|
| **Refinery gas** | C₁–C₄ | < 25°C | Gas | Camping gas (LPG), fuel |
| **Gasoline (petrol)** | C₅–C₁₀ | 25–75°C | Liquid | Car fuel |
| **Naphtha** | C₅–C₁₀ | 75–120°C | Liquid | Feedstock for chemicals |
| **Kerosene (paraffin)** | C₁₀–C₁₆ | 150–250°C | Liquid | Jet fuel, heating |
| **Diesel (gas oil)** | C₁₅–C₂₅ | 220–350°C | Liquid | Diesel engines, heating |
| **Fuel oil (heavy)** | C₂₀–C₅₀ | 300–400°C | Thick liquid | Ships' fuel, power stations |
| **Bitumen/tar** | > C₅₀ | > 400°C | Solid | Road surfacing, roofing |

**Trends from bottom to top of column:** shorter chain length → lower boiling point → more volatile → more flammable → less viscous → lighter colour

### Cracking

> **Cracking** is the thermal decomposition of long-chain alkane molecules into shorter, more useful alkanes and alkenes.

**Why is cracking necessary?**
- Demand for short-chain hydrocarbons (petrol, jet fuel) is greater than supply from fractional distillation
- Long-chain fractions (heavy oils) are in excess
- Cracking converts excess long-chain to useful short-chain + alkenes (for plastics)

**Two main types:**

| Type | Conditions | Products |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Thermal cracking** | Very high T (700–1200°C), high P | Wide range of alkenes and alkanes |
| **Catalytic cracking** | High T (~500°C), Al₂O₃/SiO₂ catalyst | Branched alkanes (better petrol) + aromatic compounds |

**General equation:**
$$\underbrace{\text{C}_{10}\text{H}_{22}}_{\text{long chain alkane}} \xrightarrow{\Delta, \text{catalyst}} \underbrace{\text{C}_8\text{H}_{18}}_{\text{octane}} + \underbrace{\text{C}_2\text{H}_4}_{\text{ethene}}$$

Always: **long chain alkane → shorter alkane + alkene(s)**

## 20.2 Polymers

> A **polymer** is a very large molecule (macromolecule) made by joining together many small repeating units called **monomers**.

### Addition Polymerisation

- Monomers are **alkenes** (contain C=C double bond)
- The C=C breaks open and molecules join together
- No other product formed (no atoms lost)

**General representation:**
$$n \text{ CH}_2=\text{CH}_2 \rightarrow -(\text{CH}_2-\text{CH}_2)_n-$$

**Common addition polymers:**

| Monomer | Polymer | Uses |
|---------|---------|------|
| Ethene (CH₂=CH₂) | Poly(ethene) / polyethylene (LDPE/HDPE) | Plastic bags, bottles, pipes |
| Propene (CH₃CH=CH₂) | Poly(propene) / polypropylene | Rope, food containers, carpet |
| Chloroethene (CH₂=CHCl) | Poly(chloroethene) / PVC | Pipes, electrical insulation, flooring |
| Phenylethene (CH₂=CHC₆H₅) | Poly(phenylethene) / polystyrene | Packaging foam, cups |
| Tetrafluoroethene (CF₂=CF₂) | PTFE (Teflon) | Non-stick pans, electrical insulation |

**Identifying the monomer from polymer structure:**
- Look at the repeating unit: each –CH₂–CH(X)– unit means monomer was CH₂=CH(X)

### Condensation Polymerisation

- Monomers have **two functional groups** each (difunctional)
- A **small molecule** (H₂O or HCl) is eliminated with each bond formed
- Produces polyesters, polyamides

**Polyesters (e.g. Terylene / PET):**
- Formed from: **diol** (two –OH groups) + **dicarboxylic acid** (two –COOH groups)
- Linkage: **ester bonds** (–COO–)
- e.g. ethane-1,2-diol + benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid → PET + H₂O

**Polyamides (e.g. Nylon-6,6):**
- Formed from: **diamine** (two –NH₂ groups) + **dicarboxylic acid** (two –COOH groups)
- Linkage: **amide bonds** (–CONH–)
- e.g. hexanedioic acid + 1,6-diaminohexane → Nylon-6,6 + H₂O

## 20.3 Plastics

### Thermosoftening vs Thermosetting Plastics

| Feature | Thermosoftening (thermoplastic) | Thermosetting |
|---------|--------------------------------|--------------|
| Structure | Long polymer chains with **weak** intermolecular forces | **Cross-linked** chains (strong covalent bonds between chains) |
| On heating | Softens and can be remoulded | Does not soften; chars/burns |
| Recyclable | **Yes** | No |
| Examples | Poly(ethene), PVC, polystyrene | Bakelite, melamine, epoxy resin |

### Environmental Issues with Plastics

| Issue | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Non-biodegradable** | Most plastics resist decomposition — persist for centuries in landfill and oceans |
| **Marine pollution** | Microplastics ingested by wildlife; photodegradation breaks plastics into microplastics but doesn't destroy them |
| **Combustion** | Burning can release toxic gases (HCl from PVC, dioxins from chlorinated plastics) |
| **Landfill** | Occupies space; some leach chemicals |
| **Recycling challenges** | Different polymers must be separated; not all are economically viable to recycle |

### Solutions

| Solution | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Mechanical recycling** | Melt and re-form thermoplastics |
| **Chemical recycling** | Depolymerisation — break polymer back to monomer |
| **Biodegradable plastics** | Made from starch/PLA; break down naturally |
| **Incineration with energy recovery** | Burns plastic as fuel; energy captured |
| **Reduce and reuse** | Minimise plastic use |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Petroleum | Complex mixture of hydrocarbons formed from ancient marine organisms |
| Fractional distillation | Separation of crude oil into fractions based on boiling point differences |
| Cracking | Thermal decomposition of long-chain alkanes into shorter alkanes and alkenes |
| Polymer | Large macromolecule made of many repeating monomer units |
| Addition polymerisation | Alkene monomers join together using the C=C double bond; no other product |
| Condensation polymerisation | Monomers with two functional groups join; small molecules (H₂O) eliminated |
| Thermosoftening plastic | Polymer that softens on heating; recyclable |
| Thermosetting plastic | Cross-linked polymer; does not soften on heating |
