## 14.1 The Properties of Metals

Most elements in the Periodic Table are metals. Their properties arise from **metallic bonding** — a lattice of positive ions surrounded by a sea of delocalised electrons.

### Physical Properties of Metals

| Property | Typical value | Reason (metallic bonding) |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| **Electrical conductivity** | Good | Delocalised electrons carry charge |
| **Thermal conductivity** | Good | Electrons and lattice vibrations transfer energy |
| **Malleability** | Can be hammered into sheets | Ion layers can slide without breaking the bond |
| **Ductility** | Can be drawn into wire | Same — layers slide |
| **Hardness** | Generally hard | Strong metal-electron attraction |
| **Melting/boiling point** | Generally high | Strong metallic bonds require lots of energy |
| **Density** | Generally high | Closely packed ions |
| **Lustre** | Shiny | Delocalised electrons reflect light |
| **Sonorous** | Ring when struck | — |

### Exceptions and Anomalies
- Mercury (Hg): **liquid** metal at room temperature (very low melting point: −39°C)
- Gallium (Ga): melts just above room temperature (~30°C)
- Tungsten (W): highest melting point of all metals (~3422°C)
- Sodium and potassium: much **softer and less dense** than typical metals

## 14.2 Uses of Metals

| Metal | Key properties exploited | Uses |
|-------|------------------------|------|
| **Copper (Cu)** | Excellent conductor; ductile; doesn't corrode | Electrical wiring, water pipes, cookware |
| **Iron/Steel** | Strong, magnetic, cheap | Construction, tools, vehicles, machinery |
| **Aluminium (Al)** | Low density, oxide layer resists corrosion, good conductor | Aircraft, food packaging, overhead cables, window frames |
| **Gold (Au)** | Very unreactive, ductile, good conductor | Jewellery, electrical contacts, dentistry |
| **Silver (Ag)** | Best conductor, reflective | Photography, mirrors, jewellery, cutlery |
| **Titanium (Ti)** | Strong, low density, resistant to corrosion | Aircraft, medical implants (hip joints) |
| **Zinc (Zn)** | Galvanising (protective coating on steel), alloys | Galvanising, batteries, brass |
| **Lead (Pb)** | Dense, soft, resistant to corrosion | Car batteries, radiation shielding (now less used — toxic) |
| **Tungsten (W)** | Very high melting point | Filaments in light bulbs, cutting tools |
| **Platinum (Pt)** | Unreactive, excellent catalyst | Catalytic converters, laboratory equipment |
| **Mercury (Hg)** | Liquid at room temperature, good conductor | Thermometers (being phased out — toxic), switches |

## 14.3 Alloys

> An **alloy** is a **mixture** of a metal with one or more other elements (usually metals or carbon), designed to give improved properties compared to the pure metal.

### Why Alloys Have Different Properties from Pure Metals

In a pure metal, layers of identical-sized atoms can slide over each other easily (making it soft and malleable). In an alloy, **different-sized atoms disrupt the regular lattice** → layers cannot slide as easily → the alloy is **harder and stronger**.

### Common Alloys

| Alloy | Composition | Properties | Uses |
|-------|------------|-----------|------|
| **Steel (mild)** | Fe + 0.1–0.3% C | Stronger than pure Fe, malleable | Beams, car bodies |
| **Steel (stainless)** | Fe + ~18% Cr + ~8% Ni | Corrosion-resistant, hard | Cutlery, surgical instruments, sinks |
| **Steel (high-carbon)** | Fe + 0.6–1.5% C | Very hard, brittle | Cutting tools, springs |
| **Brass** | Cu + Zn (~30%) | Harder than Cu, attractive colour | Musical instruments, taps, decorative fittings |
| **Bronze** | Cu + Sn (~12%) | Harder than Cu, resistant to corrosion | Medals, statues, bearings, coins |
| **Solder** | Pb + Sn (50:50) | Low melting point | Joining electronic components |
| **Duralumin** | Al + Cu + Mg + Mn | Strong, low density | Aircraft frames |
| **Amalgam (dental)** | Hg + Ag/Sn/Cu | Hard, easy to shape | Dental fillings (being replaced) |
| **Nichrome** | Ni + Cr | High electrical resistance, heat-resistant | Heating elements in toasters, kettles |

### Steel in Detail

Pure iron is **too soft** for most uses because its regular layers slide easily.

| Type | Carbon content | Properties | Uses |
|------|---------------|-----------|------|
| Wrought iron | < 0.1% C | Very soft, malleable | Decorative ironwork |
| Mild steel | 0.1–0.3% C | Strong and malleable | Car bodies, bridges |
| High-carbon steel | 0.6–1.5% C | Very hard, brittle | Knives, cutting tools |
| Stainless steel | + Cr and Ni | Hard + corrosion resistant | Cutlery, medical equipment |

### Aluminium Alloys
- Pure Al is **soft and weak** but has excellent corrosion resistance
- Adding Cu, Mg, Mn → duralumin: **strong but still light**
- Crucial for aerospace industry

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Metallic bonding | Attraction between lattice of positive metal ions and sea of delocalised electrons |
| Alloy | Mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (to improve properties) |
| Malleability | Ability to be hammered into different shapes without breaking |
| Ductility | Ability to be drawn into a wire without breaking |
| Corrosion | Degradation of a metal by reaction with substances in the environment |
