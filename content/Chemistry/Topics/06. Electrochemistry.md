## 6.1 Types of Electrical Conductivity

### Conductors and Non-conductors

| Type | Conducts electricity? | How | Examples |
|------|----------------------|-----|---------|
| **Metallic conductor** | Yes | Delocalised electrons | Cu, Fe, graphite |
| **Ionic compound (solid)** | No | Ions fixed in lattice | NaCl(s) |
| **Ionic compound (molten)** | Yes | Ions free to move | NaCl(l) |
| **Ionic compound (aqueous)** | Yes | Ions free to move | NaCl(aq) |
| **Covalent compound** | No | No ions or free electrons | Sugar solution |
| **Exception: acids in water** | Yes | Ionise to form ions | HCl(aq) → H⁺ + Cl⁻ |

> **Electrolyte** = a substance that conducts electricity when molten or dissolved in water (by ionic movement)

### Electrolysis

> **Electrolysis** is the decomposition of an ionic compound (electrolyte) by passing an electric current through it in the molten or dissolved state.

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Anode** | Positive electrode; **oxidation** occurs (anions lose electrons) |
| **Cathode** | Negative electrode; **reduction** occurs (cations gain electrons) |
| **Electrolyte** | Ionic compound in liquid/aqueous form |
| **Anion** | Negative ion; moves toward anode |
| **Cation** | Positive ion; moves toward cathode |

> Memory aid: **OIL RIG** — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)  
> **AN OX, RED CAT** — Anode = OXidation; Cathode = REDuction

## 6.2 Products of Electrolysis

### Molten Ionic Compounds

Simple rule: only the two ions present.

| Electrolyte | Cathode product | Anode product |
|-------------|----------------|--------------|
| Molten NaCl | Na (sodium) | Cl₂ (chlorine) |
| Molten Al₂O₃ | Al (aluminium) | O₂ (oxygen) |
| Molten PbBr₂ | Pb (lead) | Br₂ (bromine) |

### Aqueous Solutions — Discharge Rules

Water contributes H⁺ and OH⁻ ions alongside the electrolyte ions.

**At the cathode (reduction — cation discharged):**
- If metal is **below hydrogen** in reactivity (e.g. Cu, Ag): **metal deposited**
- If metal is **above hydrogen** (e.g. Na, Mg, Zn) or solution is dilute: **hydrogen gas** produced
- Rule: H₂ discharged preferentially from dilute solutions of active metals

**At the anode (oxidation — anion discharged):**
- If **concentrated** halide (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) present: **halogen** produced
- Otherwise: **oxygen** produced (from OH⁻ or H₂O)

### Common Aqueous Electrolysis Outcomes

| Electrolyte | Cathode product | Anode product |
|-------------|----------------|--------------|
| Dilute H₂SO₄ | H₂ | O₂ |
| CuSO₄ (aq) with Cu electrodes | Cu deposited | Cu dissolves (from anode) |
| CuSO₄ (aq) with inert (C/Pt) electrodes | Cu deposited | O₂ |
| Dilute NaCl | H₂ | O₂ |
| Concentrated NaCl (brine) | H₂ | Cl₂ |
| NaCl (molten) | Na | Cl₂ |

### Half-equations

**At cathode (reduction):**
$$\text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu}$$
$$2\text{H}^+ + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{H}_2$$

**At anode (oxidation):**
$$2\text{Cl}^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}_2 + 2e^-$$
$$4\text{OH}^- \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{O}_2 + 4e^-$$
$$\text{Cu} \rightarrow \text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^-$$

### Industrial Applications

| Process | Electrolyte | Products | Importance |
|---------|------------|---------|-----------|
| **Chlor-alkali (brine)** | Concentrated NaCl(aq) | Cl₂, H₂, NaOH | PVC, bleach, soap, margarine |
| **Extraction of Al** | Molten Al₂O₃ in cryolite | Al, O₂ | Aluminium metal production |
| **Electroplating** | Solution of plating metal salt | Metal deposited on object | Corrosion protection, decoration |
| **Purification of Cu** | CuSO₄(aq), Cu electrodes | Pure Cu at cathode | Electrical wiring |

### Electroplating
- Object to be plated = **cathode**
- Plating metal = **anode** (dissolves to replenish ions)
- Electrolyte = solution of plating metal salt
- e.g. Silver plating: object = cathode, Ag anode, AgNO₃ solution

## 6.3 Hydrogen as a Fuel

### Hydrogen as an Energy Source

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Combustion reaction | H₂(g) + ½O₂(g) → H₂O(l); ΔH = −286 kJ/mol |
| Products | Water only — **no CO₂ emissions** |
| Energy density | Very high (per gram) |
| Fuel cells | Convert chemical energy directly to electrical energy |

### Hydrogen Fuel Cell
- Hydrogen + oxygen → water + electricity + heat
- At anode: H₂ → 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation)
- At cathode: O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O (reduction)
- More efficient than combustion engine (~60% vs ~25%)

### Advantages and Disadvantages of Hydrogen as Fuel

| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-----------|--------------|
| Only water produced (clean) | Production currently uses fossil fuels (grey H₂) |
| High energy per gram | Difficult to store (very light, flammable) |
| Renewable (if made by electrolysis from solar/wind) | Infrastructure (fuel stations) not widely available |
| Fuel cells more efficient than engines | Explosive — safety concerns |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Electrolysis | Decomposition of electrolyte by passage of electric current |
| Electrolyte | Substance that conducts electricity when molten or dissolved |
| Anode | Positive electrode; oxidation occurs |
| Cathode | Negative electrode; reduction occurs |
| Oxidation | Loss of electrons (at anode) |
| Reduction | Gain of electrons (at cathode) |
