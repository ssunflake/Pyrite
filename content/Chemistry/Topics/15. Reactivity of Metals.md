## 15.1 The Metal Reactivity Series

> The **reactivity series** is a list of metals arranged in order of decreasing reactivity, based on how readily they lose electrons to form positive ions.

### The Reactivity Series (Most → Least Reactive)

```
K   (potassium)   ← Most reactive
Na  (sodium)
Ca  (calcium)
Mg  (magnesium)
Al  (aluminium)
C   (carbon) ← non-metal, included for reference
Zn  (zinc)
Fe  (iron)
Ni  (nickel)
Sn  (tin)
Pb  (lead)
H   (hydrogen) ← reference point
Cu  (copper)
Ag  (silver)
Au  (gold)       ← Least reactive
Pt  (platinum)
```

### Reactions with Water and Acid

| Metal | With cold water | With steam | With dilute acid |
|-------|----------------|-----------|-----------------|
| K, Na, Ca | Vigorous reaction, H₂ produced | — | Too violent/explosive |
| Mg | Very slow with cold water | Burns brightly | Vigorous: H₂ produced |
| Al | Very slow (oxide layer) | Yes | Slow (oxide layer) |
| Zn, Fe | No reaction | Yes with steam | H₂ produced (moderate) |
| Pb, Sn | No reaction | No reaction | Slow reaction |
| Cu, Ag, Au | No reaction | No reaction | No reaction |

**With dilute acid (e.g. H₂SO₄ or HCl):**
$$\text{Zn}(s) + 2\text{HCl}(aq) \rightarrow \text{ZnCl}_2(aq) + \text{H}_2(g)$$
$$\text{Fe}(s) + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4(aq) \rightarrow \text{FeSO}_4(aq) + \text{H}_2(g)$$

**With water (Na):**
$$2\text{Na}(s) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}(aq) + \text{H}_2(g)$$

**With oxygen (reactions):**
$$2\text{Mg}(s) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{MgO}(s) \quad \text{(bright white flame)}$$
$$4\text{Fe}(s) + 3\text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3(s) \quad \text{(rusting, slow)}$$

### Aluminium's Anomaly
- Al is reactive (high in series) but appears unreactive
- A **thin, tough layer of Al₂O₃** forms immediately on the surface
- This **oxide layer** protects the underlying metal from further reaction
- The oxide layer can be removed by: using concentrated acid, amalgamation with Hg

## 15.2 Metal Displacement Reactions

> A **displacement reaction** occurs when a **more reactive metal** displaces (pushes out) a **less reactive metal** from a solution of its salt.

$$\text{More reactive metal} + \text{Salt of less reactive metal} \rightarrow \text{Salt of more reactive metal} + \text{Less reactive metal}$$

### Examples

| Reaction | Observation |
|----------|------------|
| Zn + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu | Blue solution fades; red-brown Cu deposits on Zn |
| Fe + CuSO₄(aq) → FeSO₄(aq) + Cu | Blue solution fades; red-brown Cu deposits on Fe |
| Cu + ZnSO₄(aq) → No reaction | No change (Cu less reactive than Zn) |
| Mg + FeSO₄(aq) → MgSO₄(aq) + Fe | Pale green colour fades; grey Fe deposits |
| Zn + FeSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Fe | Pale green colour fades; grey Fe deposits |
| Fe + CuCl₂(aq) → FeCl₂(aq) + Cu | Blue solution fades |

### Thermite Reaction
$$2\text{Al}(s) + \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3(s) \rightarrow \text{Al}_2\text{O}_3(s) + 2\text{Fe}(l) + \text{heat}$$

- Al displaces Fe from iron(III) oxide
- Extremely exothermic — produces molten iron
- Used to weld railway tracks

### Carbon and Hydrogen as "Displacement Agents"

Carbon (coke) can displace metals below it in the reactivity series from their oxides:
$$\text{ZnO}(s) + \text{C}(s) \rightarrow \text{Zn}(s) + \text{CO}_2(g)$$
$$2\text{CuO}(s) + \text{C}(s) \rightarrow 2\text{Cu}(s) + \text{CO}_2(g)$$

Hydrogen can also reduce metal oxides below hydrogen in the series:
$$\text{CuO}(s) + \text{H}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{Cu}(s) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(g)$$

### Redox Nature of Displacement Reactions

In Zn + CuSO₄:
- Zn: 0 → +2 (oxidised — it is the **reducing agent**)
- Cu: +2 → 0 (reduced — CuSO₄ is the **oxidising agent**)

$$\text{Zn} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e^- \quad \text{(oxidation)}$$
$$\text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu} \quad \text{(reduction)}$$

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Reactivity series | List of metals in order of decreasing reactivity |
| Displacement reaction | More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal from its salt solution |
| Reducing agent | Substance that reduces another (and is itself oxidised); here, the more reactive metal |
| Thermite reaction | Highly exothermic reaction of Al with Fe₂O₃ to produce Fe and Al₂O₃ |
