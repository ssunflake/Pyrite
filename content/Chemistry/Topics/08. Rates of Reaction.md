## 8.1 Factors Affecting the Rate of Reaction

> The **rate of reaction** is the change in amount (or concentration) of reactant or product per unit time.

$$\text{Rate} = \frac{\text{change in amount}}{\text{time}}$$

### Five Key Factors

| Factor | Increase in factor → rate? | Explanation |
|--------|---------------------------|------------|
| **Concentration** (solutions) | Increases | More particles per volume → more frequent collisions |
| **Pressure** (gases) | Increases | More particles per volume → more frequent collisions |
| **Temperature** | Increases | Particles have more kinetic energy → more frequent AND more energetic collisions; more particles exceed Eₐ |
| **Surface area** (solids) | Increases | More reactant surface exposed → more collisions possible |
| **Catalyst** | Increases | Provides alternative pathway with lower Eₐ → more particles exceed Eₐ |

### Measuring Rate of Reaction

**Methods:**
- **Gas collection** — measure volume of gas produced over time (e.g. marble + HCl → CO₂)
- **Mass loss** — measure decrease in mass as gas escapes
- **Colour change / light absorption** — spectrophotometer
- **pH change** — for acid-producing reactions
- **Precipitate** — time until solution turns cloudy (disappearing cross method)

**Rate graph characteristics:**
- Steeper slope = faster rate
- Slope flattens when reaction slows (reactants being used up)
- Levels off completely when reaction is finished

### Effect of Temperature — Detailed
- 10°C rise approximately **doubles** the rate for many reactions
- Not just more collisions — also more particles with energy ≥ Eₐ (from Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution)
- The **proportion** of particles exceeding Eₐ increases significantly

### Effect of Concentration — Detailed
- Doubling concentration: more particles per unit volume
- Frequency of collisions increases proportionally
- More successful collisions per second → faster rate

## 8.2 Collision Theory of Reaction Rate

> **Collision theory** states that for a reaction to occur, particles must **collide** with **sufficient energy** (≥ activation energy) and the **correct orientation**.

### Three Requirements for a Successful Collision
1. **Collision must occur** (particles must meet)
2. **Sufficient energy** — total KE ≥ activation energy Eₐ
3. **Correct orientation** — reactive parts of molecules must face each other

### Why Not All Collisions Lead to Reaction
- Most collisions lack sufficient energy (< Eₐ)
- Some have wrong orientation
- Result: only a small fraction of collisions are "successful"

### Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

- Shows the distribution of particle kinetic energies at a given temperature
- **Area under the curve = total number of particles** (constant)
- The **shaded area to the right of Eₐ** = fraction of particles that can react

**Effect of temperature increase:**
- Curve shifts right and flattens
- More particles to the right of Eₐ (larger shaded area)
- Rate increases dramatically

**Effect of catalyst:**
- Eₐ moves to the left on the graph
- More particles now exceed the (lower) Eₐ
- Rate increases without temperature change

### Catalysts

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Heterogeneous** | Different phase to reactants; provides surface for adsorption | Fe in Haber process, Pt/Rh in catalytic converter, V₂O₅ in Contact process |
| **Homogeneous** | Same phase as reactants | Acid in esterification |
| **Biological (enzyme)** | Protein catalyst in living organisms | Amylase, pepsin |

**Catalyst characteristics:**
- Not consumed in reaction
- Can be reused
- Does not change ΔH (products and reactants unchanged)
- Lowers activation energy (provides alternative pathway)
- Increases both forward and reverse rates equally (in reversible reactions)

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Rate of reaction | Change in amount of reactant or product per unit time |
| Activation energy | Minimum energy required for a reaction to occur |
| Collision theory | Particles must collide with sufficient energy and correct orientation to react |
| Catalyst | Substance that increases reaction rate without being consumed; lowers Eₐ |
| Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution | Graph showing spread of kinetic energies of particles at a given temperature |
