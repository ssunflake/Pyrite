## 12.1 The Importance of Salts — An Introduction

> A **salt** is an ionic compound formed when the hydrogen ion (H⁺) of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or ammonium ion (NH₄⁺).

### Solubility Rules (Must Learn!)

| Salt / compound | Solubility | Exceptions |
|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| **All nitrates** | Soluble | None |
| **All sodium, potassium, ammonium salts** | Soluble | None |
| **All chlorides** | Soluble | AgCl (insoluble), PbCl₂ (insoluble) |
| **All sulfates** | Soluble | BaSO₄, PbSO₄, CaSO₄ (insoluble/sparingly) |
| **All carbonates** | Insoluble | Na₂CO₃, K₂CO₃, (NH₄)₂CO₃ (soluble) |
| **All hydroxides** | Insoluble | NaOH, KOH, Ba(OH)₂ (soluble); Ca(OH)₂ (slightly) |
| **All oxides** | Insoluble | Na₂O, K₂O (react with water) |

## 12.2 Preparation of Salts

The method of preparation depends on the solubility of the salt:

### Method 1: Acid + Metal (for soluble salts; metals above H in series)
$$\text{Zn}(s) + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4(aq) \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4(aq) + \text{H}_2(g)$$

**Procedure:**
1. Add excess metal to acid (excess ensures all acid is used)
2. Filter off excess metal
3. Evaporate/crystallise the filtrate
4. Filter, wash with distilled water, dry crystals

### Method 2: Acid + Insoluble Base (Metal oxide or hydroxide) — titration not needed
$$\text{CuO}(s) + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4(aq) \rightarrow \text{CuSO}_4(aq) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$$

**Procedure:**
1. Warm acid, add excess insoluble base (e.g. CuO) until no more dissolves
2. Filter off excess base
3. Evaporate filtrate to crystallise
4. Filter, wash, dry

### Method 3: Neutralisation with titration (for soluble acid + soluble alkali)

Used when both reactants are soluble (e.g. NaOH + HCl → NaCl)

**Procedure:**
1. Use indicator (or pH meter) to find exact volume of acid needed to neutralise alkali
2. Record volumes
3. Repeat without indicator (same volumes)
4. Evaporate to crystallise the pure salt

### Method 4: Precipitation (for insoluble salts)

$$\text{Pb(NO}_3)_2(aq) + \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4(aq) \rightarrow \text{PbSO}_4(s) + 2\text{NaNO}_3(aq)$$

**Procedure:**
1. Mix solutions of two soluble salts whose ions form an insoluble product
2. Filter off the precipitate
3. Wash with distilled water to remove soluble impurities
4. Dry the precipitate

**Common precipitate preparations:**

| Insoluble salt | Mix these solutions |
|---------------|-------------------|
| BaSO₄ | BaCl₂(aq) + Na₂SO₄(aq) |
| AgCl | AgNO₃(aq) + NaCl(aq) |
| PbSO₄ | Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + Na₂SO₄(aq) |
| PbI₂ | Pb(NO₃)₂(aq) + KI(aq) |
| CaCO₃ | CaCl₂(aq) + Na₂CO₃(aq) |
| BaCO₃ | BaCl₂(aq) + Na₂CO₃(aq) |

### Summary: Which Method for Which Salt?

| Salt required | Solubility | Method |
|--------------|-----------|--------|
| ZnSO₄, MgSO₄, FeSO₄ | Soluble | Method 1 (metal + acid) |
| CuSO₄, CuCl₂ | Soluble | Method 2 (insoluble base + acid) |
| NaCl, KNO₃, NH₄Cl | Soluble | Method 3 (neutralisation + titration) |
| BaSO₄, AgCl, PbSO₄ | Insoluble | Method 4 (precipitation) |

### Crystallisation
1. Evaporate solution until **saturated** (test: small crystal forms on glass rod)
2. Allow to **cool slowly** — large crystals form
3. Filter crystals from mother liquor
4. Wash with cold distilled water (remove impurities)
5. **Pat dry** between filter papers or dry in oven at low temperature

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Salt | Ionic compound formed when H⁺ of acid is replaced by metal ion or NH₄⁺ |
| Precipitate | Insoluble solid formed when two solutions are mixed |
| Filtrate | Liquid that passes through the filter |
| Crystallisation | Process of forming pure crystals from a saturated solution by cooling |
| Titration | Technique for measuring exact volumes of solutions that react completely |
