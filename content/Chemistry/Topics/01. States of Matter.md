## 1.1 States of Matter

The three states of matter differ in arrangement, movement and energy of their particles.

| Property | Solid | Liquid | Gas |
|----------|-------|--------|-----|
| **Shape** | Fixed | Takes shape of container | Fills whole container |
| **Volume** | Fixed | Fixed | Not fixed |
| **Particle arrangement** | Regular, close-packed | Irregular, close together | Irregular, far apart |
| **Particle movement** | Vibrate about fixed positions | Slide past each other | Move rapidly in all directions |
| **Forces** | Very strong | Moderate | Very weak / negligible |
| **Compressibility** | Not compressible | Not compressible | Easily compressible |
| **Density** | High | High | Very low |

### Changes of State

| Change | Direction | Energy |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Melting | Solid → Liquid | Absorbed (endothermic) |
| Freezing | Liquid → Solid | Released (exothermic) |
| Boiling / Evaporation | Liquid → Gas | Absorbed |
| Condensation | Gas → Liquid | Released |
| Sublimation | Solid → Gas | Absorbed |
| Deposition | Gas → Solid | Released |

> ⚠️ **During a change of state, temperature stays constant** — energy is used to break/form intermolecular forces, not to raise kinetic energy. This produces the **flat sections** on a heating curve.

### Heating Curve Key Points
- Slope = energy input raising temperature
- **Flat section 1** (melting point): solid → liquid
- **Flat section 2** (boiling point): liquid → gas
- Gradient depends on specific heat capacity of the substance
- Impurities: **lower** the melting point and broaden the range (used as a purity test)

## 1.2 Kinetic Particle Theory of Matter

> The **kinetic particle theory** states that all matter consists of particles in constant random motion, and temperature is a measure of the **average kinetic energy** of those particles.

### Key Principles

- Higher temperature → particles have more kinetic energy → move faster
- Particles in a substance have a **range of speeds** (Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution)
- At the same temperature, **lighter particles move faster** on average than heavier ones

### Explaining Properties Using KPT

**Why solids are rigid:** Particles locked in fixed lattice positions by strong forces; only vibrate.

**Why liquids flow:** Particles have enough energy to overcome some forces and slide past each other.

**Why gases fill containers:** Particles have high KE, negligible intermolecular forces — spread out to fill space.

**Why gases are compressible:** Large spaces between particles allow compression.

**Gas pressure:** Billions of particle collisions with container walls per second → net outward force = pressure.

### Gas Law Relationships (qualitative)
| Change | Effect on pressure (constant volume) | Reason |
|--------|--------------------------------------|--------|
| Temperature ↑ | Pressure ↑ | More frequent, harder collisions |
| Temperature ↓ | Pressure ↓ | Less frequent, softer collisions |

**Boyle's Law** (constant temperature): P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ — decrease volume → particles collide more frequently → pressure rises.

### Evaporation vs Boiling

| Feature | Evaporation | Boiling |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Temperature | Any temperature (below b.p.) | Specific boiling point only |
| Where | Surface only | Throughout the liquid |
| Bubbles | No | Yes |
| Speed | Slow | Fast |

**Evaporation causes cooling:** The fastest-moving (highest KE) particles escape → average KE of remaining liquid drops → temperature falls.

## 1.3 Mixtures of Substances and Diffusion

### Pure Substances vs Mixtures

| | Pure substance | Mixture |
|-|---------------|---------|
| Composition | Fixed | Variable |
| Melting/boiling point | **Sharp and fixed** | Range (impure) |
| Properties | Fully defined | Depend on components |
| Can be separated? | No (by physical means) | Yes |

### Diffusion

> **Diffusion** is the net movement of particles from a region of **higher** concentration to a region of **lower** concentration, due to random particle motion.

- Occurs in **gases** and **liquids** (not solids)
- Passive — no energy input needed
- Rate increases with: **higher temperature**, **lower molecular mass**

### Ammonia–HCl Experiment
- NH₃ gas (Mr = 17) and HCl gas (Mr = 36.5) released at opposite ends of a tube
- NH₃ is lighter → travels **faster** and **further**
- White NH₄Cl ring forms **closer to the HCl end**

$$\text{NH}_3(g) + \text{HCl}(g) \rightarrow \text{NH}_4\text{Cl}(s) \text{ (white solid)}$$

### Solutions, Colloids and Suspensions

| Type | Particle size | Settles? | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|---------|
| Solution | < 1 nm | No | Salt water |
| Colloid | 1–100 nm | No | Milk, fog |
| Suspension | > 100 nm | Yes | Chalk in water |

### Solubility Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Solute** | Substance dissolved |
| **Solvent** | Liquid that dissolves the solute |
| **Solution** | Homogeneous mixture of solute + solvent |
| **Saturated solution** | Maximum solute dissolved at a given temperature |
| **Solubility** | Mass (g) of solute in 100 g solvent at a stated temperature |

- Solubility of **solids** generally increases with temperature
- Solubility of **gases** decreases with temperature (e.g. CO₂ in drinks)

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Kinetic particle theory | All matter is made of particles in constant random motion |
| Diffusion | Net movement of particles from high to low concentration |
| Evaporation | Faster-moving liquid particles escaping from the surface |
| Pure substance | Substance with a sharp, fixed melting/boiling point |
| Saturated solution | Contains maximum dissolved solute at that temperature |
