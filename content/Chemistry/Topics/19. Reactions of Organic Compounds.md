## 19.1 Characteristic Reactions of Different Homologous Series

### Alkane Reactions

**1. Combustion (most important):**

Complete: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O (excess O₂)
Incomplete: 2CH₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO + 4H₂O (limited O₂)

**2. Substitution reactions with halogens (in UV light):**

$$\text{CH}_4 + \text{Cl}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{UV}} \text{CH}_3\text{Cl} + \text{HCl}$$

- UV light provides energy to break Cl–Cl bond → free radicals
- H atoms progressively replaced: CH₄ → CH₃Cl → CH₂Cl₂ → CHCl₃ → CCl₄
- Alkanes are generally **unreactive** except with halogens (UV) and in combustion

### Alkene Reactions

Alkenes are more reactive than alkanes due to the **C=C double bond** (acts as a site for reactions).

**1. Combustion** (same pattern as alkanes but tend to produce sootier flames — higher C:H ratio)
$$\text{C}_2\text{H}_4 + 3\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{CO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**2. Addition reactions** — the double bond breaks and atoms are added across it:

| Reaction | Reagent | Condition | Product |
|----------|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Hydrogenation** | H₂ | Ni catalyst, 150°C | Alkane (C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆) |
| **Hydration** | H₂O (steam) | H₃PO₄ catalyst, 300°C, 60 atm | Alcohol (C₂H₄ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH) |
| **Addition of bromine** | Br₂ (in water or CCl₄) | Room temperature | Dibromoalkane; bromine water decolourises |
| **Addition of HBr** | HBr(g) | Room temperature | Bromoalkane (C₂H₄ + HBr → CH₃CH₂Br) |
| **Polymerisation** | Many monomers | Initiator/high P/T | Addition polymer |

**Test for alkene:** Shake with **bromine water (orange)** → goes **colourless** (addition of Br₂ across C=C)

Alkanes do NOT decolourise bromine water in the dark (they require UV light).

## 19.2 Chemistry of Ethanol

**Molecular formula:** C₂H₅OH (or C₂H₆O)
**Structural formula:** CH₃–CH₂–OH

### Production of Ethanol

**Method 1 — Fermentation (batch process):**

$$\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6(aq) \xrightarrow{\text{yeast, 25–35°C, anaerobic}} 2\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}(aq) + 2\text{CO}_2(g)$$

- Raw material: glucose from sugar cane, wheat, maize
- Catalyst: enzymes in yeast
- Conditions: ~30°C, no oxygen (anaerobic), pH ~4–5
- Maximum ethanol ~15% (higher concentrations kill yeast)
- Ethanol separated by **fractional distillation**

**Method 2 — Hydration of ethene (continuous process):**

$$\text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2(g) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(g) \xrightarrow{\text{H}_3\text{PO}_4, 300°C, 60 atm} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH}(g)$$

- Raw material: ethene from cracking crude oil
- Catalyst: phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄)
- Continuous process — faster and purer product

### Comparison: Fermentation vs Hydration

| Feature | Fermentation | Hydration of Ethene |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Raw material | Glucose (sugar/starch) — **renewable** | Ethene (from crude oil) — **non-renewable** |
| Conditions | ~30°C, atmospheric pressure | 300°C, 60 atm — high energy |
| Rate | Slow, batch process | Fast, continuous |
| Purity | Impure (needs distillation) | High purity |
| Sustainability | Renewable feedstock | Non-renewable |

### Reactions of Ethanol

**1. Combustion:**
$$\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}(l) + 3\text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow 2\text{CO}_2(g) + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$$
(Used as a fuel — biofuel)

**2. Oxidation to ethanoic acid (by bacteria or K₂Cr₂O₇/H⁺):**
$$\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH} \xrightarrow{[\text{O}]} \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$$
(In wine-making: ethanol → ethanoic acid when air enters = wine vinegar)

**3. Dehydration to ethene:**
$$\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}(g) \xrightarrow{\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3, 180°C} \text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2(g) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(g)$$

**4. Esterification** (see 19.3)

## 19.3 Carboxylic Acids and Esters

### Carboxylic Acids

Functional group: **–COOH** (carboxyl group)

| Name | Formula |
|------|---------|
| Methanoic acid | HCOOH |
| Ethanoic acid | CH₃COOH |
| Propanoic acid | C₂H₅COOH |
| Butanoic acid | C₃H₇COOH |

**Properties:**
- Weak acids (partially ionise): CH₃COOH ⇌ CH₃COO⁻ + H⁺
- pH ~3 for ethanoic acid (dilute)
- React with carbonates, metals, metal oxides, bases (like other acids)

**Reactions of ethanoic acid:**
$$2\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 \rightarrow 2\text{CH}_3\text{COONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2$$
$$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

### Esters

> An **ester** is formed by the **condensation reaction** (esterification) of a **carboxylic acid** and an **alcohol**, with concentrated H₂SO₄ as catalyst and water produced.

$$\underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}}_{\text{acid}} + \underbrace{\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}}_{\text{alcohol}} \underset{-H_2O}{\xrightarrow{\text{conc. H}_2\text{SO}_4, \Delta}} \underbrace{\text{CH}_3\text{COOC}_2\text{H}_5}_{\text{ester}} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$

**Naming esters:** [alkyl] [acid anion ending in -oate]
- CH₃COOC₂H₅ = **ethyl ethanoate** (from ethanoic acid + ethanol)
- HCOOCH₃ = **methyl methanoate** (from methanoic acid + methanol)
- CH₃COOCH₃ = **methyl ethanoate** (from ethanoic acid + methanol)

**Properties of esters:**
- Pleasant fruity smells (used as food flavourings and perfumes)
- Insoluble or slightly soluble in water
- Volatile — evaporate quickly (used as solvents: nail varnish remover = ethyl ethanoate)

**Hydrolysis of esters (reverse of esterification):**
$$\text{Ester} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \underset{\text{acid/base catalyst}}{\rightleftharpoons} \text{Alcohol} + \text{Carboxylic acid}$$

### Fats and Oils
- Naturally occurring esters (glycerol + 3 fatty acids = triglyceride)
- **Oils:** unsaturated fatty acids (liquid) — contain C=C bonds
- **Fats:** saturated fatty acids (solid)
- Margarine made by **hydrogenation** of vegetable oils (Ni catalyst): C=C + H₂ → C–C

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Addition reaction | Reaction where atoms/molecules add across a double bond |
| Substitution reaction | One atom/group replaced by another |
| Fermentation | Conversion of glucose to ethanol and CO₂ by enzymes in yeast |
| Esterification | Condensation reaction of acid and alcohol to form ester + water |
| Hydrolysis | Reaction with water to break a bond (reverse of condensation) |
| Biofuel | Fuel produced from biological (renewable) sources |
