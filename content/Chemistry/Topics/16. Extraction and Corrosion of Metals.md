## 16.1 Metal Extraction and the Reactivity Series

> Most metals occur naturally as **ores** (compounds in rocks). Extraction = obtaining pure metal from its ore. The method depends on the metal's position in the reactivity series.

### Extraction Methods by Reactivity

| Position in series | Metal | Method | Reason |
|-------------------|-------|--------|--------|
| Very reactive (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al) | Potassium, sodium, magnesium, aluminium | **Electrolysis of molten ore** | Too reactive to be reduced by carbon |
| Moderate (Zn, Fe, Sn, Pb) | Zinc, iron, tin, lead | **Reduction by carbon (coke)** in a blast furnace or similar | Carbon is a cheaper, more accessible reducing agent |
| Below hydrogen (Cu, Ag, Au) | Copper, silver, gold | **Found native or easily reduced / electrolytic refining** | Low reactivity; Cu purified by electrolysis |

---

### Extraction of Iron — Blast Furnace

**Raw materials:** Iron ore (Fe₂O₃ / haematite), coke (C), limestone (CaCO₃), hot air

**Reactions in the blast furnace:**

1. **Burning coke** — produces heat:
$$\text{C}(s) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{CO}_2(g)$$

2. **Formation of CO** (the actual reducing agent):
$$\text{CO}_2(g) + \text{C}(s) \rightarrow 2\text{CO}(g)$$

3. **Reduction of iron ore:**
$$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3(s) + 3\text{CO}(g) \rightarrow 2\text{Fe}(l) + 3\text{CO}_2(g)$$

4. **Limestone decomposes** (removes acidic impurities):
$$\text{CaCO}_3(s) \xrightarrow{\Delta} \text{CaO}(s) + \text{CO}_2(g)$$

5. **CaO reacts with SiO₂** (silica impurity) to form slag:
$$\text{CaO}(s) + \text{SiO}_2(s) \rightarrow \text{CaSiO}_3(l) \text{ (slag)}$$

**Output:**
- Molten iron (pig iron) — contains ~4% C; brittle
- Slag (calcium silicate) — used in road construction

**Pig iron → Steel:** Remove excess carbon by blowing oxygen through molten pig iron; add alloying elements

### Extraction of Aluminium — Electrolysis

**Ore:** Bauxite (Al₂O₃ + impurities)

**Purification:** Bauxite refined to pure aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) by Bayer process

**Why electrolysis?** Al is too reactive to be reduced by carbon.

**Electrolyte:** Molten Al₂O₃ dissolved in **cryolite** (Na₃AlF₆)
- Cryolite lowers the melting point of Al₂O₃ from ~2050°C to ~950°C → **saves energy**

**Electrodes:** Carbon (graphite) — both anode and cathode

**Half-equations:**
- **Cathode:** Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (liquid, sinks to bottom, tapped off)
- **Anode:** 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻

**Problem with anodes:** Oxygen produced at anode reacts with the carbon:
$$\text{C}(s) + \text{O}_2(g) \rightarrow \text{CO}_2(g)$$
→ Carbon anodes burn away and must be **replaced regularly**.

**Why Al is expensive:** Large amounts of electrical energy required; anodes need regular replacement.

---

### Extraction of Copper — Electrolytic Refining

Impure copper (from smelting) is purified by **electrolysis**:
- **Anode** = impure copper (dissolves: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻)
- **Cathode** = pure copper (grows: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu)
- **Electrolyte** = copper(II) sulfate solution

Less reactive impurities (Ag, Au) fall to the bottom as **anode sludge** — valuable by-product.

## 16.2 Corrosion of Metals

> **Corrosion** is the degradation of a metal by chemical reaction with its environment (typically reaction with oxygen and/or water).

### Rusting of Iron

**Conditions required:** Both water (H₂O) AND oxygen (O₂) are required. (Salt and acid accelerate rusting.)

$$4\text{Fe}(s) + 3\text{O}_2(g) + x\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow 2\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 \cdot x\text{H}_2\text{O}(s) \quad \text{(hydrated iron(III) oxide = rust)}$$

**Proving both O₂ and H₂O are needed:**
| Tube | Contents | Result |
|------|----------|--------|
| A | Nail + boiled water (no O₂) + oil layer | No rust |
| B | Nail + drying agent (no H₂O) + air | No rust |
| C | Nail + tap water + air | Rusts |

### Methods of Rust Prevention

| Method | How it works | Examples |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Painting** | Physical barrier to water and O₂ | Bridges, car bodies |
| **Greasing/oiling** | Physical barrier | Tools, machinery |
| **Plastic/polymer coating** | Physical barrier | Dishwashers, garden furniture |
| **Electroplating** | Deposits thin layer of unreactive metal | Tin-plating (food cans), chromium-plating (taps) |
| **Galvanising (zinc coating)** | Physical barrier + sacrificial protection | Corrugated iron, gates |
| **Sacrificial protection** | More reactive metal (Mg or Zn) attached; corrodes first | Ship hulls (Mg blocks), underground pipelines |
| **Alloying** | Change composition → corrosion resistant | Stainless steel (+ Cr, Ni) |

### Sacrificial Protection in Detail
- Zinc is more reactive than iron
- Zinc is oxidised preferentially: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
- Iron is protected even if coating is scratched (as long as Zn is present)
- Used: galvanised steel, sacrificial anodes on ships and pipelines

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Ore | Naturally occurring rock containing a metal compound in sufficient quantity to be worth extracting |
| Smelting | Extraction of a metal from its ore by reduction, usually using carbon |
| Electrolysis | Decomposition using electricity; used for very reactive metals |
| Rusting | Corrosion of iron in the presence of both water and oxygen |
| Sacrificial protection | Using a more reactive metal to protect a less reactive one from corrosion |
| Galvanising | Coating iron/steel with zinc to prevent rusting |
