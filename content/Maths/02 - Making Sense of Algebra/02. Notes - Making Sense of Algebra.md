# 02. Making Sense of Algebra

> [!note] Chapter Summary
> Algebra is the language of mathematics — using letters to represent unknown or variable quantities. This chapter covers algebraic notation, simplifying expressions, expanding brackets, factorising, and substitution.

## Algebraic Notation

| Expression | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| 3a | 3 × a (the × is dropped) |
| ab | a × b |
| a² | a × a |
| a/b | a ÷ b |
| 2(x + 3) | 2 × (x + 3) |
| ³√a | cube root of a |

> [!tip] Convention
> Write number × letter (not letter × number): write 5x, not x5.
> The number part is the **coefficient**. In 7y², the coefficient is 7.

### Terms and Expressions
- **Term**: a single algebraic quantity — e.g. 3x, −2y², 5
- **Expression**: a combination of terms — e.g. 3x² − 2x + 5
- **Equation**: an expression set equal to something — e.g. 3x + 1 = 10
- **Identity**: true for all values — e.g. (x+1)² ≡ x² + 2x + 1
- **Formula**: a rule connecting variables — e.g. A = πr²

## Like Terms and Simplifying

**Like terms** have exactly the same variable(s) raised to the same power(s).

| Like terms | Not like terms |
|-----------|---------------|
| 3x and 7x | 3x and 3x² |
| 5ab and −2ab | 5ab and 5a |
| 4y² and −y² | 3xy and 3x |

Collect like terms by adding/subtracting their coefficients:

> [!example] Simplify: 4x² + 3x − x² + 5 − 7x + 2
> Group: (4x² − x²) + (3x − 7x) + (5 + 2)
> = **3x² − 4x + 7**

## Index Laws in Algebra

| Law | Example |
|-----|---------|
| xᵐ × xⁿ = xᵐ⁺ⁿ | x³ × x⁴ = x⁷ |
| xᵐ ÷ xⁿ = xᵐ⁻ⁿ | x⁶ ÷ x² = x⁴ |
| (xᵐ)ⁿ = xᵐⁿ | (x³)⁴ = x¹² |
| x⁰ = 1 | 5x⁰ = 5 |
| x⁻ⁿ = 1/xⁿ | x⁻³ = 1/x³ |
| x^(1/2) = √x | x^(1/3) = ³√x |

> [!example] Simplify: (3x²y)³
> = 3³ × x⁶ × y³ = **27x⁶y³**

> [!example] Simplify: (2x³)² ÷ (4x)
> = 4x⁶ ÷ 4x = **x⁵**

## Expanding Brackets

### Single Bracket
Multiply every term inside the bracket by the term outside.

a(b + c) = ab + ac

> [!example] Expand: 3x(2x − 5)
> = 3x × 2x + 3x × (−5)
> = **6x² − 15x**

### Double Brackets (FOIL / Grid Method)
(a + b)(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd

> [!example] Expand: (x + 3)(x − 2)
> F: x × x = x²
> O: x × (−2) = −2x
> I: 3 × x = 3x
> L: 3 × (−2) = −6
> = **x² + x − 6**

### Special Products — must memorise

| Identity | Expanded Form |
|----------|--------------|
| (a + b)² | a² + 2ab + b² |
| (a − b)² | a² − 2ab + b² |
| (a + b)(a − b) | a² − b² |

> [!warning] Common Mistake
> (x + 3)² ≠ x² + 9
> (x + 3)² = x² + **6x** + 9 ← the middle term is always missed!

### Three Brackets
Expand two of them first, then multiply by the third.

> [!example] Expand: (x + 1)(x + 2)(x + 3)
> First: (x + 1)(x + 2) = x² + 3x + 2
> Then: (x² + 3x + 2)(x + 3)
>     = x³ + 3x² + 3x² + 9x + 2x + 6
>     = **x³ + 6x² + 11x + 6**

## Factorising

Factorising is the reverse of expanding — expressing as a product.

### Common Factor
Find the HCF of all terms and pull it out.

> [!example] Factorise: 6x² − 9x
> HCF of 6x² and 9x is 3x
> = **3x(2x − 3)**

### Grouping (four terms)
> [!example] Factorise: ax + ay + bx + by
> = a(x + y) + b(x + y)
> = **(a + b)(x + y)**

### Difference of Two Squares
a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b)

> [!example] Factorise: 4x² − 25
> = (2x)² − 5² = **(2x + 5)(2x − 5)**

### Factorising Quadratics: x² + bx + c
Find two numbers that **multiply to c** and **add to b**.

> [!example] Factorise: x² + 7x + 12
> Need two numbers: product = 12, sum = 7 → **3 and 4**
> = **(x + 3)(x + 4)**

> [!example] Factorise: x² − 5x − 14
> Product = −14, sum = −5 → **−7 and +2**
> = **(x − 7)(x + 2)**

### Factorising Quadratics: ax² + bx + c (a ≠ 1)
Method: Multiply a × c, find two numbers with that product and sum b, split the middle term.

> [!example] Factorise: 2x² + 7x + 3
> a × c = 6; need product 6, sum 7 → 1 and 6
> = 2x² + x + 6x + 3
> = x(2x + 1) + 3(2x + 1)
> = **(x + 3)(2x + 1)**

## Substitution

Replace each variable with a given number and evaluate.

> [!example] If a = 3, b = −2, find: 2a² − 3b + 1
> = 2(3)² − 3(−2) + 1
> = 2(9) + 6 + 1
> = 18 + 6 + 1 = **25**

> [!warning] Always substitute into brackets first: a² means (a)², not a then squared separately.

---

## Changing the Subject of a Formula

Rearrange so that the desired variable is on its own on the left.

> [!example] Make r the subject of: A = πr²
> A/π = r²
> r = **√(A/π)**

> [!example] Make u the subject of: v = u + at
> u = **v − at**

> [!example] Make x the subject of: y = (3x + 1)/(x − 2)
> y(x − 2) = 3x + 1
> xy − 2y = 3x + 1
> xy − 3x = 2y + 1
> x(y − 3) = 2y + 1
> x = **(2y + 1)/(y − 3)**

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Coefficient | The number multiplying a variable in a term |
| Like terms | Terms with the same variable(s) to the same power |
| Expression | A combination of terms with no equals sign |
| Equation | A statement of equality between two expressions |
| Identity (≡) | True for all values of the variable |
| Formula | A rule connecting variables |
| Expand | Multiply out brackets |
| Factorise | Express as a product of factors |
| Substitute | Replace variables with given numerical values |
| Subject | The variable on its own on one side of a formula |
