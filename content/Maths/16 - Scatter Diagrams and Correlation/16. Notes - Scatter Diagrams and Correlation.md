## Scatter Diagrams

A **scatter diagram** (scatter graph) plots pairs of values as points — one variable on each axis.

Used to investigate whether there is a **relationship (correlation)** between two variables.

---

## Correlation

### Positive Correlation
As one variable increases, the other **increases** too.
→ Points slope upward left to right.

> [!example] Height and shoe size — taller people tend to have larger feet.

### Negative Correlation
As one variable increases, the other **decreases**.
→ Points slope downward left to right.

> [!example] Temperature and hot drink sales — warmer weather → fewer hot drinks sold.

### No Correlation
No pattern in the scatter of points.

> [!example] Shoe size and IQ — no relationship expected.

### Strength of Correlation

| Description | Appearance |
|------------|-----------|
| Strong positive | Points close to an upward line |
| Weak positive | Points loosely scattered upward |
| Strong negative | Points close to a downward line |
| Weak negative | Points loosely scattered downward |
| No correlation | Random scatter |

---

## Line of Best Fit

A line of best fit (trend line) is drawn **through the middle** of the data points, with roughly equal numbers of points on each side.

### Rules for Drawing:
1. The line **does not have to pass through the origin** (unless the data clearly suggests it)
2. The line should pass through or near the **mean point** (x̄, ȳ)
3. There should be roughly **equal numbers of points** above and below the line
4. The line should reflect the general direction of the data

> [!tip] Always calculate and plot the mean point (x̄, ȳ) first — your line of best fit should pass through it.

---

## Using the Line of Best Fit

### Interpolation
Predicting a value **within** the range of the data. This is **reliable**.

### Extrapolation
Predicting a value **outside** the range of the data. This is **unreliable** — the relationship may not continue beyond the data range.

> [!example] Data: hours revised (0–10) vs. test score (20–90)
> Using line of best fit to predict score for 7 hours (within range) → interpolation, reliable
> Using it to predict score for 15 hours (outside range) → extrapolation, unreliable

---

## Correlation vs Causation

> [!warning] Correlation does NOT imply causation!
> Two variables can be correlated without one causing the other.
> Example: Ice cream sales and drowning deaths both increase in summer — but ice cream doesn't cause drowning. Both are caused by a third variable (hot weather).

---

## Exam Technique

When asked to describe correlation:
1. State the **type**: positive / negative / no correlation
2. State the **strength**: strong / weak
3. State the **context**: "As X increases, Y tends to increase/decrease"

> [!example] "There is a **strong positive** correlation — as the number of hours studied increases, the test score tends to increase."

---

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Scatter diagram | Graph plotting pairs of data values as points |
| Correlation | A relationship (linear trend) between two variables |
| Positive correlation | Both variables increase together |
| Negative correlation | One increases as the other decreases |
| Line of best fit | A straight line representing the trend through the data |
| Mean point | (x̄, ȳ) — the line of best fit should pass through this |
| Interpolation | Prediction within the data range |
| Extrapolation | Prediction beyond the data range (less reliable) |
| Causation | One variable directly causes a change in another |
