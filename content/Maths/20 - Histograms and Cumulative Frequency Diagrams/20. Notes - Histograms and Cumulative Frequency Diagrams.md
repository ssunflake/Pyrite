## Histograms

A **histogram** is used for **continuous grouped data**. It looks like a bar chart but:
- There are **no gaps** between bars
- The **area** of each bar represents the frequency (NOT the height)
- The y-axis shows **frequency density**, not frequency

### Frequency Density

$$\text{Frequency Density} = \frac{\text{Frequency}}{\text{Class Width}}$$

Rearranging:
$$\text{Frequency} = \text{Frequency Density} \times \text{Class Width}$$

> [!example]
> | Class | Freq | Class width | Freq density |
> |-------|------|------------|-------------|
> | 0 ≤ x < 10 | 20 | 10 | 2.0 |
> | 10 ≤ x < 15 | 25 | 5 | 5.0 |
> | 15 ≤ x < 25 | 30 | 10 | 3.0 |
> | 25 ≤ x < 40 | 45 | 15 | 3.0 |

Plot frequency density on the y-axis against the class boundaries on the x-axis.

> [!warning] Unequal class widths are normal in histograms — this is exactly why we use frequency density instead of frequency. A taller bar does NOT always mean higher frequency.

### Reading Frequency from a Histogram
Frequency = Frequency Density × Class Width = **Area of bar**

> [!example] Bar from 20 to 30, height (FD) = 4.5
> Frequency = 4.5 × 10 = **45**

## Cumulative Frequency

**Cumulative frequency** = running total of frequencies up to and including each class.

> [!example]
> | Class | Freq | Cumulative Freq |
> |-------|------|----------------|
> | 0–10 | 5 | 5 |
> | 10–20 | 12 | 17 |
> | 20–30 | 18 | 35 |
> | 30–40 | 10 | 45 |
> | 40–50 | 5 | 50 |

---

## Cumulative Frequency Graph (Ogive)

- Plot **cumulative frequency** against the **upper boundary** of each class
- Join points with a smooth S-shaped curve (ogive)
- The curve always starts at (lower boundary of first class, 0)

> [!tip] Always plot at the **upper class boundary**, not the midpoint.

### Reading Off the Graph

For total frequency n:

| Quantity | Position on y-axis | Read off x |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Median (Q2) | n/2 | → draw horizontal → read x |
| Lower quartile (Q1) | n/4 | → draw horizontal → read x |
| Upper quartile (Q3) | 3n/4 | → draw horizontal → read x |
| IQR | Q3 − Q1 | (calculated) |
| Percentile p | p×n/100 | → read x |

> [!example] n = 80. Find median.
> Go to y = 40 on cumulative frequency axis → draw horizontal → read off x value = e.g. **32.5**

### Estimating Number Above/Below a Value
- To find how many are below a value: read off the cumulative frequency at that x-value
- To find how many are above: total − cumulative frequency at that x

## Box Plots from Cumulative Frequency

Read Q1, median, Q3 from the graph, note min and max, then draw a box plot.

```
|─────|──────|──|──────|──────|
Min  Q1   Median  Q3   Max
```

## Comparing Distributions using Cumulative Frequency

> [!example] Group A has median 55, IQR 20. Group B has median 62, IQR 35.
> "Group B performed better on average (higher median) but Group A was more consistent (smaller IQR)."

---

## Frequency Polygons (Recap)

- Plot frequency (or frequency density) at the **midpoint** of each class
- Join all points with straight lines
- Useful for comparing two distributions on the same axes

---

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Histogram | Bar chart for continuous data where area = frequency |
| Frequency density | Frequency ÷ class width; the y-axis of a histogram |
| Cumulative frequency | Running total of frequencies |
| Ogive | S-shaped cumulative frequency curve |
| Quartile | Q1, Q2, Q3 divide the data into four equal parts |
| Percentile | The value below which p% of the data falls |
| IQR | Interquartile range = Q3 − Q1 |
