## 3.1 Overview

English has **24 consonant phonemes**. Each can be described by three features: **place of articulation**, **manner of articulation**, and **voicing**.

## 3.3 Tricky Sounds — Special Notes

### /θ/ vs /ð/ — both spelled "th"
- /θ/ is **voiceless**: *think, bath, teeth, method, breath*
- /ð/ is **voiced**: *the, this, that, breathe, father, bathe*
- **Test:** Hand on throat — /ð/ vibrates, /θ/ does not
- Most **function words** (the, this, that, they, there, them, those, then, though, thus) use /ð/
- Most **content words** with *th* use /θ/ (think, three, through, throw)

### /ŋ/ — the velar nasal
- **Never** appears at the start of English words
- Spelled **-ng** (sing, ring, strong) or **-nk** (sink, think → /ŋk/)
- *Singer* = /ˈsɪŋər/ — the /g/ drops (derived from *sing*)
- *Finger* = /ˈfɪŋgər/ — the /g/ stays (it's part of the root)

### /r/ — rhotic vs non-rhotic
- In **RP (British)**: /r/ is only pronounced *before* a vowel — *red, carry, very* — but silent in *car, bird, here*
- **Linking-r**: when a word ending in written *r* is followed by a vowel: *far away* → /fɑːr əˈweɪ/
- **Intrusive-r**: speakers sometimes insert /r/ between vowels even where there's no written *r*: *law and order* → /lɔːr ənd ˈɔːdə/

### /h/ — sometimes silent
Words with silent h: *hour, honest, heir, honour, vehicle, ghost, rhyme, rhythm*

### /j/ — the palatal approximant
Appears as:
- Spelled **y**: *yes, year, yellow*
- Part of **/juː/**: *new, few, beauty, use, cute, huge*
- In GenAm, /j/ is often dropped after alveolars: *new* = /nuː/ (not /njuː/)

### /w/ — silent in wr- words
*write, wrong, wrist, wrap, wrestle, wreck, wren* — all have silent w

## 3.4 Consonant Clusters

English allows complex consonant clusters (multiple consonants in a row without intervening vowels):

| Position | Common Clusters | Examples |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| **Initial (onset)** | /str-/, /spr-/, /spl-/, /skr-/, /thr-/, /shr-/, /sk-/, /st-/, /sp-/ | *street, spring, splash, scream, three, shrink* |
| **Final (coda)** | /-nd/, /-nt/, /-ŋk/, /-sts/, /-lts/, /-lps/, /-ksθs/ | *sand, hint, sink, tests, bolts, helps, sixths* |

> [!note] *sixths* /sɪksθs/ has four final consonants — among the most complex codas in English. Many speakers simplify these in natural speech.

## 3.6 Aspiration

Voiceless plosives /p t k/ are **aspirated** (followed by a puff of air — transcribed [pʰ tʰ kʰ]) in certain positions:

| Position | Aspiration | Example | Transcription |
|----------|-----------|---------|---------------|
| **Start of stressed syllable** | YES — aspirated | *pin* | [pʰɪn] |
| **After /s/** | NO — unaspirated | *spin* | [spɪn] |
| **Unstressed syllable** | Weak / no aspiration | *upper* | /ˈʌp.ə/ |

> 💡 **Test:** Hold a thin piece of paper in front of your mouth. Say *pin* — the paper should flutter. Say *spin* — it should barely move.

> [!note] Aspiration is **allophonic** in English (doesn't change meaning). In languages like Thai, Hindi, and Mandarin, aspirated vs unaspirated plosives are **distinct phonemes** — swapping them changes words entirely.

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Consonant cluster** | Two or more consonants in a row without a vowel between them |
| **Aspiration** | A small burst of air following a voiceless plosive, marked [ʰ] — e.g. [pʰ] in *pin* |
| **Allophone** | A phonetic variant of a phoneme that doesn't change meaning — e.g. aspirated vs unaspirated /p/ in English |
| **Linking-r** | In non-rhotic accents, a written *r* that resurfaces before a vowel-initial word |
| **Intrusive-r** | An /r/ inserted in speech between vowels even where there is no written *r* |
| **Velar nasal /ŋ/** | The nasal consonant made at the velum (soft palate); spelled *ng* or *n* before /k/ |
| **Dental fricative** | /θ/ and /ð/ — produced with the tongue touching the upper teeth, both spelled *th* |
| **Affricate** | A consonant combining a stop + fricative: /tʃ/ (chip) and /dʒ/ (jam) |
| **Minimal pair** | Two words differing in exactly one phoneme, proving two sounds are separate phonemes |
| **Yod-dropping** | The omission of /j/ after alveolars in words like *new* → /nuː/ (common in GenAm) |
