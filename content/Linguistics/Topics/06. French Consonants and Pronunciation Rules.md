## 6.1 Overview

French has **20 consonant phonemes**. Most are similar to English, but there are four key differences:
- **The French R (/ʁ/)** — uvular, produced at the back of the throat
- **The palatal nasal (/ɲ/)** — like "ny" in *canyon*, spelled *gn*
- **The labiopalatal approximant (/ɥ/)** — no English equivalent, spelled *u* before a vowel
- **Many final consonants are SILENT** — with liaison as the major exception

## 6.3 The French R — /ʁ/

The French R is a **uvular fricative or approximant** — the most iconic difference between French and English pronunciation.

**Where it's produced:** At the back of the throat, where the back of the tongue approaches the **uvula** (the soft tissue that hangs at the back of the mouth).

**How to produce it:**
1. Relax your mouth and tongue completely
2. Position the back of your tongue near the soft palate/uvula
3. Try to "gargle" at the back of your throat while voicing — this produces /ʁ/

| Feature | French /ʁ/ | English /r/ |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Place | Uvular (back of throat) | Post-alveolar (tongue tip, behind teeth) |
| Manner | Fricative / approximant | Approximant |
| Sound | Guttural, back-of-throat | Retroflex or bunched, front of mouth |
| Spelling | r, rr | r, rr, wr, rh |

Practice words: *rouge* /ʁuʒ/, *Paris* /paʁi/, *merci* /mɛʁsi/, *bonjour* /bɔ̃ʒuʁ/, *arrêt* /aʁɛ/

> ⚠️ **Common error:** Using an English /r/ or a Spanish trilled /r/ instead of the uvular /ʁ/. While some French dialects do use a uvular trill, the standard Parisian /ʁ/ is a fricative/approximant — not a trill.

## 6.4 GN = /ɲ/ — The Palatal Nasal

In French, *gn* represents the **single sound /ɲ/** — a palatal nasal. It is similar to the *ny* in English *canyon* or the *ñ* in Spanish.

| French | Pronunciation | English meaning |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| *champagne* | /ʃɑ̃paɲ/ | champagne |
| *montagne* | /mɔ̃taɲ/ | mountain |
| *agneau* | /aɲo/ | lamb |
| *Espagne* | /ɛspaɲ/ | Spain |
| *vigne* | /viɲ/ | vine |
| *oignon* | /ɔɲɔ̃/ | onion |

> [!note] *gn* is **never** pronounced as separate /g/ + /n/ in French.

## 6.5 CH = /ʃ/, NOT /tʃ/

In French, *ch* = **/ʃ/** (the English *sh* sound) — it is **never /tʃ/** (the English *ch* sound):

| French | IPA | English equivalent sound |
|--------|-----|--------------------------|
| *chat* | /ʃa/ | "sha" — like *sh* in *ship* |
| *chercher* | /ʃɛʁʃe/ | "shair-shay" |
| *Charles* | /ʃaʁl/ | "sharl" |

**Exceptions** (mostly borrowed words): *chrome* /kʁɔm/, *chaos* /kaos/, *choriste* /kɔʁist/

## 6.6 Silent Final Consonants

French final consonants are **usually silent**. Key mnemonic: **CaReFuL** — the consonants **C, R, F, L** are often *pronounced* at word endings; most others are silent.

| Consonant | Usually silent? | Exceptions / Notes | Examples |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|---------|
| **s** (plural, verb endings) | YES — silent | — | *les amis* /le zami/, *vous mangez* /vu mɑ̃ʒe/ |
| **t** (verb endings, many nouns/adjectives) | YES — silent | — | *il mange* /il mɑ̃ʒ/, *et* /e/, *vingt* /vɛ̃/ |
| **d** | YES — silent | — | *grand* /ɡʁɑ̃/, *pied* /pje/ |
| **x** (plurals) | YES — silent | *six, dix* pronounced /sis/, /dis/ when alone | *deux* /dø/, *voix* /vwa/ |
| **p** | YES — silent | — | *trop* /tʁo/, *beaucoup* /boku/, *loup* /lu/ |
| **g** (end of words) | YES — silent | — | *long* /lɔ̃/, *sang* /sɑ̃/ |
| **z** (verb endings) | YES — silent | — | *vous parlez* /vu paʁle/ |
| **c** (end) | Often **PRONOUNCED** | Some exceptions | *avec* /avɛk/, *chic* /ʃik/, *parc* /paʁk/ |
| **r** (end) | Often **PRONOUNCED** | **-er infinitives** → r is silent | *mer* /mɛʁ/, *sur* /syʁ/ BUT *parler* /paʁle/ |
| **f** (end) | Often **PRONOUNCED** | *neuf* + vowel → /v/ (liaison) | *neuf* /nœf/, *chef* /ʃɛf/ |
| **l** (end) | Often **PRONOUNCED** | — | *seul* /sœl/, *journal* /ʒuʁnal/, *il* /il/ |

> 💡 **CaReFuL memory tip:** The letters in **CaReFuL** = C, R, F, L — the ones most likely to be pronounced at word endings.

## 6.7 Liaison (La Liaison)

**Liaison** is when a normally silent final consonant **is** pronounced because the next word begins with a vowel or **mute h**. The consonant links to the start of the next word.

### Liaison Sound Changes

| Written consonant | Pronounced as | Examples |
|------------------|---------------|---------|
| s, x | /z/ | *les amis* → /le‿zami/, *deux enfants* → /dø‿zɑ̃fɑ̃/ |
| d | /t/ | *grand ami* → /ɡʁɑ̃‿tami/ |
| f (*neuf* only) | /v/ | *neuf ans* → /nœ‿vɑ̃/, *neuf heures* → /nœ‿vœʁ/ |
| n, t, r | same sound | *mon ami* → /mɔ̃‿nami/, *ils ont* → /il‿zɔ̃/ |

### Liaison Rules

| Type | Rule | Examples |
|------|------|---------|
| **Obligatoire** (mandatory) | After determiners: *les, des, ces, mes, tes, ses, nos, vos, leurs* | *les amis* /le‿zami/ |
| **Obligatoire** | After number before noun | *un enfant* /œ̃‿nɑ̃fɑ̃/, *deux enfants* /dø‿zɑ̃fɑ̃/ |
| **Obligatoire** | After pronoun before verb | *vous avez* /vu‿zave/, *ils ont* /il‿zɔ̃/ |
| **Obligatoire** | After adjective before noun | *un grand homme* /œ̃ ɡʁɑ̃‿tɔm/ |
| **Interdite** (forbidden) | After *et* | *et il* → /e il/ (NOT /e‿til/) |
| **Interdite** | Before **aspirate h** | *les haricots* → /le aʁiko/ (NO liaison) |
| **Interdite** | After a singular noun | *un enfant intelligent* — no liaison after *enfant* |
| **Facultative** (optional) | In formal or literary speech | More liaison = more formal register |

## 6.8 Elision (L'Élision)

**Elision** is the dropping of a final vowel (replaced by an apostrophe) when the next word begins with a vowel or mute h.

| Before elision | After elision | Examples |
|----------------|---------------|---------|
| *le* | *l'* | *l'ami* /lami/, *l'homme* /lɔm/, *l'eau* /lo/ |
| *la* | *l'* | *l'amie* /lami/, *l'heure* /lœʁ/, *l'école* /lekɔl/ |
| *je* | *j'* | *j'aime* /ʒɛm/, *j'ai* /ʒe/, *j'habite* /ʒabit/ |
| *me* | *m'* | *il m'appelle* /il mapɛl/, *elle m'a dit* |
| *te* | *t'* | *il t'aime* /il tɛm/ |
| *se* | *s'* | *il s'appelle* /il sapɛl/ |
| *de* | *d'* | *d'accord* /dakɔʁ/, *d'habitude* /dabityd/ |
| *ne* | *n'* | *n'est-ce pas?* /nɛs pa/, *je n'ai pas* /ʒ ne pa/ |
| *que* | *qu'* | *qu'est-ce que* /kɛskə/, *qu'il* /kil/ |
| *si* + *il* | *s'il* | *s'il vous plaît* /sil vu plɛ/ |

## 6.9 Mute H vs Aspirate H

All French h's are **phonetically silent** — you never produce an /h/ sound. However, h words behave in two different ways grammatically:

| Type | Behaviour | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **H muet** (mute h) | Treated as vowel-initial → triggers elision & liaison | *l'homme*, *les hommes* /le‿zɔm/, *l'heure*, *l'habitude* |
| **H aspiré** (aspirate h) | Treated as consonant-initial → **blocks** elision & liaison | *le haricot* (NOT l'haricot), *les haricots* /le aʁiko/ (no liaison) |

**Must be learned word by word:**

| H muet (triggers liaison/elision) | H aspiré (blocks liaison/elision) |
|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| *homme, heure, hiver, habiter, hôtel, honnête, humain, herbe* | *haricot, hibou, honte, haut, héros, hasard, hibou, hockey* |

> ⚠️ **Test:** If you can say *l'___* or *les ___* with a /z/ liaison → it's h muet. If you must say *le/la ___* with no elision → it's h aspiré.

## 6.10 Other Key Pronunciation Rules

### C and G Before Vowels

| Letter | Before | Sound | Examples |
|--------|--------|-------|---------|
| **c** | e, i, y | /s/ | *certain, cinq, cycle, cerise* |
| **c** | a, o, u | /k/ | *carte, côte, culture, comme* |
| **ç** | a, o, u | /s/ (forced) | *ça, leçon, reçu, garçon* |
| **g** | e, i, y | /ʒ/ | *geste, girafe, gym, âge* |
| **g** | a, o, u | /ɡ/ | *garçon, golf, guide, grand* |
| **gu** | e, i | /ɡ/ (u silent) | *guerre, guide, bague, guitare* |

### Double Consonants

In French, double written consonants are pronounced as a **single consonant** (unlike Italian):
- *belle* /bɛl/, *homme* /ɔm/, *classique* /klasik/, *appel* /apɛl/
- The main effect of a double consonant is that it **denasalises** the preceding vowel (see [[05. French Vowels]])

### -tion Pronunciation

| Language | -tion | IPA | Example |
|----------|-------|-----|---------|
| **French** | -tion = /sjɔ̃/ | /nasjɔ̃/ | *nation* |
| **English** | -tion = /ʃən/ | /ˈneɪʃən/ | *nation* |

> ⚠️ **Common error:** English speakers apply the English /ʃən/ to French words. Always use /sjɔ̃/ in French: *condition* /kɔ̃disjɔ̃/, *information* /ɛ̃fɔʁmasjɔ̃/.

### Aspiration — French plosives are NEVER aspirated

Unlike English /p t k/ which are aspirated at the start of stressed syllables (a puff of air), French /p t k/ are always **unaspirated**:
- English *pin* = [pʰɪn] (with air burst)
- French *pain* = [pɛ̃] (no air burst)

This gives French its characteristic "crisp" sound on stops.

### French T and D are Dental

French /t/ and /d/ are produced with the tongue touching the **upper teeth** (dental), not the alveolar ridge (like English /t/ and /d/). This is why French speech sounds slightly different even on familiar sounds.

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Liaison** | The pronunciation of a normally silent final consonant when the next word begins with a vowel or mute h |
| **Élision** | The dropping of a final vowel (replaced by apostrophe) before a vowel-initial word |
| **H muet** | A French word starting with h that behaves as vowel-initial — triggers liaison and elision |
| **H aspiré** | A French word starting with h that behaves as consonant-initial — blocks liaison and elision |
| **CaReFuL** | Mnemonic for French consonants often pronounced at word ends: C, R, F, L |
| **Uvular /ʁ/** | The French R produced at the back of the throat near the uvula — not rolled or alveolar |
| **Palatal nasal /ɲ/** | The *gn* sound in French — like *ny* in *canyon*; produced with the tongue body at the hard palate |
| **Labiopalatal /ɥ/** | The French sound in *nuit, lui* — like /w/ but with /y/ lip rounding; no English equivalent |
| **Denasalisation** | When a nasal vowel becomes oral — caused by doubled consonants (*bon* → *bonne*) |
| **Aspiration** | A puff of air following voiceless plosives — present in English, **absent** in French |
| **Dental consonant** | A consonant produced with the tongue touching the upper teeth — French /t/ and /d/ are dental |
