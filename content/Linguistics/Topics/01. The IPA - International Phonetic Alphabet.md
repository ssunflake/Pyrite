## 1.1 What the IPA Is and Why It Exists

The **International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)** is a standardised system of symbols where **one symbol = one sound**, regardless of language or spelling. It was created by the International Phonetic Association in 1888 to solve a fundamental problem: spelling is an unreliable guide to pronunciation.

Examples of why spelling fails:
- *though*, *through*, *tough*, *cough*, *bough*, *hiccough* — all contain **-ough** but have six different pronunciations
- *fish* could theoretically be spelled *ghoti*: **gh** as in *tough*, **o** as in *women*, **ti** as in *nation*

The IPA allows linguists, language learners, and dictionaries to represent pronunciation precisely and consistently.

## 1.2 Notation Conventions

| Notation | Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **/ /** | **Phonemic transcription** — broad, showing phonemes (contrastive sounds) | /kæt/ |
| **[ ]** | **Phonetic transcription** — narrow, showing actual pronunciation details | [kʰæt] |

> [!note] For language learning, **phonemic /.../ transcription** is usually sufficient. Phonetic [...]  notation captures fine details like aspiration or exact vowel length.

**Stress markers:**
- ˈ (before syllable) = primary stress: *button* /ˈbʌt.ən/
- ˌ (before syllable) = secondary stress: *organisation* /ˌɔː.ɡən.aɪˈzeɪ.ʃən/
- . between syllables = syllable boundary

## 1.3 The Vowel Quadrilateral

The IPA vowel chart is laid out as a **quadrilateral** representing the mouth:

| | **Front** | **Central** | **Back** |
|--|--|--|--|
| **Close** (high) | /i/ · /y/ | | /u/ |
| **Close-mid** | /e/ · /ø/ | /ə/ | /o/ |
| **Open-mid** | /ɛ/ · /œ/ | /ɜ/ | /ɔ/ |
| **Open** (low) | /a/ | | /ɑ/ |

Reading the chart:
- **Horizontal axis** = tongue position front → back
- **Vertical axis** = tongue height (close = tongue high; open = tongue low)
- **Left of pair** = unrounded lips; **right of pair** = rounded lips

> 💡 **Tip:** Think of the quadrilateral as a cross-section of the mouth viewed from the side. The corners represent the extreme vowel positions.

## 1.4 Place of Articulation

The **place of articulation** is where in the vocal tract the airflow is obstructed or shaped.

| Place | Articulators Involved | Example Sound | Example Word |
|-------|-----------------------|---------------|--------------|
| **Bilabial** | Both lips | /p/, /b/, /m/ | *pin*, *bin*, *man* |
| **Labiodental** | Lower lip + upper teeth | /f/, /v/ | *fat*, *van* |
| **Dental** | Tongue tip + upper teeth | /θ/, /ð/ | *think*, *this* |
| **Alveolar** | Tongue tip + alveolar ridge (behind upper teeth) | /t/, /d/, /s/, /z/, /n/, /l/ | *top*, *sit* |
| **Post-alveolar** | Tongue blade just behind alveolar ridge | /ʃ/, /ʒ/, /tʃ/, /dʒ/, /r/ | *ship*, *measure* |
| **Palatal** | Tongue body + hard palate | /j/ | *yes* |
| **Velar** | Tongue back + soft palate (velum) | /k/, /g/, /ŋ/ | *cat*, *sing* |
| **Glottal** | Vocal folds | /h/ | *hat* |

> [!note] French adds the **uvular** place (tongue back + uvula) for /ʁ/ — the distinctive French R.

---

## 1.5 Manner of Articulation

The **manner of articulation** describes how the airflow is shaped or obstructed.

| Manner | Description | Example Sounds | Example Words |
|--------|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Plosive (Stop)** | Airflow completely blocked, then released with a burst | /p b t d k g/ | *pin, bin, cat, dog* |
| **Fricative** | Airflow partially blocked, creating turbulent friction | /f v θ ð s z ʃ ʒ h/ | *fat, van, thin, ship* |
| **Affricate** | Plosive + fricative combined in one sound | /tʃ dʒ/ | *chip, jam* |
| **Nasal** | Air flows through the nose; mouth is blocked | /m n ŋ/ | *map, nap, sing* |
| **Lateral** | Air flows around the sides of the tongue | /l/ | *leg* |
| **Approximant** | Articulators approach but don't create friction | /r j w/ | *red, yes, wet* |

## 1.6 Voiced vs Voiceless

**Voicing** refers to whether the **vocal folds (cords) vibrate** during a sound.

- **Voiced**: vocal folds vibrate → /b d g v ð z ʒ dʒ m n ŋ l r j w/
- **Voiceless**: vocal folds held apart, no vibration → /p t k f θ s ʃ tʃ h/

**Test:** Place your hand gently on your throat. Say **sss** then **zzz** — you should feel vibration for /z/ but not for /s/.

### Voiced / Voiceless Pairs in English

Many English consonants form **minimal pairs** differing only in voicing:

| Voiceless | Voiced | Minimal Pair |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| /p/ | /b/ | *pin* / *bin* |
| /t/ | /d/ | *ten* / *den* |
| /k/ | /g/ | *cap* / *gap* |
| /f/ | /v/ | *fan* / *van* |
| /θ/ | /ð/ | *think* / *this* |
| /s/ | /z/ | *sip* / *zip* |
| /ʃ/ | /ʒ/ | *shoe* / *measure* (initial vs medial) |
| /tʃ/ | /dʒ/ | *chin* / *gin* |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Phoneme** | The smallest unit of sound that distinguishes meaning in a language (e.g. /p/ vs /b/ in *pin*/*bin*) |
| **Allophone** | A variant pronunciation of a phoneme that does not change meaning (e.g. aspirated [pʰ] and unaspirated [p] are allophones of /p/ in English) |
| **Minimal pair** | Two words that differ in exactly one phoneme: *cat* /kæt/ vs *bat* /bæt/ |
| **Transcription (phonemic)** | Representation of speech sounds using IPA within / / |
| **Transcription (phonetic)** | Detailed representation using IPA within [ ], capturing fine phonetic detail |
| **Place of articulation** | Location in the vocal tract where airflow is shaped or blocked |
| **Manner of articulation** | How the airflow is shaped to produce a sound |
| **Voicing** | Whether the vocal folds vibrate during a sound |
| **Vowel quadrilateral** | A diagram representing vowel sounds by tongue height (vertical) and tongue backness (horizontal) |
| **IPA** | International Phonetic Alphabet — one symbol per sound, universal across all languages |
