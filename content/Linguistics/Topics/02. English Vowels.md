## 2.1 Overview

English has approximately **20 vowel sounds** — more than most European languages. They divide into:
- **Monophthongs** — single, steady vowel sounds (short and long)
- **Diphthongs** — vowels that glide from one position to another

> [!note] Vowel sounds vary between accents. These notes use **RP (Received Pronunciation)** as the standard British English reference, with key **General American (GenAm)** differences noted.

---

## 2.2 Short Monophthongs

| IPA | Keyword | Example Words | Mouth Position |
|-----|---------|---------------|----------------|
| /ɪ/ | KIT | *bit, sit, gym, women, pretty* | High front, lax; lips slightly spread |
| /e/ | DRESS | *bed, head, said, friend, any* | Mid front, lax; mouth half open |
| /æ/ | TRAP | *cat, hand, man, laugh* (some dialects) | Low front; wide open mouth, tongue pushed forward |
| /ʌ/ | STRUT | *cup, blood, does, love, enough* | Mid-low central; relaxed, neutral mouth |
| /ɒ/ | LOT | *hot, watch, was, want, dog* | Low back, lightly rounded **(RP only; see 2.7)** |
| /ʊ/ | FOOT | *put, book, could, wolf, woman* | High back, lax; loosely rounded lips |

> 💡 **Tip:** Short vowels are "lax" — the tongue and lip muscles are relaxed. Long vowels are "tense" — muscles are more active.

---

## 2.3 Long Monophthongs

| IPA | Keyword | Example Words | Mouth Position |
|-----|---------|---------------|----------------|
| /iː/ | FLEECE | *see, sea, machine, field, pizza* | High front, tense; lips spread |
| /ɑː/ | PALM/START | *car, far, father, bath* (RP), *spa* | Low back, unrounded; jaw fully open |
| /ɔː/ | THOUGHT | *saw, caught, door, four, war, walk* | Mid-high back, rounded |
| /uː/ | GOOSE | *too, blue, through, shoe, move* | High back, tense; lips strongly rounded |
| /ɜː/ | NURSE | *bird, word, heard, fur, earth, learn* | Mid central; lips neutral (RP non-rhotic) |

> [!note] The length mark **ː** indicates a long vowel. However, length and quality are *both* different — /ɪ/ and /iː/ are not just short/long versions of the same sound; they are produced with the tongue in different positions.

## 2.4 The Schwa /ə/

The schwa is the **most common vowel sound in English** — it only ever appears in **unstressed syllables**.

When a syllable loses stress, its vowel typically reduces to /ə/ (a neutral, relaxed, mid-central sound).

| Word | Full form | With schwa |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| *about* | /æbaʊt/ | /əˈbaʊt/ |
| *banana* | — | /bəˈnɑːnə/ |
| *the* (before consonant) | /ðiː/ | /ðə/ |
| *a / an* | /eɪ/ | /ə/, /ən/ |
| *police* | — | /pəˈliːs/ |
| *family* | — | /ˈfæm.ɪ.li/ (the last i often → /ə/) |

Schwa can be spelled with almost any vowel letter:

| Spelling | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **a** | *about* /əˈbaʊt/ |
| **e** | *the* /ðə/, *listen* /ˈlɪs.ən/ |
| **o** | *police* /pəˈliːs/ |
| **i** | *family* /ˈfæm.ə.li/ |
| **u** | *supply* /səˈplaɪ/ |
| **ou** | *glamour* /ˈɡlæm.ə/ |

## 2.5 Diphthongs

A **diphthong** is a vowel sound that glides from one position to another within a single syllable.

| IPA | Keyword | Example Words | Glide Movement |
|-----|---------|---------------|----------------|
| /eɪ/ | FACE | *day, rain, break, they, eight, great* | from /e/ → /ɪ/ (mid front → high front) |
| /aɪ/ | PRICE | *my, night, buy, tied, eye, height* | from /a/ → /ɪ/ (low → high front) |
| /ɔɪ/ | CHOICE | *boy, coin, noise, toy, avoid* | from /ɔ/ → /ɪ/ (back rounded → high front) |
| /əʊ/ | GOAT | *go, stone, know, though, soul* | from /ə/ → /ʊ/ (central → high back) **(RP)** |
| /aʊ/ | MOUTH | *now, south, crowd, house, how* | from /a/ → /ʊ/ (low → high back) |
| /ɪə/ | NEAR | *here, ear, idea, beer, weird* | from /ɪ/ → /ə/ (high front → central schwa) |
| /eə/ | SQUARE | *there, fair, care, wear, they're* | from /e/ → /ə/ (mid front → schwa) |
| /ʊə/ | CURE | *tour, pure, sure, poor* | from /ʊ/ → /ə/ (high back → schwa) **(becoming rare — merging with /ɔː/)** |

> 💡 **Tip:** The last three (/ɪə/, /eə/, /ʊə/) are called **centring diphthongs** because they glide towards the central schwa. In General American, these become rhotic vowels (/ɪr/, /ɛr/, /ʊr/).

## 2.6 Spelling → Sound Patterns

English spelling is inconsistent, but some patterns are reliable:

| Spelling pattern | Sound | Examples |
|-----------------|-------|---------|
| **-igh-** | /aɪ/ | *night, light, right, sigh* |
| **-oo-** | /uː/ or /ʊ/ | *moon* /uː/, *book* /ʊ/ |
| **-ou-** | /aʊ/, /ʌ/, /uː/, /ɒ/ | *house, enough, through, cough* |
| **-ea-** | /iː/ or /e/ | *sea* /iː/, *head* /e/ |
| **-ow-** | /aʊ/ or /əʊ/ | *now* /aʊ/, *know* /əʊ/ |
| **-ai- / -ay-** | /eɪ/ | *rain, say, they* |
| **Magic-E rule** | lengthens vowel | *cap* /æ/ → *cape* /eɪ/; *bit* /ɪ/ → *bite* /aɪ/; *hop* /ɒ/ → *hope* /əʊ/ |
| **-tion / -sion** | /ʃən/ | *nation, session* |
| **-ture** | /tʃə/ | *nature, picture* |

**Why is English spelling so irregular?**
- The **Great Vowel Shift** (1400–1700) changed pronunciation dramatically *after* spelling conventions were fixed by the printing press
- Words borrowed from **French, Latin, Greek, Norse** each brought their own spelling conventions
- Regional variation meant different areas spelled differently before standardisation

## 2.7 RP vs General American — Key Differences

| Feature | RP (British) | GenAm (American) | Example |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Rhoticity** | Non-rhotic: /r/ only before vowels; *car* = /kɑː/ | Rhotic: /r/ pronounced everywhere; *car* = /kɑːr/ | *car, bird, here* |
| **TRAP-BATH split** | *bath, dance, path* = /ɑː/ | *bath, dance, path* = /æ/ | *bath* /bɑːθ/ vs /bæθ/ |
| **LOT vowel** | /ɒ/ — rounded back vowel | /ɑ/ — unrounded; LOT-PALM merged | *hot* /hɒt/ vs /hɑt/ |
| **GOAT** | /əʊ/ | /oʊ/ | *go, stone* |
| **NURSE** | /ɜː/ | /ɝː/ (rhotic) | *bird, word* |
| **Flapping** | /t/ between vowels = /t/ | /t/ between vowels = /ɾ/ (tap) | *butter* /ˈbʌtə/ vs /ˈbʌɾər/ |
| **Yod** | /njuː/ (new), /tjuːn/ (tune) | /nuː/, /tuːn/ — yod dropped after alveolars | *new, Tuesday* |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Monophthong** | A vowel with a single, steady quality — the tongue does not move during the sound |
| **Diphthong** | A vowel sound that glides from one tongue position to another within a single syllable |
| **Schwa /ə/** | The unstressed, neutral mid-central vowel — the most common vowel in English |
| **RP (Received Pronunciation)** | The traditional prestige accent of British English, used as a reference standard |
| **General American (GenAm)** | The standard accent of North American English, the reference for American pronunciation |
| **Rhotic** | An accent where /r/ is pronounced after vowels (e.g. GenAm) |
| **Non-rhotic** | An accent where /r/ is silent after vowels unless followed by another vowel (e.g. RP) |
| **Great Vowel Shift** | A major change in English vowel pronunciation (c. 1400–1700) that created many spelling-sound mismatches |
| **Tense vowel** | A vowel produced with greater muscular tension; generally longer (/iː, uː, eɪ…/) |
| **Lax vowel** | A vowel produced with relaxed muscles; generally shorter (/ɪ, ʊ, e…/) |
| **Magic-E rule** | In English spelling, a final silent *e* signals that the preceding vowel is long: *cap* → *cape* |
| **Centring diphthong** | A diphthong that ends in schwa /ə/: /ɪə/, /eə/, /ʊə/ |
