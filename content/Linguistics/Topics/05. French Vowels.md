## 5.1 Overview

French has approximately **16 vowel sounds**. Compared to English:
- **Fewer** vowel sounds overall, but includes sounds **English lacks entirely**
- **No diphthongs** — French vowels are **pure monophthongs** (the tongue stays still throughout)
- **Three front rounded vowels**: /y/, /ø/, /œ/ — no English equivalent
- **Four nasal vowels**: /ɑ̃/, /ɛ̃/, /ɔ̃/, /œ̃/

> [!note] French vowels are "pure" — unlike English diphthongs (/eɪ/, /əʊ/, etc.), French vowels do not glide. *été* is /e-te/, not /eɪ-teɪ/.

## 5.2 Oral Vowels — Full Reference Table

| IPA | Spelling(s) | Example Words | Description & Mouth Position |
|-----|-------------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| /a/ | a, à | *chat, patte, là, ami* | Open front, unrounded; like English *cat* but more front |
| /ɑ/ | â, some -as | *pâte, bas, gaz* | Open back, unrounded; **merging with /a/** in modern French — mostly historical |
| /e/ | é, -er, -ez, -es (monosyllables) | *été, parler, chez, les* | Close-mid front, unrounded; **pure — no glide**. Like the *e* in *hey* without the final /ɪ/ |
| /ɛ/ | è, ê, -ai-, -ei-, e (closed syllable) | *père, fête, fait, belle, avec* | Open-mid front, unrounded; like English *bed* |
| /i/ | i, î, y | *vie, île, stylo, bicyclette* | Close front, unrounded; **pure** — like English /iː/ but without any glide |
| /o/ | ô, -au-, -eau-, o (open syllable/final) | *côte, eau, beau, mot, dos* | Close-mid back, rounded; **pure** — like English *go* but without the final /ʊ/ |
| /ɔ/ | o (closed syllable, before /r/) | *port, porte, sort, or, sol* | Open-mid back, rounded; like English *thought* but shorter |
| /u/ | ou, où, oû | *vous, tout, pour, où, coup, goût* | Close back, rounded; like English /uː/ but **more tense and rounded** |
| /y/ | u, û | *tu, pur, flûte, sur, vue, salut* | Close front, **rounded** — **NO English equivalent** |
| /ø/ | eu, œu (open syllable) | *peu, deux, feu, bleu, nœud* | Close-mid front, **rounded** — **NO English equivalent** |
| /œ/ | eu, œ (closed syllable) | *peur, heure, sœur, seul, œil, jeune* | Open-mid front, **rounded** — **NO English equivalent** |
| /ə/ | e (unstressed, unaccented) | *le, de, ce, que, refaire, venir* | Mid central, unrounded; **schwa** — often silent in rapid speech |

## 5.3 How to Pronounce the Front Rounded Vowels

These three vowels are the most challenging for English speakers because English has no rounded front vowels.

### /y/ — the French *u*
1. Position your tongue as if about to say English /iː/ (*see*) — tongue high and forward
2. Now round your lips tightly as if saying /uː/ (*you*)
3. Hold both positions simultaneously → you are producing /y/

Practice words: *tu* /ty/, *vu* /vy/, *pur* /pyʁ/, *rue* /ʁy/, *sur* /syʁ/

### /ø/ — *eu* in an open syllable
1. Position your tongue as if saying /e/ (*le* in English)
2. Round your lips as if saying /o/
3. Hold both → you are producing /ø/

Practice words: *peu* /pø/, *deux* /dø/, *feu* /fø/, *bleu* /blø/

### /œ/ — *eu* in a closed syllable
Like /ø/ but with a more relaxed, open jaw:
1. Start as for /ø/ but drop the jaw slightly more
2. Lips still rounded, tongue slightly lower

Practice words: *peur* /pœʁ/, *heure* /œʁ/, *sœur* /sœʁ/, *seul* /sœl/

> 💡 **Tip:** The key distinction between /ø/ and /œ/ is **syllable type**: open syllable → /ø/ (*peu, feu*); closed syllable → /œ/ (*peur, heure*). A closed syllable ends in a consonant sound.

## 5.4 Nasal Vowels

French nasal vowels are produced by allowing air to pass through **both the mouth and the nose** simultaneously. They occur when a vowel is followed by n or m, and that n or m is NOT followed by another vowel.

| IPA | Spellings | Example Words | Notes |
|-----|-----------|---------------|-------|
| /ɑ̃/ | an, am, en, em | *dans, enfant, temps, chambre, vent, plan* | Open back nasal; the most common nasal vowel |
| /ɛ̃/ | in, im, ain, aim, ein, yn, ym | *vin, pain, main, plein, syntaxe, lymphe* | Open-mid front nasal |
| /ɔ̃/ | on, om | *bon, pont, ombre, son, maison* | Close-mid back nasal |
| /œ̃/ | un, um | *un, brun, parfum* | **Merging with /ɛ̃/** in most modern French — only conservative speakers distinguish them |

> ⚠️ **Critical rule for nasalisation:** A vowel is **nasalised** when it is followed by n or m AND the n/m is **not** followed by another vowel or another n/m.

| Word | Pronunciation | Reason |
|------|---------------|--------|
| *bon* | /bɔ̃/ | o + n (final) → nasal |
| *bonne* | /bɔn/ | o + nn → oral! Double n denasalises |
| *vin* | /vɛ̃/ | i + n (final) → nasal |
| *vigne* | /viɲ/ | i + gn → oral |
| *an* | /ɑ̃/ | a + n (final) → nasal |
| *année* | /ane/ | a + nn → oral; double n breaks nasalisation |
| *en* | /ɑ̃/ | nasal |
| *ennui* | /ɑ̃nɥi/ | the first n is the nasal, the double nn makes the vowel oral |

## 5.5 Accent Marks and Vowel Sounds

| Accent | Name | Effect on Sound | Examples |
|--------|------|-----------------|---------|
| **é** | accent aigu | Always /e/ — close-mid | *été, café, bébé, décider* |
| **è** | accent grave on e | Always /ɛ/ — open-mid | *père, mère, après, très* |
| **ê** | accent circonflexe on e | /ɛ/ — historically marked a lost 's' | *fête, bête, même, tête* |
| **â** | accent circonflexe on a | Historically /ɑ/ (now often /a/ for many speakers) | *pâte, château, grâce* |
| **î, û, ô** | accent circonflexe on i, u, o | **No consistent sound change** — historical spelling marker | *île, flûte, côte* |
| **à, ù** | accent grave on a, u | **No sound change** — purely grammatical distinction | *à* (to) vs *a* (has); *où* (where) vs *ou* (or) |
| **ç** | cédille | c → /s/ before a, o, u | *ça, leçon, reçu, garçon, façon* |
| **ë, ï, ü** | tréma (diaeresis) | The two vowels are pronounced **separately** | *Noël /nɔ.ɛl/*, *naïf /na.if/*, *Noëlle* |

> ⚠️ **Most important accents to remember:** **é** is always /e/, and **è/ê** are always /ɛ/. These are the most frequently tested distinctions.

## 5.6 E Muet — The Silent E

The letter **e** without an accent mark represents either **/ə/** (schwa) or is entirely silent. This is called *e muet* (silent e) or *e caduc* (unstable e).

| Position | Rule | Examples |
|----------|------|---------|
| **End of a word** | Almost always silent in natural speech | *une* /yn/, *table* /tabl/, *belle* /bɛl/, *comme* /kɔm/ |
| **End of a syllable within a word** | Often dropped in fast speech | *samedi* /samdi/ (fast) vs /samədi/ (careful) |
| **After multiple consonants** | May be kept to avoid impossible consonant clusters | *vendredi* — middle e retained to prevent /vndrdi/ |
| **Formal or slow speech** | E muet retained more | Poetry, song, formal oratory often preserve e muet for rhythm |

**The "Three-Consonant Rule" (loi des trois consonnes):**
An e muet is kept (pronounced as /ə/) if dropping it would create an unpronounceable cluster of **three or more consonants**.

> 💡 **Tip:** In classical French poetry, e muet counts as a syllable (even though it's silent in speech). This is why verse scansion counts syllables differently from spoken French.

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Monophthong** | A single, pure vowel sound — the tongue position is stable. All French oral vowels are monophthongs |
| **Diphthong** | A gliding vowel sound — French has essentially none (unlike English) |
| **Nasal vowel** | A vowel produced with air flowing through both the mouth and nose simultaneously |
| **Front rounded vowel** | A vowel where the tongue is positioned forward (as for /i/) but the lips are rounded — French /y/, /ø/, /œ/ have no English equivalent |
| **E muet / e caduc** | The unstressed, unaccented *e* which is often silent in French speech |
| **Denasalisation** | When a nasal vowel becomes oral — triggered by doubled consonants: *bon* /bɔ̃/ → *bonne* /bɔn/ |
| **Accent aigu (é)** | The acute accent — always signals the /e/ sound |
| **Accent grave (è)** | The grave accent on *e* — always signals the /ɛ/ sound |
| **Accent circonflexe (ê, â, ô, î, û)** | The circumflex — on *e* gives /ɛ/; on other vowels is largely historical |
| **Tréma (ë, ï)** | Diaeresis — signals that two adjacent vowels are pronounced separately |
| **Cédille (ç)** | Cedilla — forces *c* to be pronounced /s/ before *a*, *o*, *u* |
