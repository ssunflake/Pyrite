## 3.1 What Is Summary Writing?

Summary writing tests your ability to **read a text carefully, identify its most relevant points, and express them concisely in your own words**.

Unlike comprehension or language analysis, summary writing is not concerned with how the writer uses language — it is entirely about **content**: what information is being communicated, and whether you can select and restate it accurately.

Key principles of a good summary:
- **Concise** — shorter than the original, every sentence earns its place
- **Accurate** — meaning must not be distorted or changed
- **Selective** — only points relevant to the specific question are included
- **In your own words** — lifting text directly scores zero for quality marks

> 💡 **Exam tip:** The question will specify WHAT you are summarising (e.g., "Summarise what the writer says about the disadvantages of social media"). Read this focus carefully and stick to it throughout — do not include points from parts of the text the question does not ask about.

---

## 3.2 Step-by-Step Summary Process

Follow these five steps for every summary task:

| Step | Action | Why It Matters |
|------|--------|---------------|
| **1. Read** | Read the whole source text carefully at least once | Understand the overall argument before selecting specific points |
| **2. Annotate** | Re-read and underline/highlight only points directly relevant to the question | Prevents you from including irrelevant material |
| **3. Select** | Choose the best 6–10 distinct, non-overlapping points | More points = more content marks; but they must all be different |
| **4. Rephrase** | Translate each point into your own words using synonyms and restructured sentences | Quality marks require your own words — no verbatim copying |
| **5. Link** | Write your summary as connected prose with a clear opening sentence and linking phrases | A cohesive paragraph reads better and signals fluency |

## 3.3 Identifying Key Points vs Supporting Detail

This is the most important and most frequently tested skill in summary writing.

| Type | Description | Include in Summary? |
|------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Key point** | A main idea that directly answers the summary question | YES — always include |
| **Supporting detail** | An example, statistic, or explanation that elaborates on a key point | Usually NO — it is secondary to the main point |
| **Irrelevant material** | Information from the text that does not relate to the question's specific focus | NEVER — it gains no marks and wastes words |

### Worked Example of Selection:

**Source text excerpt:**
*"Teenagers today spend an average of six hours daily on screens. This excessive use leads to sleep deprivation, as the blue light emitted by devices suppresses melatonin production. Furthermore, the constant notifications disrupt concentration, making it difficult to focus on schoolwork. Social comparison on platforms such as Instagram has also been linked to lower self-esteem in young people."*

**Summary question:** *Summarise the negative effects of screen use on teenagers.*

| Point from Text | Key Point or Detail? | Include? |
|----------------|---------------------|---------|
| Teenagers spend 6 hours daily on screens | Context/cause — not an effect | No |
| Screen use leads to sleep deprivation | **KEY POINT** — a negative effect | Yes |
| Blue light suppresses melatonin | Supporting detail (explains sleep deprivation) | Only if needed for clarity |
| Notifications disrupt concentration | **KEY POINT** — a negative effect | Yes |
| Social comparison linked to lower self-esteem | **KEY POINT** — a negative effect | Yes |

## 3.4 How to Rephrase Without Losing Meaning

You must not copy phrases directly from the source text. Quality marks depend on the extent to which your language is your own.

### Rephrasing Techniques:

| Technique | Original (Source) | Rephrased (Your Words) |
|-----------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Synonym substitution** | "excessive use" | "overuse" / "too much time spent" |
| **Sentence restructuring** | "The noise made sleeping difficult." | "It became hard to sleep because of the constant sound." |
| **Changing word class** | "She spoke angrily." | "Her speech was full of anger." |
| **Combining two short points** | Two separate short sentences | One combined summary sentence |
| **Using a broader / general term** | "dogs, cats, and rabbits" | "pets" |
| **Active to passive (or vice versa)** | "Screens suppress melatonin." | "Melatonin production is disrupted by screen use." |

> [!Note] Rephrasing must NOT change the meaning of the original point. Take care with synonyms — a word that looks similar may carry different connotations in context. When in doubt, restructure the sentence rather than just swapping words.

## 3.5 Writing a Clear Opening Sentence

Your opening sentence should:
- Directly address the focus of the question
- Introduce the subject of your summary
- Set the framework for what follows
- Be written entirely in your own words

| Poor Opening | Strong Opening |
|-------------|---------------|
| "In this text, the writer talks about many things." | "The text identifies several significant risks that excessive screen use poses to the health and wellbeing of teenagers." |
| "This is a summary about screens." | "Young people who spend too much time on screens face a range of serious consequences, including physical, academic, and emotional harms." |

---

## 3.6 Linking Phrases for Smooth Summaries

A good summary is not a list — it is connected prose. Using varied linking phrases makes your summary read fluently.

| Function | Phrases to Use |
|----------|---------------|
| **Adding a new point** | Furthermore, In addition, Moreover, Another significant concern is that, The text also notes that |
| **Contrasting** | However, On the other hand |
| **Sequencing** | Firstly, Secondly, Finally |
| **Emphasising a point** | Notably, Of particular concern is the fact that |
| **Summarising overall** | Overall, In summary, The text highlights that |

## 3.7 What to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Loses Marks | How to Fix It |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| **Copying sentences verbatim** | Earns zero quality marks, regardless of content accuracy | Rephrase everything — change sentence structure and vocabulary |
| **Including your own opinion** | Summaries are neutral; no personal commentary | Remove phrases like "I think" or "In my opinion" |
| **Including irrelevant points** | Goes against the specific focus of the question | Constantly check: does this point answer the question? |
| **Writing too much** | Padding is inefficient; every sentence must add a NEW point | If a sentence doesn't contain a new point, delete it |
| **Writing too little** | Fewer distinct points = fewer content marks | Aim for 6–10 distinct points from across the text |
| **Analysing language** | Summary is about content — not how the writer says things | Describe the idea, not the technique used to express it |
| **Repeating the same point** | Two versions of the same idea earn only one content mark | Check: is this a genuinely new point, or am I saying the same thing again? |

## 3.8 Target Length and Mark Scheme

### Target Length
- Typically **100–200 words** (always check the question for the specific target)
- This is roughly one third of the length of the source text
- If the question says "about 100 words," aim for 90–115 words

### How Marks Are Awarded

Cambridge IGCSE Summary questions are marked under two categories:

| Mark Category | What Is Assessed | How to Maximise Marks |
|--------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Content marks** | Whether you have found the correct relevant key points | Select distinct points that directly answer the question; aim for 6–10 |
| **Quality marks** | Whether you have used your own words and written fluently | Rephrase everything; write in connected prose, not a bullet list |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Both mark categories carry equal weight. Even if you identify every correct point, you will lose half the marks if you copy them directly. Rephrasing is not optional.

## 3.9 Common Mistakes Table

| Mistake | Example of the Mistake | Corrected Version |
|---------|----------------------|------------------|
| **Verbatim copying** | "the constant notifications disrupt concentration" | "regular phone alerts make it difficult for students to concentrate" |
| **Same point twice** | Point 1: "screens cause sleep problems" / Point 2: "devices interfere with sleep" | Only include each distinct idea once |
| **Vague rephrasing** | "Things are bad for young people" | "Teenagers suffer several harmful effects, including poor sleep and reduced focus" |
| **Adding opinion** | "The writer is right that screens are very dangerous" | Simply state the point without personal evaluation |
| **Missing the question focus** | Summarising the whole text when question asks only about health effects | Re-read the question; only include health-related points |
| **Too short** | Only 3 points in 60 words | Re-read the text; more points are available — find them |

## 3.10 Worked Mini-Example

### Source Text (Excerpt):
*"Air pollution in urban areas has reached alarming levels in recent decades. The primary contributor is vehicle emissions, which release nitrogen dioxide and harmful particulate matter into the air. These pollutants cause serious respiratory problems, including asthma and chronic lung disease. Children are particularly vulnerable, as their lungs are still developing and therefore less resistant to damage. Additionally, prolonged exposure to polluted air has been linked to heart disease in adults over the age of 40. Mental health impacts are also emerging, with studies suggesting that high pollution levels correlate with increased rates of anxiety and depression."*

---

### Step 1 — Annotate Key Points (underline these):
1. Vehicle emissions are the main cause of urban air pollution *(cause — only include if question asks for causes)*
2. Pollutants cause respiratory problems: asthma, lung disease ✓
3. Children especially at risk as lungs still developing ✓
4. Links to heart disease in adults over 40 ✓
5. High pollution linked to anxiety and depression ✓

---

### Step 2 — Rephrase Each Point:
1. Cars and other vehicles are the biggest source of pollution *(if relevant to question)*
2. These particles damage the lungs, leading to breathing difficulties including asthma
3. Young people face greater danger as their lungs have not yet fully formed
4. Older adults face an increased risk of heart conditions
5. Polluted air has also been connected to mental health problems such as anxiety

---

### Model Summary (Question: Summarise the health effects of air pollution):

*Urban air pollution poses serious risks to human health. These harmful particles damage the lungs, causing breathing difficulties such as asthma and other chronic conditions. Young people are particularly at risk, as their lungs are still in the process of developing and are therefore more vulnerable to lasting damage. In adults over 40, exposure to polluted air has also been linked to heart disease. Furthermore, emerging research suggests that high levels of pollution correlate with increased rates of anxiety and depression, indicating a mental health impact in addition to physical harm.*

*(85 words — well within target; 4 distinct content points; fully rephrased)*

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Summary** | A condensed version of a text's key points, expressed in the reader's own words |
| **Key point** | A main idea directly relevant to the specific focus of the summary question |
| **Supporting detail** | Evidence, examples, or elaboration that supports a key point but is not itself a key point |
| **Paraphrase** | Expressing someone else's ideas in your own words without changing the meaning |
| **Verbatim** | Word for word; copying exactly from the source text |
| **Content marks** | Marks awarded for identifying the correct, relevant key points |
| **Quality marks** | Marks awarded for using own words and writing in fluent, connected prose |
| **Synthesis** | Combining information from different parts of a text into a single coherent summary |
| **Relevance** | How closely a point relates to the specific focus of the summary question |
| **Rephrasing** | Rewriting a point in different words while preserving the original meaning |
