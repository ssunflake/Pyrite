## 2.1 WHAT vs HOW vs WHY — The Three Levels of Analysis

The key to high-level language analysis is moving beyond simply explaining what a text says, to how and why the writer says it. Many students only reach the "what" level.

| Level | Question Asked | Example Answer |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| **WHAT (basic)** | What does the text say? | "The writer describes a dark forest." |
| **HOW (intermediate)** | How does the writer create this effect? | "The writer uses the metaphor 'teeth of oak' to describe the trees." |
| **WHY (high-level)** | Why does the writer make this choice — what is the purpose or effect? | "...creating a threatening, predatory atmosphere that builds the reader's unease and implies danger lurking within the natural world." |

> [!Note] Answering only "what" the writer says produces a retelling of content, not analysis. To access Bands 4 and 5 (top marks), you must consistently discuss HOW (technique) and WHY (purpose/effect on the reader).

## 2.2 Writer's Purposes

Writers choose language deliberately to achieve specific purposes. A single text may serve more than one purpose simultaneously.

| Purpose | Definition | Typical Techniques |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **To inform** | Give the reader knowledge or facts | Facts, statistics, clear structure, formal register, third person |
| **To persuade** | Change the reader's mind or move them to action | AFOREST techniques, direct address, rhetorical questions, emotive language |
| **To entertain** | Engage, delight, or amuse the reader | Vivid description, humour, narrative techniques, varied language |
| **To describe** | Create a vivid picture in the reader's mind | Sensory language, imagery, figurative language, varied sentence structure |
| **To argue** | Present a reasoned case for a viewpoint | Logical structure, evidence, counter-argument, formal register |
| **To advise** | Guide or direct the reader | Second person ("you"), imperative verbs, clear sequential structure |
| **To analyse** | Examine something in careful detail | Formal register, cause and effect, hedging language ("it could be argued") |

## 2.3 Sentence Types and Their Effects

### Simple Sentence
**Definition:** A single independent clause containing a subject and a verb.
**Effect:** Directness, clarity, impact, shock, finality.
**Example:** *"She ran."* / *"The building was empty."* / *"He was gone."*
> A well-placed simple sentence after a series of long, complex ones delivers maximum impact. The contrast in length creates sudden dramatic emphasis.

---

### Compound Sentence
**Definition:** Two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so).
**Effect:** Balance, progression, connection between equal ideas.
**Example:** *"The rain fell steadily, and the streets began to flood."*

---

### Complex Sentence
**Definition:** A main clause plus one or more subordinate clauses.
**Effect:** Nuance, cause-and-effect relationships, additional context, sophistication.
**Example:** *"Although she was terrified, she pressed on through the darkness, driven by something she could not name."*

---

### Compound-Complex Sentence
**Definition:** Combines two main clauses AND at least one subordinate clause.
**Effect:** Multi-layered, sophisticated; shows complex relationships between ideas.
**Example:** *"Although she had not slept for days, she refused to stop, and her determination became her only compass."*

---

### Minor / Fragment Sentence
**Definition:** An incomplete sentence — lacks a subject or main verb.
**Effect:** Dramatic emphasis, urgency, staccato effect, shock.
**Example:** *"Nothing. Not a sound. Not a single breath."*
> Fragment sentences placed at key moments (after tension builds) create powerful impact. They slow the reader down and force attention.

---

### Summary Table: Sentence Types and Effects

| Sentence Type | Structure | Effect Created |
|--------------|----------|---------------|
| Simple | One main clause | Directness, shock, clarity, finality |
| Compound | Two main clauses + conjunction | Balance, connection, progression |
| Complex | Main + subordinate clause | Nuance, cause-effect, additional information |
| Compound-complex | Two main + subordinate clause | Multi-layered, sophisticated meaning |
| Fragment / minor | Incomplete | Urgency, drama, staccato effect |

## 2.4 Sentence Length Effects

| Sentence Length | Effect on the Reader | When Writers Use It |
|----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Short (1–7 words)** | Tension, shock, urgency, directness, finality | Building to a climax; moments of revelation; after a long sentence for maximum contrast |
| **Long (20+ words)** | Detailed description, flowing movement, reflection, complexity | Calm descriptive passages; establishing setting; building an argument |
| **Varied** | Engagement; prevents monotony; creates rhythm | Throughout — varying length is a mark of skilled writing |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** When discussing sentence length, always explain the specific effect **in context**. "The short sentence creates tension" is a basic observation. "The short sentence 'She was alone.' placed after three long, complex descriptions of the crowd around her creates a sudden, suffocating sense of isolation" is an analytical response.

## 2.5 Word Choice: Connotation, Denotation, and Register

### Connotation vs Denotation

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Denotation** | The literal, dictionary meaning of a word | "Home" = a place where someone lives |
| **Connotation** | The emotional and cultural associations of a word | "Home" connotes warmth, safety, belonging, family, comfort |

Writers choose words for their **connotations**, not just their denotations. Analysing why a writer chose one word rather than a simpler alternative is a core exam skill.

**Example analysis:**
> The writer uses the verb "lurked" rather than "waited." While both words describe remaining in one place, "lurked" carries sinister, predatory connotations — implying a secretive, threatening intent. This word choice creates unease in the reader long before any explicit threat is revealed.

**Another example:**
> Describing a character's home as a "hovel" rather than a "cottage" carries connotations of poverty, squalor, and degradation — telling us immediately about social status and living conditions without stating them directly.

---

### Register and Word Choice

| Register | Vocabulary Style | Typical Effect |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Formal** | Sophisticated, precise, technical vocabulary | Authority, credibility, professional distance |
| **Informal** | Simple, colloquial, conversational | Warmth, accessibility, intimacy, friendliness |
| **Specific** | Precise nouns and verbs (the "right" word) | Creates a vivid, exact picture; shows knowledge |
| **Vague** | Non-specific, generic nouns and verbs | Creates mystery, uncertainty, or deliberate ambiguity |

## 2.6 Structural Devices

Structure is not just about the overall shape of a text — writers use structural devices at sentence and paragraph level to create specific effects.

| Device | Definition | Effect |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Repetition** | Repeating a word, phrase, or sentence structure | Emphasis; drills an idea into the reader; creates rhythm |
| **Listing (tricolon)** | Three items in a row (the "rule of three") | Rhythm, accumulation of detail, sense of completeness |
| **Anaphora** | Repeating the same word(s) at the start of successive clauses | Powerful, building emphasis; rhetorical momentum |
| **Parallelism** | Repeated grammatical structure across clauses | Balance, elegance, sophistication, rhetorical power |
| **Juxtaposition** | Placing contrasting ideas, characters, or images side by side | Highlights differences; creates tension, irony, or moral point |
| **Contrast** | Opposing ideas or descriptions within a text | Emphasises differences; adds depth and complexity |
| **Cyclical structure** | A text that ends with a reference to how it began | Resolution, irony, a sense that nothing has changed, or that everything has |
| **Volta** | A turning point or shift in tone, argument, or perspective | Surprise, development, or a revelation that reframes everything before it |

## 2.8 How to Write a Language Analysis Paragraph — MQEP

The most effective structure for language analysis combines four elements:

```
METHOD    →  Name and identify the specific technique used
QUOTATION →  Quote the exact relevant word(s) from the text
EFFECT    →  Explain the effect this creates on the reader
PURPOSE   →  Connect this to the writer's overall aim or theme
```

### Worked Example — Full MQEP Paragraph

**Question:** How does the writer use language to convey a sense of loss? [5]

> The writer employs an extended metaphor to convey the depth of the narrator's grief. The narrator describes memory as "a tide that drags everything you love out to sea," comparing the erosion of memory to the relentless and uncontrollable force of the ocean. The effect of this comparison is one of helplessness — the reader understands that the narrator feels utterly powerless to retain what she has lost, just as no human being can stop the tide. Furthermore, the active verb "drags" carries violent, forceful connotations, implying that loss is not a gentle fading but something wrenching and painful. This serves the writer's wider purpose of exploring the psychological devastation of grief, encouraging the reader to feel deep sympathy for the narrator's experience.

**Breakdown:**
- **M** → "employs an extended metaphor"
- **Q** → "a tide that drags everything you love out to sea"
- **E** → "The effect of this comparison is one of helplessness..."
- **P** → "serves the writer's wider purpose of exploring the psychological devastation of grief"

---

## 2.9 PEEL Structure for Analysis

For sustained, high-mark analysis responses:

```
POINT      →  Make your analytical claim (what is the writer doing here?)
EVIDENCE   →  Quote precisely from the text
EXPLAIN    →  Analyse the technique and its effect on the reader in detail
LINK       →  Connect to the question focus / writer's overall purpose / theme
```

## 2.10 Technique → Effect Reference Table (17 Techniques)

| Technique | Definition | Effect on the Reader |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Metaphor** | Direct comparison; one thing IS another | Creates bold, imaginative imagery; invites interpretation |
| **Simile** | Comparison using 'like' or 'as' | Makes abstract ideas concrete and relatable |
| **Extended metaphor** | A metaphor developed across a passage | Coherence and depth; explores an idea from multiple angles |
| **Personification** | Non-human thing given human qualities | Creates relationship between character and environment |
| **Alliteration** | Repetition of initial consonant sounds | Creates rhythm; draws attention to specific words; can be harsh or soft |
| **Onomatopoeia** | Word that imitates a sound | Immediacy; pulls the reader into the scene sensorially |
| **Hyperbole** | Deliberate exaggeration | Emphasis; humour; emotional intensity |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Environment reflecting emotion | Creates and foreshadows mood; makes the world feel responsive to character |
| **Juxtaposition** | Contrasting ideas placed together | Highlights difference; creates irony or moral tension |
| **Rhetorical question** | Question asked for effect, not answer | Engages reader; makes them consider the writer's viewpoint |
| **Anaphora** | Repeated words at clause beginnings | Drives home a point with cumulative rhetorical power |
| **Short sentence** | Brief, single statement | Shock, finality, or emphasis; creates maximum impact by contrast |
| **Emotive language** | Words designed to trigger emotion | Creates sympathy, outrage, anxiety, or excitement |
| **Listing** | Series of items or ideas | Overwhelms with detail; shows scale, variety, or accumulation |
| **Irony** | Saying the opposite of what is meant | Creates humour, distance, or sharp criticism |
| **Symbolism** | Object representing an idea | Adds layers of meaning; rewards attentive reading |
| **Oxymoron** | Contradictory terms combined | Captures paradox; creates intrigue; forces the reader to hold opposites together |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Language analysis** | The examination of how a writer uses specific words and techniques to create effects |
| **Technique** | A deliberate language or structural choice made by the writer |
| **Effect** | The impact a technique has on the reader — emotional, intellectual, or atmospheric |
| **Purpose** | The writer's reason for making a particular language choice |
| **Connotation** | The emotional or cultural associations of a word beyond its literal meaning |
| **Denotation** | The literal, dictionary meaning of a word |
| **Register** | The level of formality in language, selected to suit audience and purpose |
| **Tone** | The writer's attitude towards their subject, conveyed through language choices |
| **Imagery** | Language that appeals to the senses to create mental pictures |
| **MQEP** | Method–Quotation–Effect–Purpose: a paragraph framework for language analysis |
| **Structural device** | A technique that operates at the level of whole-text or paragraph organisation |
| **Volta** | A sudden turn or shift in tone, argument, or perspective within a text |
| **Metalanguage** | The vocabulary used to discuss and analyse language itself |
| **Tricolon** | A list of three items; the "rule of three" |
| **Anaphora** | Repetition of words at the beginning of successive clauses for emphasis |
