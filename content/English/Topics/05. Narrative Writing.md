## 5.1 Story Structure — Freytag's Pyramid

The classic five-stage narrative arc, known as Freytag's Pyramid, provides a reliable structure for any story:

```
                          ▲
                         /|\
                        / | \
          Rising       /  |  \   Falling
          Action      /   |   \  Action
                     /    |    \
     Exposition  ───/     |     \─── Resolution
                       CLIMAX
```

| Stage | Description | What to Include |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Exposition** | Introduces the characters, setting, and initial situation | Establish the "normal world" — who, where, and when; hint at the coming conflict |
| **Rising Action** | Tension and conflict build; events complicate the situation | Obstacles, complications, character under pressure; reader's anxiety increases |
| **Climax** | The turning point — the moment of greatest tension or change | The most dramatic or significant single event in the story |
| **Falling Action** | Consequences of the climax unfold | The aftermath; things begin to settle or unravel |
| **Resolution** | The situation is resolved — happily, tragically, or ambiguously | Final state of characters and world; any unanswered questions are closed or deliberately left open |

### Exam Reality — Streamlined Structure

In a timed exam (30–40 minutes for one writing question), you cannot write all five full stages. Use this practical approach instead:

```
OPENING    →  Hook the reader immediately (in medias res, mystery, dialogue)
BODY       →  Build tension toward one central dramatic moment
CLIMAX     →  The turning point — your most powerful writing
ENDING     →  A single strong closing beat (twist, reflection, circular reference)
```

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Quality and control matter far more than length. An examiner would rather read 400 words of excellent, controlled narrative than 800 words of rushed, undeveloped writing. Plan for 5 minutes, write for 30 minutes, check for 5 minutes.

## 5.2 Narrative Perspectives and Their Effects

Choose ONE perspective and maintain it throughout your story. Switching is a serious error.

| Perspective | Description | Effect | Best Used When |
|------------|-------------|--------|---------------|
| **1st person ("I")** | Narrator is a character within the story | Deep intimacy and immediacy; limits view to one perspective; narrator may be unreliable | You want the reader to feel "inside" the character's experience |
| **2nd person ("you")** | Reader is directly addressed as the protagonist | Unusual and immersive; pulls reader into the story; can feel disorientating | You want a distinctive, unconventional effect |
| **3rd person limited** | Narrator uses "he/she/they" but stays close to one character's perspective | Some intimacy; slightly more distance than 1st person; still focused view | You want closeness with one character but slightly more narrative freedom |
| **3rd person omniscient** | Narrator knows all characters' thoughts and feelings | God-like view; can provide dramatic irony; broader scope | Longer stories where multiple characters' perspectives matter |

### Unreliable Narrators
A first-person narrator whose account the reader cannot fully trust is a sophisticated technique. Signs of unreliability:
- The narrator misunderstands events
- Other characters' actions contradict what the narrator says
- The narrator's emotional state distorts their account

## 5.3 Opening Techniques — Hooking the Reader

The first paragraph of your story is the most important. It must grab the reader immediately.

| Technique | How It Works | Strong Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **In medias res** | Begin in the middle of the action — no preamble or slow build-up | *"The car was already on fire when she pulled him out."* |
| **Dialogue opener** | Begin with a line of speech that raises immediate questions | *"'Don't look down,' he said. I looked down."* |
| **Setting / atmosphere** | Create a vivid, atmospheric scene that immediately establishes mood | *"The village had been silent for three days. No birds. No wind. Nothing at all."* |
| **Mystery / hook** | An intriguing statement that poses immediate questions | *"The last thing I remember before the accident is laughing."* |
| **Character revelation** | Dive straight into revealing something unusual about a character | *"My grandmother had one rule: she never lied. She had two thousand exceptions."* |
| **Second person address** | Involve the reader directly | *"You have been here before, though you do not know it yet."* |
| **Shocking statement** | A surprising opening fact or claim | *"By the time I realised what she had done, it was already too late to stop her."* |

> [!note] Avoid opening with "It was a dark and stormy night," "Once upon a time," or waking up from a dream — these are clichéd and signal to examiners that the writer lacks originality.

## 5.4 Character Development — Showing, Not Telling

Strong characters are revealed through action, dialogue, and detail — not through physical description or direct statements about personality.

| Method | What It Does | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Action** | Character's choices under pressure reveal their values and personality | He doesn't say he's generous — he quietly pays for a stranger's meal and says nothing |
| **Dialogue** | What and how a character speaks reveals personality, relationships, and emotional state | Speaks in clipped, defensive sentences when asked about her family |
| **Thought / internal monologue** | Reveals the gap between what a character shows and what they feel | *She smiled. Nodded. Said of course, it was fine. It was not fine.* |
| **Reaction** | How a character responds to events reveals who they really are | Stays completely calm while everyone around them panics |
| **Others' responses** | How other characters perceive and react to a character | Everyone in the room defers to him before he has spoken a word |

### Character Flaws — Making Characters Believable

Perfect characters are boring. Give your protagonist at least one flaw:
- Overconfidence, cowardice, jealousy, recklessness, stubbornness, dishonesty, naivety

A character who overcomes (or fails to overcome) their flaw is the heart of any compelling narrative.

> [!note] Do NOT spend whole paragraphs describing a character's physical appearance. Examiners are not impressed by long descriptions of what someone looks like. What a character DOES is far more revealing and interesting.

## 5.5 Setting and Atmosphere — Environmental Storytelling

Setting does not just tell us where a story takes place — it creates atmosphere and can reflect or contrast with character emotions.

| Technique | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Matching weather or environment to the emotional mood | A storm breaking as the conflict reaches its peak |
| **Symbolic settings** | A place that represents an idea or emotional state | A locked room = entrapment; an open field = freedom; a crumbling house = a decaying relationship |
| **Sensory engagement** | Appealing to all five senses to make the setting feel real | Not just what is seen, but heard, smelled, and felt |
| **Contrasted setting** | A beautiful exterior concealing something sinister | A bright sunny garden where something terrible happened |
| **Dynamic setting** | A setting that changes to mirror plot development | The weather brightening as a character begins to heal |

### Environmental Storytelling Principle:
Rather than describing your setting all at once in a single block, weave details of setting into the action as the story progresses.

**Block description (weak):**
> *"The house was old. It had cracked walls and broken windows. The garden was overgrown."*

**Woven into action (strong):**
> *"She pushed open the gate — it resisted, hinges thick with rust. Her shoe sank into the grass, which was so high it reached her knee. She did not look at the windows. She knew what she would see."*

## 5.6 Dialogue — Rules and Techniques

### Punctuation Rules for Speech:

```
"I don't believe you," she said, her voice barely a whisper.
                        ↑ comma before the speech tag; lowercase 'she'

"You should," he replied. "It happened exactly as I described."
             ↑ full stop ends the tag; new speech starts with capital letter

"Then why," Maria pressed, "did no one else see it?"
            ↑ tag interrupts speech; comma before and after the tag; lowercase continues
```

### Key Dialogue Rules:
- Start a **new paragraph** for each new speaker — every time
- Punctuation goes **inside** the closing quotation marks
- Use a **comma** before the speech tag if the speech could continue
- Use a **full stop** if the speech ends a complete thought and no tag follows
- Vary your speech tags — whispered, muttered, snapped, demanded, insisted — but don't force unusual tags where "said" works better

### Functions of Dialogue in Narrative:

| Function | Example Use |
|----------|------------|
| **Reveal character** | What people say and how they say it tells us who they are |
| **Advance plot** | Characters share information that moves the story forward |
| **Create conflict** | Disagreement, misunderstanding, tension between characters |
| **Control pace** | Short, snappy dialogue speeds the narrative; longer exchanges slow it |
| **Provide context** | Exposition delivered through natural conversation feels less forced |

## 5.7 "Show Don't Tell" — The Most Important Principle

"Show don't tell" means demonstrating an emotion, quality, or idea through specific details, actions, and sensory language — rather than simply stating it.

| Telling (Weak) | Showing (Strong) |
|---------------|-----------------|
| "She was nervous." | "She wound the strap of her bag over and over her fingers, her eyes darting to the clock above the door." |
| "He was furious." | "He set the coffee cup down with such deliberate, controlled care that the counter cracked." |
| "The place felt sad." | "Toys lay scattered in the mud. A single wellington boot sat by the gate, waiting for a foot that would never return." |
| "It was bitterly cold." | "Her breath came in clouds. She pressed her hands deep into her armpits and tried, unsuccessfully, not to shiver." |
| "She was brave." | "Her hands were shaking. She walked in anyway." |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Every time you write a sentence that starts with "she was [emotion]" or "he felt [adjective]," stop and rewrite it as a piece of showing. This single habit will transform the quality of your narrative writing.

## 5.8 Pacing — Controlling Speed

Pacing is how fast or slow a narrative feels. It is controlled through deliberate language and structural choices.

| Technique | Effect on Pace | When to Use It |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| **Short, simple sentences** | Speeds up; creates tension, urgency, action | Action scenes; moments of shock; building to a climax |
| **Long, flowing sentences** | Slows down; suits reflection, description, calm | Calm moments; character thought; setting description |
| **Dialogue** | Speeds up; dynamic and immediate | Tense exchanges; arguments; plot-advancing conversations |
| **Rich description** | Slows down; immerses the reader in detail | Key setting moments; before or after the climax |
| **Time jumps** | Speeds up narrative time; skips over less important events | Transitioning between scenes; moving forward in time |
| **Slow motion moments** | Expands a single moment with minute, intense detail | The climax; a moment of revelation; an act of violence or crisis |

### Pacing Example — The Same Event at Two Speeds:

**Fast (action, tension):**
> *She ran. Door. Stairs. Another door. The sound behind her — closer now. She didn't look back.*

**Slow (reflection, calm):**
> *She sat at the window for a long while, watching the light change on the garden below — the way it shifted from gold to amber to a pale, exhausted grey, as if the day itself were reluctant to end.*

## 5.9 Narrative Arc Planning — Quick Brainstorm Method

In the exam, spend 5 minutes planning before you write. Use this template:

```
1. HOOK:     What is the first sentence? (Write it before anything else)
2. WHO:      1–2 main characters — give each a clear want and a flaw
3. WHERE:    Setting — one vivid sensory detail you will use
4. TENSION:  What is the central conflict? (internal / external / both)
5. CLIMAX:   What is the turning point — the single most dramatic moment?
6. END:      Final line — does it resolve, twist, reflect, or open up?
```

> 💡 **Exam tip:** The most common reason for weak narrative endings is not planning ahead. Write your ending in your plan BEFORE you begin writing the story. This means you can build towards it deliberately, creating foreshadowing and coherence.

## 5.10 Ending Techniques

Your final paragraph is the last impression you leave. Make it count.

| Ending Type | Description | Effect |
|------------|-------------|--------|
| **Circular ending** | Ends with an echo of how it began — a repeated image, phrase, or situation | Creates a satisfying sense of completeness; can suggest stasis or irony if nothing has truly changed |
| **Twist ending** | Reveals new information that reframes everything the reader thought they understood | Surprises and satisfies; makes the reader want to re-read |
| **Open ending** | The conflict is not fully resolved — the story ends with ambiguity | Creates lingering unease or thought; forces the reader to draw their own conclusions |
| **Resolved ending** | All questions answered; characters reach a clear final state | Satisfying and conclusive; provides emotional closure |
| **Reflective ending** | The narrator reflects on the meaning or significance of what happened | Creates emotional resonance; elevates the story's theme |

### Strong Final Lines — Examples:

- **Circular:** *"She put the letter in the drawer. She had done it a hundred times before. She would do it a hundred times again."*
- **Twist:** *"It was only when she reached the car that she realised the keys she was holding were not hers."*
- **Reflective:** *"I don't know what became of the boy from the bus. I have thought about him more often than I can explain."*

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Narrative** | A story; a structured account of events |
| **Perspective / Point of view** | The position from which a story is told (1st, 2nd, or 3rd person) |
| **Narrator** | The voice telling the story (may or may not be a character within it) |
| **Exposition** | The opening stage of a story; introduces characters, setting, and situation |
| **Climax** | The moment of greatest tension or the major turning point in a narrative |
| **Resolution** | The final stage; conflict is resolved and the story concludes |
| **In medias res** | A Latin term meaning "into the middle of things"; beginning a story mid-action |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Using weather or environment to reflect the emotional state of characters |
| **Show don't tell** | Conveying emotion and character through specific action and detail, not direct statement |
| **Pacing** | The speed at which a narrative moves, controlled through sentence length and structure |
| **Dialogue** | Spoken words between characters in a narrative |
| **Circular structure** | A narrative that ends by returning to its beginning |
| **Freytag's Pyramid** | A model of narrative structure: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, resolution |
| **Unreliable narrator** | A narrator whose account of events cannot be fully trusted |
| **Internal conflict** | A struggle within a character's own mind or emotions |
| **External conflict** | A struggle between a character and an outside force (person, nature, society) |
