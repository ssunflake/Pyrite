## 1.1 Types of Comprehension Questions

There are three main categories of comprehension question in Cambridge IGCSE English Language:

| Question Type | Description | What the Examiner Expects |
|--------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| **Explicit (surface)** | The answer is stated directly in the text | Find and paraphrase the relevant section; sometimes a direct short quote is acceptable |
| **Implicit (inferential)** | The answer is suggested but not directly stated — you must "read between the lines" | Infer meaning from textual clues and support every inference with embedded evidence |
| **Evaluative** | You are asked to judge how effectively the writer achieves a particular effect or purpose | Make a clear, personal judgement supported by detailed evidence and analysis |

> [!Note] Most IGCSE questions blend more than one type. A question asking "How does the writer create tension?" requires both implicit reading AND language analysis. Never answer only "what" when the question asks "how."

---

## 1.2 Command Words and What They Mean

Understanding the command word is the single most important step before writing your answer.

| Command Word | What the Question Wants | Common Mistake to Avoid |
|-------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Explain** | Give reasons for something or describe how it works | Describing without giving a "because" — no cause, no marks |
| **Describe** | Give a detailed account of what something is like | Listing without elaboration or evidence |
| **Suggest** | Offer a possible interpretation supported by evidence | Stating it as definite fact rather than as an inference |
| **How** | Explain the method, technique, or way in which something is done | Identifying WHAT instead of HOW — always discuss technique |
| **Why** | Give reasons or motivations | Listing effects instead of causes or reasons |
| **What** | Identify or state specific information | Being too vague or general |
| **Give** | Provide specific examples, usually directly from the text | Paraphrasing when the question wants a direct example |
| **Select** | Choose relevant material from the text | Including off-topic or tangential material |
| **Identify** | Name or point out something specific | Writing about the wrong feature or being too broad |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Underline the command word before you begin writing. It tells you exactly what kind of response is needed. Misreading "how" as "what" is one of the most common reasons for losing marks on comprehension questions.

## 1.3 Mark Allocation Strategy

Understanding how many marks are available tells you how much to write.

| Marks Available | Points / Depth Required | Structure to Use |
|----------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **1 mark** | One clear, relevant point | A single sentence |
| **2 marks** | One well-explained point OR two brief points | Two sentences or one sentence + reason |
| **3 marks** | Two explained points OR one full PEE | Short PEE paragraph |
| **4–5 marks** | Two full PEE paragraphs | Two developed paragraphs with evidence |
| **6–8 marks** | Three to four PEE paragraphs | Extended response with nuanced analysis |
| **10+ marks** | Multiple perspectives, sustained analysis | PEEL structure, evaluative commentary |

### Time Allocation by Mark Value

A rough guide for a 40-mark paper in 1h 45min:

| Marks | Time to Spend |
|-------|--------------|
| 1–2 marks | 2–4 minutes |
| 3–5 marks | 5–8 minutes |
| 6–10 marks | 10–15 minutes |
| 10+ marks | 15–20 minutes |

> [!Note] Do not over-write on low-mark questions. Writing a full essay for a 2-mark question wastes time you need for the higher-value questions.

## 1.4 PEE Structure — Point, Evidence, Explanation

PEE is the core framework for answering comprehension and analysis questions at IGCSE.

```
POINT       →  State your main idea or direct answer clearly
EVIDENCE    →  Quote or reference the specific part of the text
EXPLANATION →  Explain how the evidence supports your point
              (What does it suggest? What is the effect? Why does the writer use this?)
```

### Worked Example

**Question:** How does the writer convey the character's fear in this passage? [3]

---

**Weak answer (no PEE — narrative summary):**
> "The character is scared. There is a torch involved. The writer uses good description to make the reader feel worried."

This answer identifies WHAT happens but not HOW or WHY the writer creates the effect. It would score 0–1 marks.

---

**Strong answer (full PEE):**
> The writer conveys the character's fear through vivid physical description. He writes that "his hands trembled so violently the torch clattered to the ground," selecting the adverb "violently" to emphasise the involuntary, uncontrollable nature of the character's terror. This physical response implies that the fear has overwhelmed the character's conscious control — his body is reacting before his mind can intervene — making the emotion feel immediate and visceral to the reader.

**Breakdown of this answer:**
- **P** → "The writer conveys the character's fear through vivid physical description."
- **E** → "his hands trembled so violently the torch clattered to the ground"
- **E** → Explains the specific effect of "violently"; connects to implied loss of control; identifies the effect on the reader

---

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Your explanation must go beyond what the quote literally says. Ask yourself: *What does this word suggest? What does the reader infer? What emotion does it create?* If your explanation could be said without reading the text, it is not analytical enough.

## 1.5 Skimming, Scanning, and Close Reading

These three reading strategies serve different purposes. Knowing when to use each saves time and improves accuracy.

| Strategy | How It Works | When to Use It |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Skimming** | Read quickly, taking in headings, opening sentences, and general shape | First read-through of the text to grasp the overall topic, tone, and purpose |
| **Scanning** | Move your eyes rapidly over the text looking for specific keywords or information | When answering a specific question that asks for particular details, names, or dates |
| **Close reading** | Read slowly and carefully, analysing every word choice, punctuation mark, and structure | Language analysis questions; evaluative questions; summary writing |

### Recommended Exam Reading Process:

1. **Skim** the whole text (60–90 seconds) — understand what it is about and its overall tone
2. Read all the questions carefully — know what information you are looking for
3. **Scan** for the relevant sections of text as you answer each question
4. Apply **close reading** to language analysis and evaluation questions

## 1.6 Selecting and Using Evidence — Quoting from the Text

Knowing HOW to quote is as important as knowing WHAT to quote.

### Short Embedded Quotes (Preferred Technique)

Weave a short phrase (2–7 words) seamlessly into your own sentence. This demonstrates control and shows you have selected only the most significant language.

**Example:**
> The writer describes the sea as "a churning grey monster," implying something dangerous and barely contained beneath its surface.

### Longer Block Quotes (Use Sparingly)

Used when a longer section genuinely needs to be discussed in full. Introduce the quote with a colon, then analyse it immediately.

**Example:**
> The writer's description — "the cliffs rose sheer and black above her, and the wind came off the sea like a warning she could not read" — layers visual and tactile imagery to create a sense of overwhelming, incomprehensible threat.

### Rules for Quoting:
- Always use inverted commas (" ")
- Copy the text **exactly** — even if it contains an error
- Use [...] if you omit part of a quote to save space
- Do **not** over-quote — select only the most powerful words
- Always analyse immediately after quoting — never leave a quote "hanging" without explanation

> [!Note] Copying a whole sentence from the text and calling it a "quote" suggests you do not know which part is significant. Examiners want to see precise, targeted selection of language.

## 1.7 Reading for Tone, Mood, and Atmosphere

These three concepts are closely related but have distinct meanings. Being able to identify and discuss all three is a higher-order skill.

| Term | Definition | How to Identify It |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Tone** | The writer's attitude towards their subject or audience | Word choices — positive/negative connotations; formal/casual register; presence of irony |
| **Mood** | The emotional feeling created in the reader | How does the text make YOU feel? Look at descriptive and emotive language |
| **Atmosphere** | The overall "feel" or ambience of a scene or setting | Setting descriptions, sensory language, sentence length and pace |

### How to Identify Tone — Ask Yourself:
- Is the writer being serious, humorous, critical, nostalgic, hopeful, or despairing?
- Look at **adjective and adverb choices** — are they positive or negative?
- Look at **sentence rhythm** — long and flowing (calm, reflective) or short and punchy (tense, urgent)?
- Is there **irony or sarcasm** present?
- What is the writer's **relationship to the subject** — admiring, contemptuous, melancholic?

| Tone | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Melancholic | Deeply sad and reflective |
| Celebratory | Joyful and triumphant |
| Tense | Anxious and apprehensive |
| Nostalgic | Warm, wistful longing for the past |
| Satirical | Using humour or irony to criticise |
| Defiant | Boldly resistant or challenging |
| Anxious | Uneasy, worried |
| Wistful | Tenderly sad, with longing |
| Indignant | Angry at something perceived as unjust |
| Solemn | Serious and dignified |
| Admiring | Respectful, full of praise |
| Sceptical | Doubtful and questioning |

## 1.8 Finding Meaning from Context — Vocabulary Questions

Some questions ask you to explain the meaning of a word or phrase **as it is used in context**. This is different from giving a dictionary definition.

### Strategy:
1. Re-read the sentence the word appears in, plus the sentences before and after it
2. Look for clues in the surrounding language (what kind of text is this? what is the tone?)
3. Think about what would make sense given the subject and overall argument
4. Offer a definition that reflects the word's **specific function** in this context

**Example Question:** *Explain the meaning of the word "desolate" as used in line 12.*

**Poor answer:**
> "Desolate means very sad or empty."

**Good answer:**
> In this context, "desolate" conveys a sense of complete emptiness combined with deep emotional bleakness. The surrounding description of the abandoned buildings and the silence reinforces this — the word suggests not just physical emptiness but an overwhelming, almost suffocating sense of loss and abandonment that the narrator experiences on returning to the village.

> [!Note] Never simply give a dictionary definition. The examiner wants to see that you understand how the word is being used — its connotations, its function in context, and the effect it creates in this specific passage.

## 1.9 Common Traps to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Loses Marks | How to Avoid It |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Lifting whole sentences** | Demonstrates no selection skill or understanding of which language matters | Select only the 2–5 most significant words; embed them |
| **Going off-topic** | Does not address what the question actually asks | Reread the question before writing each paragraph |
| **No evidence** | Claims without textual support cannot be credited | Every point needs a quote or specific textual reference |
| **Retelling the story** | Narrative summary is not analysis | Focus on HOW and WHY the writer does things, not WHAT happens |
| **Vague explanations** | "It makes the reader feel interested" earns no marks | Be specific: what emotion exactly? what effect? why? |
| **Ignoring text type** | Missing that the text is an editorial, letter, speech, or travel writing | Always identify the form and purpose in your first read |
| **Rushing short questions** | Incomplete answers on 1–2 mark questions due to nerves | These should take only 2–3 minutes; answer precisely and move on |
| **Under-writing for long questions** | Short answers on high-mark questions | Count the marks: 5 marks = at least two well-developed PEE paragraphs |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Explicit information** | Information stated directly and clearly in the text |
| **Implicit information** | Information suggested or implied but not directly stated |
| **Inference** | A conclusion drawn logically from evidence in the text |
| **Evidence** | A quotation or textual reference used to support a point |
| **PEE** | Point–Evidence–Explanation: a structured analytical paragraph format |
| **PEEL** | Point–Evidence–Explanation–Link: an extended paragraph format for higher-level analysis |
| **Tone** | The writer's attitude towards their subject |
| **Mood** | The emotional feeling created in the reader by the text |
| **Atmosphere** | The overall feel or ambience of a scene or piece of writing |
| **Skimming** | Reading quickly to get the overall gist of a text |
| **Scanning** | Reading rapidly to locate specific information |
| **Close reading** | Slow, careful, analytical reading that considers every word |
| **Embedded quote** | A short quotation woven naturally and grammatically into your own sentence |
| **Context** | The surrounding words, sentences, or situation that helps explain meaning |
| **Command word** | The key instructional word in a question that tells you exactly how to respond |
| **Connotation** | The emotional or cultural associations carried by a word |
| **Metalanguage** | Vocabulary used to discuss language itself (e.g., metaphor, tone, structure) |
