## 4.1 What Is Directed Writing?

Directed writing is a task where you must:
1. **Read** a source text carefully
2. **Write a response** in a specified form (e.g., a letter, speech, article, report)
3. **Draw on and adapt material from the source text** — selecting, rephrasing, and expanding on it
4. **Write for a specific, stated audience and purpose**
5. **Use the appropriate register, layout, and language** for the given form

The task will always tell you:
- **What form** to write in (letter, report, speech, article, interview, etc.)
- **Who the audience is** (a headteacher, the general public, a friend, a newspaper reader, etc.)
- **What the purpose is** (to persuade, inform, advise, entertain, etc.)

> [!Note] Directed writing tests TWO things simultaneously: your ability to extract and adapt information from the source text, AND your ability to write convincingly in the specified form. You must do BOTH to earn high marks.

## 4.2 Forms and Their Layout Conventions

### Master Layout Reference Table

| Form | Key Layout Features | Opening | Closing |
|------|-------------------|---------|---------|
| **Formal letter** | Sender address (top right), date below, recipient name/address (left), formal salutation, paragraphs | Dear Sir/Madam | Yours faithfully |
| **Informal letter** | Optional address, friendly tone, contractions acceptable, paragraphs | Dear [First Name] | Yours sincerely / With love / Best wishes |
| **Speech** | Address audience directly, rhetorical devices, direct address ("you"), sign off | "Good morning, ladies and gentlemen / Fellow students" | "Thank you for listening / for your time" |
| **Newspaper article** | Bold or CAPS headline, byline (By [Name]), opening paragraph covering 5 Ws, subheadings, quotes from sources, third person | Headline in bold | No formal closing — article ends naturally |
| **Magazine article** | Engaging headline, subheadings, may use pull quotes, tone matches target readership | Hook sentence / attention-grabbing opening | Strong final thought or call to action |
| **Report** | Clear title, formal headings (Introduction / Findings / Recommendations / Conclusion), impersonal tone, may use numbered sections | "Introduction" section | "Recommendations" or "Conclusion" section |
| **Interview** | Q&A format, interviewer name/role on left, natural but purposeful questions, interviewee's full responses | "Interviewer: Thank you for joining us today..." | "Interviewer: Thank you for your time." |
| **Email** | Subject line, appropriate greeting, sign-off appropriate to relationship | Subject: [Topic] | Regards / Best wishes / [Name] |

### Formal Letter Layout — Full Template

```
                                    [Your Address Line 1]
                                    [Town, County, Postcode]
                                    [Date written in full: 14 January 2027]

[Recipient's Title and Name, if known]
[Organisation / School / Company]
[Recipient's Address]

Dear Sir/Madam,  [if name unknown]
Dear Mr/Ms [Surname],  [if name known]

[Opening paragraph: state clearly who you are and why you are writing]

[Body paragraph 1: first main point — developed with evidence and explanation]

[Body paragraph 2: second main point — developed with evidence and explanation]

[Body paragraph 3: third main point or counter-argument / emotional appeal]

[Closing paragraph: summarise your position; make a specific request or call to action]

Yours faithfully,  [if you opened with 'Dear Sir/Madam']
Yours sincerely,  [if you opened with the person's name]

[Your Full Name]
```

> 💡 **Exam tip:** The Yours faithfully / Yours sincerely distinction is a classic exam trap. Remember: **faithfully if you don't know the name** (both begin with a repeated-first-letter); **sincerely if you do know the name**.

### Speech Layout — Full Template

```
[Appropriate greeting for the audience]
Good morning, ladies and gentlemen,
Fellow students,
Members of the committee,

[Hook — rhetorical question, shocking statistic, bold statement, or anecdote]

[Body — develop your main arguments using AFOREST persuasive techniques;
         use direct address ("you"), inclusive language ("we"), and rhetorical questions
         to maintain engagement]

[Counter-argument and rebuttal — if applicable]

[Conclusion — summarise your key message; end with a memorable line or call to action]

Thank you for listening.
Thank you for your time and attention.
```

```
[HEADLINE IN BOLD CAPITALS — Short, punchy, engaging]

By [Your Name], [Publication Name], [Date]

[Opening paragraph: answers Who, What, Where, When, Why — the most important information first]

[Paragraph 2: Develop the story with context and background detail]

[Subheading in bold]

[Subheading in bold]
[Paragraph 4: Eyewitness/participant quotes; human interest angle]
```

## 4.3 Identifying Audience and Purpose

Before writing a single word of your response, identify these three elements from the task:

| Element | Questions to Ask | How It Shapes Your Writing |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| **Audience** | Who are you writing for? What is their age, knowledge level, and relationship to you? | Vocabulary level, formality, examples chosen, assumed knowledge |
| **Purpose** | What are you trying to achieve? Persuade? Inform? Advise? Entertain? | Techniques used, structure, emphasis, tone |
| **Form** | What layout and conventions must you use? | How you open, organise, and close the piece |

### Common Audience × Purpose × Register Combinations

| Audience | Purpose | Register |
|---------|---------|---------|
| School headteacher | Persuade to change a policy | Formal, measured, respectful |
| Fellow students | Inform about an issue | Semi-formal, engaging, relatable |
| Parents / guardians | Advise about a concern | Formal but warm and accessible |
| General public (newspaper) | Report on a news event | Neutral, objective, factual |
| Younger students | Inform or advise | Simple vocabulary, friendly tone |
| A close friend | Recount or advise | Informal, personal, conversational |
| Local council | Persuade on community issue | Formal, evidence-based, authoritative |

---

## 4.4 Register Selection

Your register must match your audience and purpose. Getting this wrong loses significant language marks.

| Feature | Formal Register | Informal Register |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Vocabulary | Sophisticated ("therefore," "considerable," "furthermore") | Simple ("so," "a lot," "really") |
| Contractions | Avoid entirely ("do not," "it is," "cannot") | Natural and common ("don't," "it's," "can't") |
| First person | Limited, impersonal ("one might argue," "it is clear that") | Free and personal ("I," "you," "we," "honestly") |
| Sentence length | Longer, more complex | Shorter, more conversational |
| Humour | Subtle and rare | Natural and frequent |
| Slang / colloquialisms | Never | Occasionally, where appropriate |

## 4.5 How to Use Source Text Material — The SELECT, ADAPT, EXPAND Method

Directed writing tasks provide a source text specifically for you to draw on. You are expected to:

### SELECT
Choose the points from the source text that are most relevant to your task. You do not need to use everything — be selective. Aim for 4–6 source points, adapted into your response.

### ADAPT
Reframe the source material to suit your new form, audience, and purpose. Change the language level, tone, and perspective as needed.

### EXPAND
Add your own ideas, invented examples, logical extensions, or emotional elaborations. This is where your writing moves beyond the source text.

### Worked Example:

**Source text says:** *"Air pollution from vehicles causes significant health problems, particularly for children."*

**Task:** Write a persuasive speech to the school council arguing for a ban on car drop-offs.

**Weak (lifted from source):**
> "Air pollution from vehicles causes significant health problems, particularly for children."

**Strong (adapted and expanded):**
> "As you are aware, vehicle emissions are now identified as one of the leading causes of respiratory illness in young people. And yet every single morning, hundreds of cars idle directly outside our school gates, pouring fumes into the air that your students — our children — breathe. How can we justify this? How can we allow it to continue?"

The writer selects the source idea, adapts the language to a speech register, adds direct address, rhetorical questions, and emotional emphasis.

## 4.6 Adding Your Own Ideas

Do not rely solely on the source text. Excellent directed writing also includes:
- Personal anecdotes (appropriate for letters and speeches)
- Invented but plausible statistics (for speeches and articles)
- Logical extensions and consequences of the source's argument
- Specific, concrete examples relevant to the stated audience

> [!Important] You are NOT being tested on factual accuracy. You can invent supporting details. What matters is that your ideas are plausible, relevant, and developed. A statistic you invent (e.g., "Research by Cambridge University suggests that...") will not be penalised — it will earn marks for developing your argument.

## 4.7 Mark Scheme

Cambridge IGCSE Directed Writing is typically marked out of 20 (or 15 in some component versions):

| Category | Marks | What Is Assessed |
|----------|-------|-----------------|
| **Content** | Up to 10 (or 15) | Range and relevance of ideas selected and adapted from the source; quality and development of your own ideas |
| **Language** | Up to 10 (or 15) | Vocabulary range, grammar accuracy, sentence structure variety, stylistic control, and adherence to form conventions |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Even perfect content written in the wrong form will lose all language marks for format. Always include the key layout conventions (greeting, sign-off, headline, section headings, etc.) appropriate to your specified form — even if the rest of the content is excellent.

## 4.8 Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | How to Avoid |
|---------|-------------|
| Writing in the wrong form entirely | Read the task at least twice; identify the form explicitly; use the correct layout conventions |
| Ignoring the source text | Select and adapt at least 4–5 specific points from the source in your response |
| Copying the source word-for-word | Adapt and expand — treat the source as material to transform, not transcribe |
| Wrong register for the audience | Ask: who is reading this? What would they expect? Formal or informal? |
| No layout conventions | Include greeting, sign-off, headline, subheadings — whatever the form requires |
| Too short | Aim for 280–400 words; plan before you write |
| No structure | Plan your paragraphs: intro, 3–4 body paragraphs, conclusion |
| Ignoring purpose | If the task says "persuade," every paragraph should make a persuasive point |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Directed writing** | A writing task specifying form, audience, and purpose, requiring use of a source text |
| **Form** | The type of text to be written (letter, speech, article, report, interview, etc.) |
| **Audience** | The person or group for whom the text is written |
| **Purpose** | The reason for writing (to persuade, inform, advise, entertain, etc.) |
| **Register** | The level of formality and style appropriate to the audience and purpose |
| **Layout conventions** | The structural and presentational features specific to a given form (e.g., greeting, headline, sign-off) |
| **Adapt** | To modify source material to suit a new form, audience, or purpose |
| **Expand** | To develop source material with your own ideas, examples, and elaborations |
| **Content marks** | Marks for the range and relevance of ideas drawn from the source and your own additions |
| **Language marks** | Marks for the quality, accuracy, and appropriateness of the writing itself |
| **Salutation** | The greeting in a letter or speech |
| **Byline** | The journalist's name appearing below a newspaper headline |
| **5 Ws** | The five opening questions of a news article: Who, What, Where, When, Why |
