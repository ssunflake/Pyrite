## 7.1 Argument vs Persuasion — The Key Difference

These two forms of writing are closely related but have a crucial distinction that must be understood before any task.

| Feature | Argumentative / Discursive | Persuasive |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------|
| **Viewpoint** | Balanced — explores multiple perspectives fairly | One-sided — promotes one view only |
| **Tone** | Measured, logical, formal, and reasoned | Passionate, urgent, emotive |
| **Structure** | Explores different perspectives across the essay | Builds a single, sustained case |
| **Goal** | To inform the reader and allow them to judge | To change the reader's mind or move them to action |
| **Counter-argument** | Always included and discussed fairly | May be included but quickly dismissed |
| **Typical command words** | Discuss, consider, explore, evaluate | Persuade, argue, convince, write a speech |
| **Example task** | "Discuss the arguments for and against school uniform." | "Write a speech to persuade your school to ban junk food." |

> [!note] Confusing these two forms is one of the most common errors. If the task says "discuss," you MUST present both sides. If it says "persuade," you may commit fully to one position. Misidentifying the form will significantly harm your mark.

## 7.2 AFOREST — Persuasive Techniques with Full Examples

AFOREST is the key mnemonic for persuasive and argumentative techniques.

| Letter | Technique | Definition | Full Example |
|--------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **A** | **Anecdote** | A short personal or relevant story used to emotionally engage the reader | *"When my cousin was only fourteen, she was forced to leave school to work in order to help her family. She never went back. Her story is not unique — it is shared by millions."* |
| **F** | **Facts** | True, verifiable statements used to build credibility and intellectual authority | *"According to the World Health Organization, air pollution kills approximately 7 million people every year — more than malaria and HIV/AIDS combined."* |
| **O** | **Opinions** | The writer's viewpoint presented as reasonable and justified | *"It is, frankly, unacceptable that we continue to allow this to happen when the evidence is so overwhelmingly clear."* |
| **R** | **Rhetorical questions** | Questions asked for effect; no genuine answer is expected | *"How many more children must suffer before we find the will to act? How many more lives must be cut short?"* |
| **E** | **Emotive language** | Words specifically chosen to provoke a strong emotional response | *"innocent victims," "devastating consequences," "heartbreaking neglect," "vulnerable children left behind"* |
| **S** | **Statistics** | Precise numerical data used to lend weight and authority to an argument | *"A 2023 survey found that 73% of secondary students feel that exam pressure is damaging their mental health."* |
| **T** | **Triplets (rule of three)** | Three words, phrases, or clauses in a row for rhythm, balance, and emphasis | *"We must act quickly, we must act decisively, and we must act together."* |

## 7.3 Additional Persuasive Techniques

Beyond AFOREST, these techniques further strengthen persuasive and argumentative writing:

| Technique | Definition | Example | Effect |
|-----------|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Direct address** | Using "you" to speak personally to the reader | "You can make a difference today." | Creates personal responsibility and connection |
| **Inclusive language** | Using "we" and "us" to unite writer and reader | "Together, we have the power to change this." | Builds solidarity; positions reader as part of the solution |
| **Imperative verbs** | Command words that urge the reader to act | "Join us. Sign the petition. Demand change now." | Creates urgency; acts as a call to action |
| **Hyperbole** | Deliberate exaggeration for emotional emphasis | "This is the single most important issue of our generation." | Creates a sense of scale and urgency |
| **Flattery** | Complimenting the intelligence or values of the audience | "As thoughtful, forward-thinking people, you already understand the gravity of this situation." | Puts the audience on-side; makes agreement feel natural |
| **Counter-argument + rebuttal** | Acknowledging and dismissing the opposing view | "Some may argue X — but this fails to account for Y, and the evidence conclusively shows Z." | Demonstrates critical thinking; strengthens the writer's position |
| **Alliteration** | Repetition of initial consonant sounds | "Politicians prioritise profit over people." | Creates a memorable, rhythmic phrase |

## 7.4 Rhetorical Questions — Detailed Analysis

A rhetorical question is one asked for effect, not for a genuine response. It is one of the most powerful tools in persuasive writing.

### Why Rhetorical Questions Work:
- They force the reader to mentally engage with the writer's point
- They imply an "obvious" answer, making agreement feel natural
- They create emotional intensity, urgency, or moral pressure
- They challenge the reader or the "other side" to justify their position

### Types of Rhetorical Questions:

| Type | Example | Effect |
|------|---------|--------|
| **Implying an obvious answer** | "Surely no reasonable person could disagree with the need for clean water?" | Makes refusal seem unreasonable or irrational |
| **Emotional challenge** | "How much longer must our children breathe polluted air?" | Creates urgency and empathy simultaneously |
| **Challenge to authority or inaction** | "If not us, then who? If not now, then when?" | Calls for personal and immediate responsibility |
| **Sarcastic rhetorical question** | "Oh, so we are simply to accept that nothing can be done?" | Mocks the opposing argument through sarcasm |

## 7.5 PEEL Structure for Argument and Persuasion

Every body paragraph in an argumentative or persuasive piece should follow PEEL:

```
POINT      →  State your argument clearly and confidently
EVIDENCE   →  Support with a fact, statistic, example, or anecdote
EXPLAIN    →  Explain how the evidence supports your point
LINK       →  Link back to the overall argument; introduce the next point
```

### Worked Example — Full PEEL Paragraph

> **P** — Teenagers are being robbed of the sleep they need to develop and learn effectively.
> **E** — A major NHS study found that one in four UK teenagers gets fewer than seven hours of sleep per night, a shortfall directly attributed to excessive homework loads combined with screen use late into the evening.
> **E** — Sleep deprivation does not simply make young people tired — it actively impairs memory consolidation, concentration, and emotional regulation. In other words, the very faculties required for learning are destroyed by the systems we have designed to promote it.
> **L** — It is therefore not merely desirable but morally necessary that schools urgently reconsider the volume and timing of work assigned outside classroom hours.

**Breakdown:**
- **P** → "Teenagers are being robbed of the sleep they need"
- **E** → NHS statistic about sleep deprivation
- **E** → Extended explanation of WHY this matters
- **L** → Moral urgency introduced; points toward the next argument

## 7.6 Counter-Argument and Rebuttal Technique

In both discursive writing and high-level persuasive writing, addressing the opposing view before dismissing it **strengthens** your argument. It demonstrates intellectual maturity and prevents your argument from appearing naively one-sided.

### Template for Counter-Argument + Rebuttal:

> *"Some might argue that [state the opposing viewpoint fairly and clearly]. However, this view fails to account for [your rebuttal], as [evidence or reasoning that undermines the counter-argument]. In fact, the evidence overwhelmingly suggests that [reaffirm your own position]."*

### Worked Example:

> *"Some argue that social media connects young people, fosters communities, and provides a platform for creative expression — and it would be dishonest to deny that these benefits exist. However, this view fails to account for the overwhelming body of evidence showing that excessive and unregulated use leads to chronic anxiety, harmful social comparison, and cyberbullying. The benefits of connection are real; so, too, are the harms. The question is whether the two can be separated — and the current evidence suggests they cannot, not without significant structural change."*

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Including a counter-argument shows the examiner that you can think critically and appreciate complexity. This is a hallmark of the top mark band. Students who argue entirely one-sidedly with no nuance typically plateau below the highest grades.

## 7.7 Formal Register for Argument Writing

Argumentative and persuasive writing (unless a speech to peers) should use a formal register.

### Formality Upgrades:

| Informal / Weak | Formal / Strong |
|----------------|----------------|
| "loads of people" | "a significant proportion of the population" |
| "kids" | "young people" / "children" / "adolescents" |
| "it's really bad" | "the consequences are deeply concerning" |
| "everyone knows that" | "it is widely acknowledged that" / "evidence consistently demonstrates that" |
| "I think this is wrong" | "it is clear that this position is untenable" / "the evidence suggests otherwise" |
| "stuff like climate change" | "pressing environmental concerns such as climate change" |
| "the government should do something" | "policymakers must take immediate and decisive action" |

## 7.8 Discursive Essay Structure

Used when the task asks you to "discuss," "consider both sides," or "evaluate."

```
INTRODUCTION
→ Introduce the topic and explain its significance
→ Briefly acknowledge that there are multiple perspectives
→ Do NOT state your final conclusion yet

PARAGRAPH 1: Argument FOR (strongest point)
→ PEEL + persuasive/analytical technique

PARAGRAPH 2: Argument FOR (second point)
→ PEEL + supporting evidence

PARAGRAPH 3: Argument AGAINST (counter-argument)
→ PEEL + acknowledge the strength of this view

PARAGRAPH 4: Rebuttal / qualification
→ Why the argument in favour outweighs the argument against

CONCLUSION
→ Weigh up both sides explicitly
→ State your final, justified viewpoint
→ Memorable, resonant closing line
```

---

## 7.9 Persuasive Essay / Speech Structure

Used when the task asks you to persuade, argue for one side, or write a speech.

```
INTRODUCTION — HOOK
→ Attention-grabbing opening (shocking statistic, rhetorical question, bold statement, anecdote)
→ Clear statement of your position

PARAGRAPH 1: Strongest logical argument
→ PEEL + fact or statistic

PARAGRAPH 2: Second argument
→ PEEL + example or anecdote

PARAGRAPH 3: Emotional appeal
→ Emotive language, personal story, direct address — make the reader FEEL the issue

PARAGRAPH 4: Counter-argument + rebuttal
→ Acknowledge the other side; then dismantle it

CONCLUSION: Call to action
→ Concise summary of your strongest points
→ Final memorable line — circular reference, command, or question
```

## 7.10 Opening Techniques — First Impressions Matter

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Shocking statistic** | "Every two minutes, a child dies from a preventable, waterborne disease. Every. Two. Minutes." |
| **Rhetorical question** | "When was the last time you truly felt safe walking home alone at night?" |
| **Bold statement** | "Our education system is, at this moment, failing an entire generation." |
| **Anecdote** | "In 2019, a sixteen-year-old stood before the United Nations and changed the direction of a global conversation. Her name was Greta Thunberg. She was not invited. She insisted." |
| **Direct address** | "You have a choice to make — and you must make it today, before it is made for you." |
| **Juxtaposition** | "We live in the richest era in human history. Tonight, 700 million people will go to bed hungry." |

## 7.11 Conclusion Techniques — Leave a Lasting Impression

| Technique | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Call to action** | "Write to your MP. Sign the petition. Refuse to be silent any longer." |
| **Circular reference** | (Returns to the opening image) "That child who dies every two minutes? They are not a statistic. They are waiting for you to act." |
| **Memorable final line** | "The future does not belong to those who waited. It belongs to those who refused to." |
| **Summary + uplift** | Briefly restate key points; end with a hopeful or urgent vision of what could be achieved |
| **Challenging question** | "The question is not whether we can afford to act. It is whether we can afford to wait any longer." |

## 7.12 Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

| Mistake | How to Avoid |
|---------|-------------|
| Being too informal | Check every sentence — would this be acceptable in a formal speech or essay? |
| Forgetting the counter-argument | In discursive writing, ALWAYS include one balanced counter-argument paragraph |
| No evidence or examples | Every argument needs support — fact, statistic, anecdote, or example |
| Repeating the same point | Each paragraph must introduce a NEW argument, not restate the previous one |
| Weak conclusion | Don't just repeat your introduction — evaluate, conclude, and create lasting impact |
| Ignoring the form | A speech must sound like a speech (greeting, direct address, rhetorical devices, sign-off) |
| Excessive emotion, no logic | Balance emotive language with reasoned argument — pure emotion reads as ranting |
| Starting paragraphs with "I think..." | Use "It is clear that..." / "The evidence demonstrates..." / "One cannot deny that..." |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Persuasive writing** | One-sided writing designed to convince the reader of a specific viewpoint |
| **Argumentative / discursive writing** | Balanced writing that explores multiple perspectives before reaching a conclusion |
| **AFOREST** | Mnemonic for persuasive techniques: Anecdote, Facts, Opinions, Rhetorical questions, Emotive language, Statistics, Triplets |
| **Counter-argument** | The opposing viewpoint, presented in order to be acknowledged and then dismissed |
| **Rebuttal** | The response that dismisses or undermines the counter-argument |
| **Rhetoric** | The art of effective or persuasive speaking and writing |
| **Rhetorical question** | A question asked for effect, not requiring a genuine answer |
| **Tricolon** | A list or pattern of three items for rhythm and emphasis |
| **Emotive language** | Words chosen to provoke a specific emotional response in the reader |
| **Call to action** | A conclusion or statement that urges the reader or audience to take specific action |
| **Register** | The level of formality appropriate to the audience and purpose |
| **Direct address** | Using "you" to speak personally to the reader or audience |
| **Inclusive language** | Using "we" to unite the writer and reader as a collective |
| **Imperative verb** | A command verb that urges action (e.g., "Act," "Join," "Demand," "Consider") |
