## 11.1 Email vs Letter — Key Differences

| Feature | Formal Email | Formal Letter | Informal Email |
|---------|-------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Opening** | *Dear Mr Smith,* | *Dear Mr Smith,* | *Hi Jake,* / *Hey,* |
| **Closing** | *Yours sincerely,* | *Yours sincerely,* | *Best,* / *Thanks,* / *See you,* |
| **Tone** | Polite, professional | Polite, professional | Relaxed, friendly |
| **Language** | Formal, no contractions | Formal, no contractions | Informal, contractions fine |
| **Subject line** | Required — concise and clear | Not applicable | Optional but helpful |
| **Length** | Concise — no padding | Can be longer | As needed |
| **Paragraphs** | Short, clear paragraphs | Traditional paragraphs | Flexible |

> [!note] In the exam, always check whether the task asks for a **formal** or **informal** email. The register must be consistent throughout — mixing formal and informal language loses marks.

## 11.2 Formal Email — Full Layout

```
To: recipient@example.com
Subject: [Clear, specific subject — e.g. "Enquiry Regarding Work Experience Placement"]

Dear [Title + Surname],

[Opening paragraph — state purpose clearly]

[Body paragraph 1 — first main point]

[Body paragraph 2 — second main point, if needed]

[Closing paragraph — next steps / what you expect / polite close]

Yours sincerely,
[Full name]
[Position / school, if relevant]
```

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Use **"Yours sincerely"** when you know the recipient's name (Dear Mr Jones). Use **"Yours faithfully"** when you don't (Dear Sir or Madam). This rule is the same as for letters.

---

## 11.3 Informal Email — Full Layout

```
To: friend@example.com
Subject: [Optional — e.g. "Weekend plans?"]

Hi [First name] / Hey [First name],

[Friendly opener — ask how they are, reference something shared]

[Main content — reason for writing, news, question]

[Any follow-up points]

[Warm closing — looking forward to reply, etc.]

[Informal sign-off],
[First name]
```

## 11.4 Opening Lines — by Purpose

| Situation | Formal opening | Informal opening |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **First contact** | *I am writing to enquire about...* | *Hope you're well! I wanted to ask about...* |
| **Replying** | *Thank you for your email of [date] regarding...* | *Thanks for getting back to me!* |
| **Making a complaint** | *I am writing to express my dissatisfaction with...* | N/A — complaints are always formal |
| **Making a request** | *I would be grateful if you could...* | *Could you possibly...?* |
| **Applying for something** | *I am writing to apply for the position of...* | N/A |
| **Following up** | *I am writing further to my previous email regarding...* | *Just following up on my last message —* |

## 11.5 Formal Email — Key Phrases Bank

### Opening phrases
- *I am writing to enquire about / regarding / with reference to...*
- *I am writing on behalf of...*
- *Further to our recent correspondence...*
- *I would like to draw your attention to...*

### Making requests
- *I would be grateful if you could...*
- *I would appreciate it if...*
- *Could you please...*
- *I would like to request...*
- *Would it be possible for you to...*

### Giving information
- *I am pleased to inform you that...*
- *I regret to inform you that...*
- *Please be advised that...*
- *I would like to bring to your attention...*

### Complaining politely
- *I am writing to express my concern regarding...*
- *I was disappointed to find that...*
- *Unfortunately, [issue] has caused considerable inconvenience.*
- *I would appreciate it if this matter could be resolved at your earliest convenience.*

### Closing phrases
- *I look forward to hearing from you.*
- *I would welcome your response at your earliest convenience.*
- *Please do not hesitate to contact me should you require further information.*
- *Thank you for your time and consideration.*

---

## 11.6 Informal Email — Key Phrases Bank

### Opening phrases
- *Hope you're doing well!*
- *It's been ages — how are things?*
- *Just wanted to drop you a quick message about...*
- *You won't believe what happened...*

### Sharing news / information
- *Guess what —*
- *So, the thing is...*
- *I've just found out that...*
- *I wanted to let you know that...*

### Making requests
- *Would you mind...?*
- *Could you possibly...?*
- *Do you think you could...?*
- *Any chance you could...?*

### Closing phrases
- *Can't wait to hear from you!*
- *Let me know what you think.*
- *Write back soon!*
- *Hope to catch up soon.*
- *Speak soon,* / *Take care,* / *All the best,*

## 11.7 Tone and Register — Formal vs Informal Contrast

| Informal | Formal equivalent |
|----------|------------------|
| *I want to ask about...* | *I am writing to enquire about...* |
| *Can you help me?* | *I would be grateful for your assistance.* |
| *I'm not happy about this.* | *I wish to express my dissatisfaction regarding this matter.* |
| *Let me know.* | *I would appreciate your response at your earliest convenience.* |
| *Thanks a lot.* | *Thank you for your time and consideration.* |
| *Sorry for the late reply.* | *Please accept my apologies for the delay in responding.* |
| *I think...* | *I would suggest... / It is my view that...* |
| *ASAP* | *at your earliest convenience* |
| *get back to me* | *respond / reply at your convenience* |

## 11.8 Subject Lines

A subject line should be **specific, brief, and informative** — it tells the recipient exactly what the email is about before they open it.

| Weak subject line | Strong subject line |
|------------------|---------------------|
| *Hello* | *Enquiry Regarding Year 12 Work Experience Placement* |
| *Question* | *Request for Extension — History Coursework* |
| *Problem* | *Complaint: Damaged Item Received — Order #4521* |
| *Meeting* | *Rescheduling Our Meeting — 15th May* |

## 11.9 Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| ✗ Using contractions in a formal email (*I'd, can't, we're*) | ✓ Full forms only (*I would, cannot, we are*) |
| ✗ Wrong sign-off (*Yours sincerely* when you wrote *Dear Sir*) | ✓ *Dear Sir/Madam* → *Yours faithfully*; *Dear Mr X* → *Yours sincerely* |
| ✗ Vague subject line (*Hello / Question*) | ✓ Specific and informative |
| ✗ Mixing registers (formal opening, then slang mid-email) | ✓ Consistent register throughout |
| ✗ No paragraph breaks — one huge block of text | ✓ Short, clear paragraphs; one idea per paragraph |
| ✗ Repeating the same phrase (*I am writing...* used three times) | ✓ Vary your sentence openers |
| ✗ Too long and rambling | ✓ Emails should be concise — every sentence earns its place |
| ✗ Forgetting to state the purpose in the opening paragraph | ✓ Always make clear why you are writing in the first paragraph |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Formal register** | Language that is professional, polite, and avoids contractions and colloquialisms |
| **Informal register** | Relaxed, friendly language appropriate for people you know well |
| **Subject line** | A brief summary of the email's content, displayed before the body is opened |
| **Salutation** | The opening greeting: *Dear Mr Smith,* or *Hi Jake,* |
| **Sign-off** | The closing phrase before your name: *Yours sincerely, / Best wishes,* |
| **Yours sincerely** | Used when you know the recipient's name (*Dear Mr Jones*) |
| **Yours faithfully** | Used when you don't know the recipient's name (*Dear Sir/Madam*) |
| **Concise** | Using only as many words as needed — no padding or repetition |
| **Tone** | The attitude or feeling conveyed by the writing — formal, warm, apologetic, assertive, etc. |
| **Purpose** | The reason for writing — always made clear in the opening paragraph |
