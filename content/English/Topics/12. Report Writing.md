## 12.1 What is a Report?

A **report** is a formal, structured piece of writing that presents **findings, analysis, and recommendations** on a specific topic. Unlike an essay, a report:
- Uses **headings and subheadings** to organise content
- Is written for a **specific audience with a specific purpose**
- Prioritises **clarity and factual accuracy** over personal expression
- Often includes **recommendations** for action

| Feature | Report | Essay |
|---------|--------|-------|
| **Headings** | Yes — required | No |
| **Tone** | Formal, impersonal | Formal, but can be more personal |
| **Purpose** | Inform + recommend | Argue or analyse |
| **Structure** | Sections with clear labels | Introduction, body, conclusion |
| **First person** | Avoided (*"it is recommended"*) | Sometimes used |
| **Audience** | Named, specific (*a headteacher, a committee*) | General or unspecified |

---

## 12.2 Report Structure — Full Layout

```
REPORT

To:      [Recipient — name/title]
From:    [Your name/title]
Date:    [Date]
Subject: [What the report is about — concise title]

──────────────────────────────────────────

1. Introduction

[State the purpose of the report, who commissioned it, and what it covers.]

2. [Section heading — e.g. "Current Situation" / "Findings"]

[Present findings, facts, or information relevant to this section.]

3. [Section heading — e.g. "Issues Identified" / "Analysis"]

[Examine the findings — what do they mean? What problems or patterns emerge?]

4. [Section heading — e.g. "Recommendations"]

[Suggest specific, actionable steps. Use numbered lists or clear paragraphs.]

5. Conclusion

[Briefly summarise the report's key findings and the importance of acting on recommendations.]
```

> 💡 **Exam tip:** The exact section headings vary by topic — choose headings that fit what you are writing about. What matters is that sections are **clearly labelled**, **logically ordered**, and **distinct** from one another.

## 12.3 The Header Block

Every report opens with a formal header identifying who it is for, who wrote it, and what it covers:

| Field | Purpose | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| **To:** | The recipient | *The Headteacher / The School Council / Ms Patel* |
| **From:** | The writer | *A. Student, Year 11 / The Student Committee* |
| **Date:** | When written | *10 May 2026* |
| **Subject:** | What the report is about | *Report on School Canteen Facilities* |

> [!note] "Subject:" (or "Re:") replaces the need for a title — but some formats use both. Either is acceptable as long as the topic is immediately clear.

## 12.4 Writing Each Section

### 1. Introduction
State:
- **Why** the report has been written (who asked for it / what prompted it)
- **What** it covers (scope)
- **How** information was gathered (if relevant — surveys, observations, interviews)

*Example:*
> *"This report has been commissioned by the Student Council to examine the adequacy of sports facilities at Westbrook School. Information was gathered through a survey of 120 students and interviews with three members of staff."*

---

### 2. Findings / Current Situation
Present information **objectively** — what you found, not what you think about it yet. This section is factual.

- Use **specific detail** — numbers, percentages, examples
- Group related findings under the same section
- Avoid personal opinion here — save that for the analysis or recommendations

*Example:*
> *"The survey revealed that 74% of students consider the current sports hall insufficient for the school's population of 850. The hall is used for six different year groups during lunch breaks, resulting in significant overcrowding. Furthermore, equipment was described as 'outdated' or 'broken' by 61% of respondents."*

---

### 3. Analysis / Issues Identified
Now **interpret** the findings:
- What do the facts suggest?
- What problems, patterns, or causes emerge?
- What are the consequences of the current situation?

*Example:*
> *"The data indicates that inadequate facilities are discouraging student participation in physical activity. If left unaddressed, this may have longer-term implications for student health and wellbeing. The lack of equipment maintenance, in particular, presents a potential safety concern."*

---

### 4. Recommendations
Give **specific, actionable** suggestions. Number them for clarity. Each recommendation should be clearly linked to a finding.

*Example:*
> *"The following recommendations are made:*
> *1. A second multi-use sports hall should be considered as part of the school's building development plan.*
> *2. A budget of no less than £5,000 should be allocated annually for equipment maintenance and replacement.*
> *3. A rota system for year group use of facilities during lunch should be introduced immediately to reduce overcrowding."*

> ⚠️ **Avoid vague recommendations:** *"Things should be improved"* is useless. Be specific: **who** should do **what**, **by when**, and **how**.

---

### 5. Conclusion
- Briefly summarise the **key findings**
- Restate the **importance** of the recommendations
- Do **not** introduce new information

*Example:*
> *"In summary, the school's sports facilities are currently insufficient to meet the needs of its student population. The recommendations outlined above, if implemented, would meaningfully improve access to physical activity and promote a healthier school environment. Prompt action is advisable."*

## 12.5 Language and Tone

Reports use **formal, impersonal language**. Avoid first person singular — prefer passive voice or collective constructions.

| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|-------------|
| *I think the school should...* | *It is recommended that the school...* |
| *We found that...* | *The findings indicate that...* / *It was found that...* |
| *In my opinion...* | *The evidence suggests...* / *It could be argued that...* |
| *Obviously / Clearly* | *The data demonstrates...* / *It is apparent that...* |
| *A lot of students* | *A significant proportion of students* / *74% of respondents* |
| *Things need to change* | *Immediate action is advisable / recommended* |
| *The canteen is really bad* | *The canteen facilities were rated as unsatisfactory by the majority of respondents* |

### Useful Report Phrases

| Function | Phrases |
|----------|---------|
| **Stating purpose** | *This report aims to... / The purpose of this report is to... / This report was commissioned in order to...* |
| **Presenting findings** | *The survey revealed that... / It was found that... / The data indicates... / Responses suggest that...* |
| **Analysing** | *This suggests... / A possible explanation for this is... / The implications of this are...* |
| **Recommending** | *It is recommended that... / The following measures are proposed... / Action should be taken to...* |
| **Concluding** | *In conclusion... / To summarise... / Overall, the findings indicate... / Prompt action is advisable.* |

## 12.6 Headings — Choosing the Right Ones

Headings should be **specific to your topic**, not generic labels. Avoid vague headings like "Body" or "Middle Section".

| Generic (weak) | Topic-specific (strong) |
|----------------|------------------------|
| *Section 2* | *Current State of the School Library* |
| *Findings* | *Student Survey Results: Canteen Satisfaction* |
| *Problems* | *Issues Identified with Public Transport Links* |
| *Things to do* | *Recommended Improvements* |

You may also use **subheadings** within a section if it contains several distinct points — but don't over-subdivide a short section.

## 12.7 Common Report Topics (Exam)

Reports are frequently set on one of these scenarios — adapt the structure to fit:

| Topic area | Typical audience | Typical sections |
|------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| School facilities | Headteacher / governors | Findings, Issues, Recommendations |
| Local community issue | Town council / mayor | Background, Current Situation, Proposals |
| Environmental problem | Committee / organisation | Overview, Findings, Analysis, Recommendations |
| Event planning | School council / teacher | Purpose, Proposed Programme, Budget, Recommendations |
| Survey results | Principal / employer | Methodology, Findings, Conclusions, Recommendations |

## 12.8 Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correction |
|---------|-----------|
| ✗ Writing in continuous prose with no headings | ✓ Use clear section headings — they are essential |
| ✗ Using first person (*"I think / I found"*) | ✓ Use impersonal constructions (*"It is recommended / The findings show"*) |
| ✗ Mixing findings and opinions in the same section | ✓ Keep facts (findings) and interpretation (analysis) in separate sections |
| ✗ Vague recommendations (*"things should improve"*) | ✓ Specific, actionable steps with clear agents |
| ✗ Forgetting the header block | ✓ Always include To / From / Date / Subject |
| ✗ Introducing new information in the conclusion | ✓ Conclusion only summarises what has already been said |
| ✗ Informal or emotional language | ✓ Measured, formal, impersonal throughout |
| ✗ No evidence or data in the findings | ✓ Use specific numbers, percentages, or examples |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Report** | A formal, structured document presenting findings and recommendations to a named audience |
| **Findings** | The factual information gathered — survey results, observations, data |
| **Analysis** | The interpretation of findings — what they mean, what problems they reveal |
| **Recommendations** | Specific, actionable suggestions for solving problems identified in the report |
| **Impersonal language** | Writing that avoids *I/we* and uses passive or third-person constructions instead |
| **Passive voice** | A grammatical construction where the subject receives the action: *It is recommended that...* |
| **Header block** | The To / From / Date / Subject section at the top of a report |
| **Subheading** | A secondary heading within a section, used to organise multiple distinct points |
| **Commissioned** | When someone in authority requests a report to be written on a specific topic |
| **Scope** | The range of topics or issues that the report covers — stated in the introduction |
| **Objective tone** | Writing that presents facts without personal emotion or bias |
