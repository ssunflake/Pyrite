## 10.1 Types of Essay

| Type | Purpose | Key features |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Argumentative** | Argue one clear position | Thesis, evidence, counterargument, rebuttal |
| **Discursive** | Explore multiple viewpoints fairly | Balanced, "on the one hand / on the other" |
| **Analytical** | Examine a text or idea in depth | Close reading, quotation, interpretation |
| **Personal / Reflective** | Share experience or opinion | First person, anecdote, thoughtful tone |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Check the task wording carefully. "Argue" means take a side. "Discuss" means present multiple views. Mixing these up is one of the most common mark-losers.

---

## 10.2 Essay Structure — The Five-Part Framework

Every essay, regardless of type, benefits from a clear shape:

| Part | Purpose | Length guide |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Introduction** | Hook + context + thesis (your central argument/claim) | 1 paragraph |
| **Body paragraph 1** | First main point — strongest argument | 1–2 paragraphs |
| **Body paragraph 2** | Second main point | 1–2 paragraphs |
| **Body paragraph 3** | Third main point / counterargument + rebuttal | 1–2 paragraphs |
| **Conclusion** | Restate thesis in new words + final thought | 1 paragraph |

> [!note] Never introduce a brand new argument in the conclusion. It signals poor planning and loses marks for structure.

---

## 10.3 Writing a Strong Introduction

A strong introduction does three things:

1. **Hook** — grabs the reader's attention
2. **Context** — briefly sets up the topic
3. **Thesis** — states your position or the essay's direction

### Six Types of Hook

| Hook type | Example |
|-----------|---------|
| **Bold statement** | *"Social media is not just changing the way we communicate — it is dismantling it."* |
| **Rhetorical question** | *"If technology makes our lives easier, why are we more anxious than ever?"* |
| **Surprising statistic** | *"Over 3.5 billion people use social media daily, yet loneliness is at an all-time high."* |
| **Anecdote** | *"The last time I looked up from my phone at a family dinner, everyone else was looking down at theirs."* |
| **Quotation** | Open with a relevant, well-chosen quote (attributed) |
| **Contrast** | *"Technology promised to connect us. In many ways, it has only succeeded in isolating us."* |

> ⚠️ **Common mistake:** Starting with *"In this essay, I will..."* — this is weak, flat, and wastes your opening. Show, don't announce.

## 10.4 Body Paragraphs — PEEL Structure

Every body paragraph should follow the **PEEL** structure:

| Letter | Element | What to write |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **P** | **Point** | State your argument for this paragraph in one clear sentence |
| **E** | **Evidence** | Support with a fact, quote, example, or statistic |
| **E** | **Explanation** | Explain how/why the evidence proves your point |
| **L** | **Link** | Connect back to the thesis OR lead into the next paragraph |

### Weak vs Strong PEEL Paragraph

> ⚠️ **Weak:** *"Social media is bad. Lots of people use it. It causes problems."*

> 💡 **Strong:** *"Social media erodes meaningful communication by prioritising surface-level interaction over genuine connection. Studies show that the average user scrolls for over two hours daily, yet reports feeling lonelier than non-users. This suggests that quantity of digital contact does not translate into quality of human connection — the very thing social media claims to offer. If we are to reverse this trend, we must first acknowledge what we are sacrificing at the altar of the 'like' button."*

## 10.5 Handling Counterarguments

In argumentative and discursive essays, **acknowledging and refuting the opposing view** strengthens your argument — it shows you have considered the full picture.

**Template structure:**

| Step | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Concede** (acknowledge the other view) | *"It is true that social media has enabled communities to form across vast distances..."* |
| **Counter** (pivot with 'however') | *"...however, this sense of community is frequently superficial, lacking the depth of in-person relationships."* |
| **Reinforce** (restate your position) | *"Ultimately, digital connection cannot replace the neurological and emotional benefits of face-to-face interaction."* |

### Useful Counterargument Phrases

- *While it may be argued that...*
- *Proponents of this view would claim...*
- *It is certainly true that... however...*
- *On the surface, this appears convincing; yet...*
- *One might counter that... nevertheless...*

## 10.6 Writing a Strong Conclusion

A conclusion should:
- **Restate the thesis** — in different words from the introduction
- **Summarise key points** — briefly, without repeating evidence
- **End with impact** — a final thought, call to action, or resonant image

> ⚠️ **Avoid:** Starting with *"In conclusion, I have shown that..."* — this is mechanical. Instead, circle back to your opening hook or leave the reader with something to think about.

### Five Types of Conclusion Ending

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Circle back to hook** | Return to your opening anecdote or image with new meaning |
| **Call to action** | *"The question is not whether we should act, but when."* |
| **Broader implication** | Zoom out — what does this mean for society / the future? |
| **Thought-provoking question** | Leave the reader reflecting, not told what to think |
| **Resonant final image** | A concrete detail or metaphor that lingers |

## 10.7 Essay Vocabulary and Connectives

### Connectives by Function

| Function | Words and phrases |
|----------|------------------|
| **Adding a point** | Furthermore, moreover, in addition, what is more, equally |
| **Contrasting** | However, nevertheless, on the other hand, conversely, despite this |
| **Conceding** | Admittedly, while it is true that, granted, it cannot be denied that |
| **Exemplifying** | For instance, for example, to illustrate, as demonstrated by |
| **Concluding** | Therefore, consequently, thus, in conclusion, ultimately, it follows that |
| **Emphasising** | Indeed, in fact, crucially, significantly, above all |
| **Sequencing** | First and foremost, subsequently, finally, in the first instance |

### Upgrading Basic Vocabulary

| Weak word | Stronger alternatives |
|-----------|----------------------|
| *good* | beneficial, advantageous, constructive, commendable |
| *bad* | detrimental, harmful, damaging, deleterious |
| *says* (in analysis) | argues, contends, asserts, implies, suggests, conveys |
| *shows* | demonstrates, illustrates, reveals, exposes, highlights |
| *important* | crucial, pivotal, significant, integral, fundamental |
| *a lot of* | a considerable number of, a substantial proportion of, the majority of |
| *I think* | It could be argued that, one might contend, evidence suggests |

## 10.8 Register and Tone

Essays should be written in **formal, academic register**:

| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|-------------|
| Contractions (*don't, can't, it's*) | Full forms (*do not, cannot, it is*) |
| Slang or colloquial language | Precise, formal vocabulary |
| First person in argumentative essays (unless personal) | Passive or impersonal constructions |
| Rhetorical padding (*"It is an interesting fact that..."*) | Direct, precise statements |
| Vague generalisations (*"Everyone knows that..."*) | Specific, evidenced claims |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Aim for a tone that is **confident but not arrogant**, **formal but not stiff**. The best essays feel like a thoughtful person talking to an intelligent reader, not a robot reciting points.

## 10.9 Planning — The 5-Minute Map

Never start writing without a plan. In exam conditions, spend approximately 5 minutes:

1. **Identify the task** — argumentative? Discursive? What is the question asking?
2. **Brainstorm** — jot 4–6 ideas quickly, no filtering
3. **Select** — choose your 3 strongest points
4. **Order** — sequence them (strongest last is often most effective)
5. **Note counterargument** — identify one opposing view to address

> 💡 **Tip:** A brief plan stops you going off-topic mid-essay and saves time in the long run — you think once, write once.

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Thesis** | The central argument or claim of an essay — stated in the introduction |
| **PEEL** | Paragraph structure: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link |
| **Counterargument** | The opposing viewpoint — acknowledging and refuting it strengthens your essay |
| **Rebuttal** | Your response to the counterargument, reasserting your position |
| **Discursive essay** | An essay that explores multiple viewpoints without necessarily taking a strong single position |
| **Argumentative essay** | An essay that takes and defends a clear position on a debatable topic |
| **Register** | The level of formality in language — essays require formal academic register |
| **Hook** | The opening sentence or sentences designed to engage the reader immediately |
| **Connective** | A word or phrase that links ideas within or between paragraphs |
| **Cohesion** | The smooth flow of ideas through a piece of writing — achieved via connectives, pronoun reference, and repetition of key terms |
