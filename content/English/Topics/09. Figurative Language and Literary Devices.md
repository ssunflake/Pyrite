## 9.2 Complete Devices Reference — 24 Devices

---

### 1. Simile
**Definition:** A comparison between two things using the words "like" or "as."
**Example:** *"Her anger spread through the room like smoke through a keyhole."*
**Effect:** Makes abstract ideas concrete and imaginable; invites the reader to picture a comparison they already understand, creating an instant visual or emotional response.

---

### 2. Metaphor
**Definition:** A direct comparison that states one thing IS another, without using "like" or "as."
**Example:** *"The classroom was a battlefield — desks overturned, voices clashing, nobody willing to surrender."*
**Effect:** Creates bold, striking imagery; collapses two different ideas into one, forcing the reader to hold both simultaneously. More powerful and direct than a simile.

---

### 3. Extended Metaphor
**Definition:** A metaphor that is sustained and developed across multiple sentences, a whole paragraph, or an entire text.
**Example:** *"Life is a journey. Some roads are smooth and straight; others wind through dark valleys with no map and no signpost. But we must keep walking."*
**Effect:** Creates thematic coherence and depth; allows the writer to explore an idea from multiple angles; rewards the attentive reader who follows the metaphor's development.

---

### 4. Personification
**Definition:** Attributing human qualities, emotions, actions, or intentions to non-human things.
**Example:** *"The old house groaned under the weight of its memories."*
**Effect:** Creates a dynamic relationship between human characters and their environment; makes the natural world feel alive, responsive, or threatening; often used to create atmosphere.

---

### 5. Alliteration
**Definition:** The repetition of the same consonant sound at the beginning of closely positioned words.
**Example:** *"The dark, damp dungeon dripped with despair."*
**Effect:** Creates rhythm and musicality; draws attention to specific words and phrases; the sound itself can create mood — soft sounds (s, l, w) feel gentle; harsh sounds (d, g, k) feel aggressive or harsh.

---

### 6. Assonance
**Definition:** The repetition of similar vowel sounds within nearby words (not necessarily at the start).
**Example:** *"The light of the dying fire filled the night sky with crimson and violet."*
**Effect:** Creates internal rhyme and musicality; can produce a melancholic, dreamlike, or hypnotic quality; often used to slow the reading pace and create a meditative mood.

---

### 7. Onomatopoeia
**Definition:** A word that phonetically imitates or suggests the sound it describes.
**Example:** *"The bacon sizzled. The oil spat. The kettle screamed."*
**Effect:** Creates immediacy and sensory immersion; puts the reader directly inside the scene by engaging their sense of hearing; can be comic, alarming, or soothing depending on the sounds chosen.

---

### 8. Hyperbole
**Definition:** Deliberate, exaggerated overstatement used for emphasis or effect.
**Example:** *"I have told you a thousand times — please stop leaving the front door open."*
**Effect:** Creates emphasis, humour, or emotional intensity; shows the scale of a feeling (frustration, love, exhaustion) in a way that literal language cannot capture. The reader knows the exaggeration is intentional, which creates a shared understanding.

---

### 9. Understatement / Litotes
**Definition:** Deliberately playing down or minimising something significant. Litotes specifically uses a negative to express a positive ("not bad" = good).
**Example (understatement):** *"The Titanic had a rather unfortunate voyage."*
**Example (litotes):** *"It was not his finest hour."* (after a catastrophic failure)
**Effect:** Creates irony and dark humour; the gap between the understatement and the reality it describes creates a powerful effect — often chilling or wryly comic; associated with British restraint.

---

### 10. Verbal Irony
**Definition:** Saying the opposite of what is genuinely meant; often sarcastic.
**Example:** *"What spectacular timing," she said, watching him arrive two hours late."*
**Effect:** Creates humour, bitterness, or critical distance; implies the speaker's real feelings without stating them; invites the reader to share in the ironic perspective.

---

### 11. Situational Irony
**Definition:** When events turn out to be the opposite of what was expected or what would seem appropriate.
**Example:** A fire station burning down; a safety expert who is injured in an accident; a lifeguard who cannot swim.
**Effect:** Creates a sense of the absurd or the tragic; often used to comment on fate, coincidence, or the systems and ironies of society; the reader feels both the shock of the unexpected and the dark logic of it.

---

### 12. Dramatic Irony
**Definition:** When the audience or reader knows something that one or more of the characters does not.
**Example:** The reader knows that the letter was never delivered, but the character still believes her friend received it and chose not to reply.
**Effect:** Creates tension, dread, or sympathy; keeps the reader emotionally invested, often shouting (metaphorically) at the character; used to explore the vulnerability of ignorance and the cruelty of misunderstanding.

---

### 13. Sarcasm
**Definition:** A sharp, biting form of irony used to mock, criticise, or express contempt.
**Example:** *"Oh, brilliant. Another meeting that could have been an email — just what Monday morning needed."*
**Effect:** Conveys contempt, frustration, or sharp criticism; creates a biting, often darkly comic tone; tells the reader exactly how the speaker feels about a situation without stating it sincerely.

---

### 14. Pathetic Fallacy
**Definition:** A form of personification where the natural world — particularly weather and environment — is used to reflect the emotional state of characters.
**Example:** *"As she read the letter, a storm broke overhead — thunder rolling like distant grief."*
**Effect:** Creates atmosphere and foreshadows events; makes the environment feel responsive to human emotion; heightens dramatic tension by making the physical world echo the internal world.

---

### 15. Foreshadowing
**Definition:** Hints, clues, or signals placed early in a text that suggest what will happen later.
**Example:** *"The day seemed too perfect. She should have known."* OR a character mentioning in chapter one that they fear fire, shortly before a fire proves crucial to the plot.
**Effect:** Creates suspense and a sense of fate; makes the reader feel unsettled without knowing why; rewards attentive re-reading; when the foreshadowed event occurs, the reader feels a sense of inevitability.

---

### 16. Juxtaposition
**Definition:** Placing two contrasting ideas, images, characters, or situations directly alongside each other to highlight their differences.
**Example:** *"In a city of gold towers and endless ambition, she slept on a piece of cardboard under the bridge."*
**Effect:** Creates stark contrast that provokes emotional or moral response; highlights inequality, paradox, or contradiction; forces the reader to hold two opposite realities simultaneously and feel the tension between them.

---

### 17. Anaphora
**Definition:** The repetition of the same word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses, sentences, or lines.
**Example:** *"We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets, we shall fight in the hills."* (Churchill)
**Effect:** Builds momentum and power with each repetition; drills a message into the reader or listener; creates a rallying, incantatory effect; associated with political and religious rhetoric.

---

### 18. Epistrophe
**Definition:** The repetition of the same word or phrase at the END of successive clauses or sentences (the reverse of anaphora).
**Example:** *"...government of the people, by the people, for the people."* (Lincoln)
**Effect:** Creates emphasis and a strong sense of closure; draws the reader's attention to the repeated concept; creates a circular, resonant rhythm that lingers.

---

### 19. Parallelism
**Definition:** The use of the same grammatical structure in successive phrases, clauses, or sentences.
**Example:** *"She came, she saw, she conquered."* / *"To read is to escape; to write is to create; to think is to be free."*
**Effect:** Creates balance, rhythm, and elegance; makes writing feel structured, controlled, and authoritative; often used in speeches and arguments to create memorable, quotable phrases.

---

### 20. Symbolism
**Definition:** Using a concrete object, place, character, colour, or event to represent an abstract idea, theme, or emotion.
**Example:** A white dove symbolising peace; a locked door symbolising missed opportunity or entrapment; a wilting rose symbolising the end of love.
**Effect:** Adds layers of meaning beneath the surface of the text; allows writers to communicate complex ideas without stating them directly; rewards careful, attentive reading; symbols often become the thematic heart of a text.

---

### 21. Euphemism
**Definition:** A mild, indirect, or less explicit expression used in place of one that might be considered harsh, blunt, or taboo.
**Example:** "He passed away" instead of "he died"; "collateral damage" instead of "civilian casualties"; "letting you go" instead of "firing you."
**Effect:** Softens the impact of difficult subjects; can reveal much about social attitudes to death, war, or failure; in analysis, note when writers use euphemism to expose how society conceals uncomfortable realities — the gap between the euphemism and its reality is itself meaningful.

---

### 22. Oxymoron
**Definition:** Combining two apparently contradictory or opposite terms in a single phrase.
**Examples:** "deafening silence," "bittersweet," "living death," "open secret," "act naturally," "cruel kindness"
**Effect:** Captures paradox or contradiction; forces the reader to hold two opposing ideas simultaneously; creates a sense of complexity and depth; suggests that truth is not always simple or one-sided.

---

### 23. Synecdoche
**Definition:** A figure of speech where a part is used to represent the whole, or the whole is used to represent a part.
**Example:** *"All hands on deck"* (hands = the sailors as people); *"The Crown will not tolerate this"* (Crown = the monarchy); *"She's got wheels"* (wheels = a car).
**Effect:** Creates economical, vivid imagery; can humanise (focusing on one specific part) or dehumanise (reducing a person to a single function); "hands" in "hands on deck" strips workers of their full humanity.

---

### 24. Imagery (Five Types)

Imagery refers to any language that creates a sensory impression in the reader's mind. It is not just about sight — all five senses can be engaged.

| Type | Sense Evoked | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Visual imagery** | Sight — colour, light, shape, movement | "Crimson streaks bled across the pale morning sky." |
| **Auditory imagery** | Sound — volume, pitch, rhythm, texture | "The distant thrum of traffic blurred into a low, constant roar." |
| **Olfactory imagery** | Smell — pleasant, unpleasant, nostalgic | "The air carried the sweet rot of overripe plums baking in the August sun." |
| **Tactile imagery** | Touch — texture, temperature, pressure, pain | "The rough rasp of old rope against her palms left red tracks she couldn't ignore." |
| **Gustatory imagery** | Taste — flavour, bitterness, sweetness | "The sharp, metallic taste of fear settled on her tongue and would not leave." |

## 9.3 Connotation vs Denotation — Revisited in Depth

| Term | Definition | How to Identify It |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Denotation** | The literal, dictionary meaning of a word | Ask: what does this word mean in simple, factual terms? |
| **Connotation** | The emotional, cultural, and associative meanings that a word carries | Ask: what does this word make people FEEL? What ideas does it bring to mind? |

### Worked Analysis Example:

**Word in question: "crumbled"** (used to describe an empire's fall)

> The writer uses "crumbled" rather than "fell" to describe the empire's end. While both words denote the same basic action of coming apart, "crumbled" carries connotations of gradual decay, weakness, and fragility — like a piece of dry bread disintegrating under slight pressure. This implies that the empire's collapse was not sudden or dramatic but long-coming, inevitable, and the result of internal rot rather than external force. The connotation transforms a historical event into a slow tragedy.

### Common Connotation Contrasts to Know:

| Word A | Word B | What's Different? |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| "thin" | "slender" | Both denote low body weight; "slender" connotes elegance, "thin" connotes frailty or poverty |
| "home" | "house" | Both denote a dwelling; "home" connotes warmth, family, belonging |
| "determined" | "stubborn" | Both denote persistence; "determined" is positive, "stubborn" is negative |
| "challenged" | "failed" | Both describe difficulty; "challenged" is a euphemistic softening |
| "assertive" | "aggressive" | Both denote forcefulness; but gendered and contextual connotations differ significantly |

## 9.4 Register — Formal, Semi-Formal, Informal, Colloquial, Slang

Register refers to the level of formality and style in a piece of writing or speech. Writers choose their register deliberately to suit their audience and purpose.

| Register | Definition | When Used | Characteristics | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|----------------|---------|
| **Formal** | Precise, sophisticated, impersonal language | Academic essays, formal letters, professional reports | Complex syntax, no contractions, technical vocabulary, third person | "The evidence suggests a strong correlation between the two variables." |
| **Semi-formal** | Professional but accessible; balanced | Quality journalism, magazine articles, professional emails | Clear syntax, some contractions acceptable, accessible vocabulary | "The data points to a clear trend — and it's one we can't afford to ignore." |
| **Informal** | Conversational, personal, relaxed | Personal letters, blogs, friendly emails, diary entries | Contractions common, first person, simple vocabulary, personal tone | "Honestly, I couldn't believe what happened when I got there." |
| **Colloquial** | Everyday spoken patterns and expressions; informal idioms | Dialogue in fiction, informal writing | Idioms, colloquial phrases, relaxed grammar | "It was a bit of a disaster, to be fair. You know what he's like." |
| **Slang** | Non-standard vocabulary used by specific social or age groups | Character voice in fiction, stylised dialogue | Slang terms, abbreviated words, highly group-specific | "That's well dodgy. Bare sketchy, innit." |

## 9.5 How to Identify and Analyse a Device — Full Exam Walkthrough

The most effective structure for a language analysis paragraph at IGCSE is **MQEP:**

```
METHOD    →  Identify and NAME the specific technique
QUOTATION →  Quote the EXACT words from the text
EFFECT    →  Explain the effect on the reader in precise detail
PURPOSE   →  Link to the writer's overall purpose or the text's themes
```

### Worked Exam Example — Full Band 5 Response

**Question:** How does the writer use language to create a threatening atmosphere? [5]

**Source text extract:** *"The sky pressed flat and grey above the house, as though the world above was holding its breath. The trees had stopped moving. Even the birds were gone."*

---

> The writer employs a combination of pathetic fallacy and personification to create a pervasive sense of threat and dread. The simile describing the sky as pressing "flat and grey... as though the world above was holding its breath" uses the image of something breathing — and pausing — to suggest a moment of terrible anticipation. The personification of the sky "holding its breath" implies that nature itself is aware of what is coming, and is bracing for it. This creates a deeply unsettling effect in the reader, as though the entire world is paused on the edge of something violent. The subsequent short sentences — "The trees had stopped moving. Even the birds were gone." — use contrast and the absence of sound to amplify the silence: the world feels not peaceful, but predatory. The writer's overall purpose is to establish psychological dread before any explicit threat appears, making the horror feel inevitable rather than sudden.

---

**Breakdown:**
- **M** → Pathetic fallacy, personification, simile, short sentences, contrast
- **Q** → "flat and grey... as though the world above was holding its breath" / "The trees had stopped moving. Even the birds were gone."
- **E** → Psychological anticipation, predatory silence, amplified dread
- **P** → "Making the horror feel inevitable rather than sudden" — connects to the writer's craft and purpose
