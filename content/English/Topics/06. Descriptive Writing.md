## 6.1 Purpose of Descriptive Writing

The purpose of descriptive writing is to **create a vivid, immersive picture in the reader's mind using carefully chosen language**. Unlike narrative writing, descriptive writing does not need to tell a story — it can simply capture a moment, a place, a person, an object, or an experience in rich, sensory detail.

**Key goals of descriptive writing:**
- Engage the reader's senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, taste)
- Create a specific, sustained mood or atmosphere
- Show rather than tell — demonstrate rather than state
- Use language precisely and inventively
- Leave the reader with a strong, clear impression

> [!note] Descriptive writing is NOT a story. It does not need characters, plot, or conflict. A description of an empty room, a market at dawn, or a moment just before a storm can all be excellent descriptive writing.

---

## 6.2 The Five Senses as a Framework

Organising your description around the five senses ensures rich, varied, and immersive detail.

| Sense | What to Describe | Example Phrases |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Sight** | Colours, shapes, light quality, movement, contrast, scale | "The amber glow of the streetlamp pooled in shallow circles on the wet cobblestones." |
| **Sound** | Volume, pitch, rhythm, texture, presence or absence of sound | "A low, rhythmic clang echoed from somewhere deep within the building." |
| **Smell** | Pleasant, unpleasant, unexpected, nostalgic, sharp, sweet | "The air carried the sharp, briny tang of the sea mixed with engine oil and rust." |
| **Touch** | Temperature, texture, pressure, weight, pain, comfort | "The bark was cold and rough under her fingertips, furred with damp moss." |
| **Taste** | Flavour, bitterness, sweetness, sensation on the tongue | "The salt of the sea air settled on her lips; she could taste the distance she had travelled." |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** You do not need to use all five senses in every piece. Choose the senses most powerful for your subject. Forced use of all five can feel mechanical and tick-boxy. Aim for three or four senses used naturally.

## 6.3 Structural Approaches — How to Organise a Description

### Zoom Structure (Panoramic → Close-Up)

Start with a wide, sweeping view of the whole scene and gradually narrow focus to a single, specific detail. This mimics a camera zooming in.

```
WIDE SHOT     →  The whole landscape, city, or scene seen from a distance
     ↓
MEDIUM SHOT   →  A section of the scene (a single building, a corner of the market)
     ↓
CLOSE-UP      →  One specific, telling detail (a cracked window, a pair of worn hands)
```

**Example:**
> *The bay stretched for miles in both directions, cliffs rising at either end like the arms of an embrace. Halfway along the curve of sand, a small fishing boat sat tilted on its side in the exposed mud. Beside the boat's hull, a child's red boot, half buried.*

---

### Temporal Structure (Time-Based)

Describe how a place or scene changes over the course of time. This gives your description an arc and allows you to show contrasts.

- Dawn → Midday → Dusk → Night
- Before → During → After
- Past → Present (nostalgic contrast)

---

### Spatial Structure (Movement Through a Place)

Move the reader physically through a space, describing what they encounter as they travel. This creates a sense of journey and progression.

- Through a door, down a corridor, into a room
- Across a landscape, arriving at a destination
- Around the edges of a space, working towards the centre

## 6.4 Imagery — Simile, Metaphor, Personification, Pathetic Fallacy

These four devices are the most commonly used in descriptive writing and the most frequently discussed in analysis.

| Device | Definition | Strong Descriptive Example |
|--------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Simile** | Comparison using 'like' or 'as' | "The fog moved through the streets like a living thing, slow and deliberate." |
| **Metaphor** | Direct comparison; one thing IS another | "The city was a machine that never stopped grinding." |
| **Extended metaphor** | A metaphor developed across multiple sentences | Describing an entire seaside town as a theatre — stage, players, audience — throughout a paragraph |
| **Personification** | Giving the environment human qualities | "The wind whispered secrets through the long grass, impatient and insistent." |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Environment reflecting human emotion | Describing heavy, grey skies and oppressive heat to mirror a character's grief or anxiety |

### Imagery — Writing Specific, Not Generic:

| Generic Image | Specific, Vivid Image |
|--------------|----------------------|
| "The sky was beautiful." | "The sky was streaked in copper and rose, as if someone had dragged a lit match across it." |
| "The room was messy." | "Books had given up on shelves entirely and lay in soft piles on the floor, their spines bent and familiar." |
| "The sea was rough." | "The waves arrived not in sequences but in crowds, jostling and shoving, each one undercutting the last." |

## 6.5 Sensory Language Choices and Their Effects

Every adjective and descriptive word should be chosen deliberately. Vague words waste an opportunity.

| Weak / Vague | Strong Alternatives | Sense Engaged |
|-------------|-------------------|--------------|
| "big" | towering, looming, vast, hulking, colossal | Sight |
| "dark" | ink-black, charcoal, sunless, shrouded, pitch | Sight |
| "loud" | thunderous, ear-splitting, jarring, percussive | Sound |
| "quiet" | hushed, muffled, library-still, breathless | Sound |
| "old" | weathered, time-worn, crumbling, fossilised | Sight / Touch |
| "nice smell" | honey-sweet, heady, floral, warm and bakery-rich | Smell |
| "bad smell" | acrid, fetid, sour, eye-watering, sulphurous | Smell |
| "rough" | sandpaper-rough, abrasive, pitted, bark-like | Touch |
| "cold" | biting, marrow-deep, glacial, raw, numbing | Touch |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** Replace every use of "nice," "big," "pretty," "scary," and "good" in your descriptive writing. These are placeholder words — a sign that you haven't yet chosen the right word. Strong descriptive writing uses the *precise* word.

## 6.6 Creating Mood and Atmosphere

Mood and atmosphere are not created by one single technique but by the **accumulation** of many deliberate choices working together.

| Element | Gloomy / Threatening Mood | Peaceful / Positive Mood |
|---------|--------------------------|------------------------|
| **Colours** | Grey, black, murky, dull, leaden, ashen | Golden, warm, soft, vibrant, luminous |
| **Sound words** | Creaking, groaning, scraping, hissing, silence | Birdsong, rustling, gentle lapping, murmuring |
| **Movement** | Lurching, swaying, flickering, skulking | Drifting, gliding, settling, flowing |
| **Temperature** | Bitter, freezing, raw, biting, chilled | Warm, gentle, soft, honeyed |
| **Sentence length** | Short, clipped sentences (tension, unease) | Long, flowing sentences (calm, reflection) |
| **Punctuation** | Dashes, ellipses, fragments (disruption) | Long, carefully balanced sentences (order) |

### The Accumulation Principle:

No single technique creates atmosphere alone. Read this example and notice how multiple choices work together:

> *The house had not been opened in years. The hall smelled of something long finished — old wood and damp paper and the ghost of cooking. Light leaked through the curtains in thin yellow lines, barely enough to see by. Somewhere above, a board shifted. Then settled. The silence after it was worse.*

Notice: pathetic fallacy in the light, sensory imagery (smell, sound), short sentences to create unease, personification of "the ghost of cooking," and the use of a sentence fragment ("Then settled") for maximum impact.

## 6.7 The Role of Adjectives and Adverbs

Use adjectives and adverbs deliberately and sparingly.

### Quality Over Quantity:

**Too many adjectives (weak):**
> *"The big, dark, scary, old, crumbling, gloomy, cold stone house."*

**Precise noun + one strong adjective (strong):**
> *"The house crouched at the end of the lane, exhaling cold."*

### Strong Verbs Beat Weak Verbs + Adverbs:

| Weak (verb + adverb) | Strong (precise verb) |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "walked slowly and tiredly" | "trudged," "plodded," "shuffled" |
| "said loudly" | "bellowed," "shouted," "roared" |
| "looked quickly" | "glanced," "darted," "flicked" |
| "moved quietly" | "slipped," "crept," "stole" |
| "ran fast" | "sprinted," "bolted," "hurtled" |

## 6.8 Varied Sentence Structure in Descriptive Writing

Variety in sentence structure prevents monotony and creates rhythm. Skilled descriptive writing uses all types.

| Structure | Example | Effect |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Long, flowing sentence** | "The afternoon light slanted through the high windows, painting soft gold coins across the floor, shifting gently with every movement of the trees outside." | Immersive, calm, detailed, reflective |
| **Short, sharp sentence** | "Nothing moved." / "She stopped." | Dramatic; draws maximum attention; contrast with long sentences |
| **Fragment sentence** | "Silence. Then footsteps. Then nothing again." | Creates tension; staccato, unsettling rhythm |
| **Sentence beginning with a verb** | "Rising above the valley, the cathedral seemed less like a building than a declaration." | Active; dynamic; immediate |
| **Sentence beginning with an adverbial** | "In the far distance, a tower rose above the mist." | Establishes spatial context; sets the scene |
| **Sentence with a parenthetical dash** | "The garden — or what had once been a garden — had given itself back to the wild." | Adds ironic or reflective aside |

## 6.9 Static vs Dynamic Description

| Type | Description | When to Use |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Static description** | Describes a still, frozen scene — like a photograph | Opening a description; establishing a setting before action begins; a moment of calm |
| **Dynamic description** | Describes movement, change, and action within a scene | Describing weather, crowds, a moment of drama, or the passing of time |

The most compelling descriptive writing blends both. A static scene interrupted by a single dynamic moment creates powerful contrast.

**Example — static then dynamic:**
> *The square was empty in the early morning, its fountain still and silent. Then a single pigeon landed on the rim — ruffled itself, dipped its head, and drank.*

---

## 6.10 Descriptive Writing vs Narrative Writing — Key Differences

| Feature | Descriptive Writing | Narrative Writing |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Primary focus** | A place, moment, person, or experience | A sequence of events over time |
| **Time** | Often a single moment, frozen or slowly unfolding | Moves through time; has a beginning, middle, and end |
| **Plot** | Not required | Central to the piece |
| **Characters** | Optional; if present, secondary to setting/impression | Central; must be developed and motivated |
| **Conflict** | Not required | Usually present |
| **Purpose** | To create an impression in the reader's mind | To tell a compelling story |

## 6.11 Common IGCSE Descriptive Prompts

| Prompt Type | Example | Strategy |
|------------|---------|---------|
| **Picture-based** | "Look at the photograph. Describe what you see and sense." | Use the image as a launching pad; go beyond what is visible — imagine the sounds, smells, what happened before, what will happen next |
| **Title-based** | "Write a description suggested by the title 'The Waiting Room'" | Interpret the title creatively; plan your central mood and imagery before writing a single word |
| **Scenario-based** | "Describe a busy street market" | Choose a specific angle (early morning; a single stall; the moment before closing) rather than trying to describe everything at once |

---

## 6.12 Structure Checklist — Before You Submit

- [ ] Engaging opening sentence that establishes setting and mood immediately
- [ ] At least three different senses engaged throughout the piece
- [ ] At least three different imagery devices used (simile, metaphor, personification)
- [ ] Varied sentence lengths and structures throughout
- [ ] Specific, precise word choices — no generic adjectives ("nice," "big," "scary")
- [ ] A clear, central mood maintained from start to finish
- [ ] A satisfying final line that rounds off the description
- [ ] Paragraphs organised using a clear structural approach (zoom / temporal / spatial)
- [ ] No unnecessary plot or story — this is description, not narrative

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Descriptive writing** | Writing that creates a vivid, sensory impression of a place, person, or experience |
| **Imagery** | Language that creates a mental picture by appealing to the five senses |
| **Simile** | A comparison using 'like' or 'as' |
| **Metaphor** | A direct comparison — one thing stated to BE another |
| **Extended metaphor** | A metaphor sustained and developed across multiple sentences |
| **Personification** | Giving human characteristics or emotions to non-human things |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | Using weather or environment to reflect the mood or emotions of characters |
| **Sensory language** | Words and phrases that appeal to the five senses |
| **Static description** | Description of a still, unchanging scene |
| **Dynamic description** | Description involving movement, action, or change |
| **Atmosphere** | The overall mood or feeling created by the writing |
| **Zoom structure** | A structural approach moving from wide panoramic view to close-up detail |
| **Connotation** | The emotional and cultural associations of a word beyond its literal meaning |
| **Pathetic fallacy** | The technique of attributing human emotions to natural phenomena or settings |
