## 8.1 Word Classes — Complete Reference Table

Every word in the English language belongs to at least one word class. Knowing these is fundamental to grammar and language analysis.

| Word Class | Definition | Examples |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Noun** | A naming word — a person, place, thing, or idea | dog, London, freedom, idea, happiness |
| **Verb** | A word expressing an action, state, or occurrence | run, is, feel, become, consider, whisper |
| **Adjective** | A word that describes or modifies a noun | blue, tall, terrifying, ancient, precise |
| **Adverb** | A word that modifies a verb, adjective, or another adverb | quickly, very, never, boldly, surprisingly |
| **Pronoun** | A word that replaces a noun | he, she, they, it, we, I, you, which, who |
| **Preposition** | A word showing the relationship between a noun and something else | in, on, under, between, beside, despite, through |
| **Conjunction** | A word that joins words, phrases, or clauses | and, but, because, although, while, so |
| **Determiner** | A word that specifies or quantifies a noun | the, a, an, some, every, my, this, those, several |
| **Interjection** | An exclamatory word or phrase expressing emotion | Oh! Alas! Well! Indeed! Goodness! Ouch! |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** When analysing language in Paper 1, naming the word class as part of your technique identification earns marks. E.g., "The writer uses the abstract noun 'freedom' to suggest..." shows greater precision than simply "the word 'freedom'."

## 8.2 Noun Types — Four Categories

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Proper noun** | Names a specific, unique person, place, brand, or thing — always capitalised | London, Shakespeare, Tuesday, Coca-Cola, Amazon, Ms Brown |
| **Common noun** | A general name for any person, place, or thing | city, writer, day, drink, school, building |
| **Abstract noun** | Names an idea, feeling, quality, concept, or state that cannot be physically touched | freedom, happiness, justice, fear, love, courage, identity |
| **Collective noun** | Names a group of people, animals, or things | a flock of birds, a pack of wolves, a committee of members, a fleet of ships |

### Abstract Nouns in Analysis:
Writers often use abstract nouns deliberately. The choice between "happiness" and "joy," or between "fear" and "terror," reflects careful consideration of degree and connotation. Identifying and analysing abstract nouns is a higher-level analytical skill.

## 8.3 Verb Types — Four Categories

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Action verb (dynamic verb)** | Describes a physical or mental action | run, think, write, shout, consider, observe |
| **Linking (stative) verb** | Connects the subject to a description or state of being | is, are, was, seem, become, appear, feel, remain |
| **Auxiliary verb** | Helps the main verb form tenses, questions, or negatives | have, has, had, do, does, did, will, shall, be |
| **Modal verb** | Expresses degree of possibility, necessity, permission, or ability | can, could, may, might, must, should, would, ought to |

### Modal Verbs and What They Express:

| Modal Verb | Expression | Example |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| **Must** | Strong obligation or certainty | "We must act now." |
| **Should** | Recommendation or expected behaviour | "Schools should provide more support." |
| **Could / Can** | Ability or possibility | "This could change everything." |
| **Might / May** | Uncertainty or weaker possibility | "It might be too late." |
| **Would** | Conditional or habitual past | "She would always arrive early." |

## 8.4 Conjunction Types

### Coordinating Conjunctions — FANBOYS

These join two independent clauses of equal grammatical status:

| Letter | Conjunction | Function | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|---------|
| **F** | For | Reason or explanation | "She left, for she could not stay." |
| **A** | And | Addition | "He shouted, and she ran." |
| **N** | Nor | Negative alternative | "He could not rest, nor could he eat." |
| **B** | But | Contrast | "She tried her best, but it was not enough." |
| **O** | Or | Alternative | "You comply, or you leave." |
| **Y** | Yet | Contrast (despite expectations) | "It was simple, yet it meant everything." |
| **S** | So | Result or consequence | "The door was locked, so she climbed in through the window." |

---

### Subordinating Conjunctions

These introduce a subordinate clause and show the relationship between it and the main clause:

| Type | Subordinating Conjunctions |
|------|--------------------------|
| **Cause / Reason** | because, since, as |
| **Time** | when, while, before, after, until, once, as soon as |
| **Condition** | if, unless, provided that, as long as |
| **Contrast** | although, though, even though, whereas, while |
| **Purpose** | so that, in order that |

---

### Correlative Conjunctions

These come in pairs and work together to join equivalent elements:

| Pair | Example |
|------|---------|
| Either...or | "Either we leave now, or we miss the train." |
| Neither...nor | "She was neither angry nor surprised." |
| Both...and | "Both the content and the language were excellent." |
| Not only...but also | "He was not only late, but also unprepared." |

## 8.5 Sentence Types — Full Reference

| Type | Definition | Example | Effect |
|------|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Simple** | One main clause — subject + verb (+ object/complement) | "She ran." / "The building collapsed." | Directness, clarity, shock, finality |
| **Compound** | Two or more main clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction | "She ran, and he followed." | Balance, progression, connection between equal ideas |
| **Complex** | One main clause + one or more subordinate clauses | "Although she was terrified, she pressed on." | Nuance, cause-effect, additional context |
| **Compound-complex** | Two+ main clauses + one+ subordinate clause | "Although she had not slept, she refused to stop, and her determination sustained her." | Multi-layered, sophisticated relationships between ideas |

### Minor (Fragment) Sentences

A minor or fragment sentence deliberately omits the full subject-verb structure for effect.

**Example:** *"Nothing. Not a sound. Not a breath."*
**Effect:** Creates urgency, shock, or staccato emphasis. Highly effective at dramatic moments.

> [!note] In analytical and argumentative writing, accidental sentence fragments are errors. In creative writing, deliberate fragments are a stylistic choice. The difference is conscious control.

## 8.6 Clause Types

| Clause Type | Definition | Example |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| **Main clause** | A clause that can stand alone as a complete, grammatically correct sentence | "She walked slowly down the corridor." |
| **Subordinate clause** | A clause that cannot stand alone — depends on the main clause for its meaning | "...although she was exhausted" / "...because the lights had failed" |
| **Relative clause** | A type of subordinate clause that gives more information about a noun; introduced by who, which, that, whose, where | "The letter, which had been unopened for years, contained nothing she had expected." |

### Restrictive vs Non-Restrictive Relative Clauses:

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Restrictive** | Essential to the meaning — if removed, the sentence would be unclear | "The student that studied hardest scored the highest." |
| **Non-restrictive** | Extra information — can be removed without changing the core meaning; uses commas | "The student, who had studied all night, scored the highest." |

## 8.7 Punctuation — Full Reference Table (Effects and Uses)

| Punctuation Mark | Name | Primary Use | Effect / Analytical Note |
|-----------------|------|------------|--------------------------|
| **.** | Full stop | Ends a declarative sentence | Creates finality; very short sentences ending with full stops create abruptness or shock |
| **,** | Comma | Separates clauses, list items, or introductory phrases | Controls pace; creates a natural pause; listing commas accumulate detail |
| **;** | Semicolon | Links two closely related independent clauses | Creates balance and connection; used for sophisticated, balanced statements |
| **:** | Colon | Introduces a list, explanation, quotation, or elaboration | Creates anticipation; announces that something important follows |
| **—** | Dash (em dash) | Parenthetical aside or dramatic pause | Creates emphasis, urgency, or a sudden digression; less formal than a colon |
| **–** | En dash | Shows a range or connection | "pages 10–20"; "the London–Paris train" |
| **...** | Ellipsis | Indicates omitted words, or trailing off | Suggests hesitation, suspense, an unfinished thought, or trailing off |
| **!** | Exclamation mark | Strong emotion or emphasis | Excitement, shock, anger, or irony; overuse diminishes its impact |
| **?** | Question mark | Ends a direct question | Direct question = genuine; rhetorical question = engages reader without expecting a response |
| **( )** | Parentheses | Encloses non-essential, aside information | Creates a conversational tone; adds context without interrupting the main sentence |
| **'** | Apostrophe | Omission (it's = it is) or possession (the cat's bowl) | Contractions signal informal register; possession signals precise detail |
| **" "** | Quotation marks | Speech, quotations from other texts, titles | Marks direct speech; can create ironic distance ("so-called experts") |
| **-** | Hyphen | Joins compound words or modifiers | Creates compound adjectives (well-known, fast-paced); prevents ambiguity |

## 8.8 Active vs Passive Voice

### Definitions and Examples:

**Active voice:** The subject performs the action.
> *"The committee rejected the proposal."*
> Subject (committee) → verb (rejected) → object (proposal)

**Passive voice:** The subject receives the action. Often uses "by" to introduce the agent.
> *"The proposal was rejected by the committee."*
> Subject (proposal) → passive verb (was rejected) → agent (by the committee)

The agent can even be omitted entirely in the passive:
> *"The proposal was rejected."* — We do not know by whom.

---

### When to Use Each Voice:

| Feature | Active Voice | Passive Voice |
|---------|-------------|--------------| 
| **Tone** | Direct, energetic, personal | Formal, detached, impersonal |
| **Focus** | On the actor — who is doing something | On the action or recipient — what is being done |
| **Clarity** | Clearer — responsibility is explicit | Can be vague — hides who is responsible |
| **Use in creative writing** | Preferred — creates pace and directness | Occasionally for variety or effect |
| **Use in reports** | Sometimes used | Strongly preferred ("It was found that...") |
| **Use in persuasion** | Preferred — direct and accountable | Sometimes used to sound authoritative |

> 💡 **Exam tip:** In language analysis, note when a writer uses passive voice to create distance or to deliberately obscure who is responsible for something. E.g., "Mistakes were made" vs "We made mistakes." This is often politically or rhetorically significant.

## 8.9 Common Grammar Errors — Identification and Correction

| Error | Definition | Incorrect Example | Corrected Example |
|-------|-----------|------------------|------------------|
| **Comma splice** | Two independent clauses joined with only a comma (incorrect) | "She was tired, she went to bed." | "She was tired; she went to bed." OR "She was tired, so she went to bed." |
| **Run-on sentence** | Two or more independent clauses with no punctuation between them | "She was tired she went to bed immediately." | Add a full stop, semicolon, or conjunction |
| **Sentence fragment** | An incomplete sentence lacking a main clause | "Running through the park at night." | "She was running through the park at night." |
| **Dangling modifier** | A modifying phrase that does not logically attach to the subject | "Walking to school, the rain started." | "Walking to school, she noticed the rain had started." |
| **Misplaced apostrophe** | Apostrophe incorrectly used for a plural (not possession) | "apple's for sale" / "the 1990's" | "apples for sale" / "the 1990s" |
| **Subject-verb disagreement** | Subject and verb do not agree in number | "The group of students are studying." | "The group of students is studying." |
| **Double negative** | Using two negatives in one clause (creates a positive meaning in standard English) | "I don't have no money." | "I don't have any money." |
| **Misplaced modifier** | A modifier placed too far from the word it describes, causing confusion | "She almost drove her children to school every day." | "She drove her children to school almost every day." |

## 8.10 Tense Consistency

Choose a tense before you begin writing and maintain it throughout. Accidental tense shifts are one of the most common errors in creative writing.

| Tense | When to Use | Example |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **Past tense** | Most narrative writing; retelling events | "She walked into the room and stopped." |
| **Present tense** | Creates immediacy; some contemporary narratives; analysis writing | "She walks into the room and stops." |
| **Future tense** | Predictions; persuasive conclusions; speculative writing | "We will face the consequences of our inaction." |

### Common Tense Consistency Errors:

**Incorrect (tense shift mid-paragraph):**
> *"She walked to the door. She opens it slowly. She looked out into the darkness."*

**Corrected:**
> *"She walked to the door. She opened it slowly. She looked out into the darkness."*

> [!note] In analysis and essay writing, always use the PRESENT TENSE when describing what a writer does in a text. ("The writer uses metaphor to..." NOT "The writer used metaphor to...")

## 8.11 Paragraphing Rules — TIPTOP

A new paragraph is required whenever one of the following changes occurs:

| Letter | Trigger for New Paragraph | Example |
|--------|--------------------------|---------|
| **T** | **Time** changes | Moving from morning to evening in a narrative |
| **I** | **Idea** changes | Moving from one argument to another in an essay |
| **P** | **Place** changes | The scene shifts location in a story |
| **T** | **Topic** changes | A new aspect of the subject is introduced |
| **O** | **Opinion/viewpoint** changes | A shift to the counter-argument |
| **P** | **Person/character** changes | Focus shifts to a different character |

### Paragraph Length Guidelines:

| Writing Type | Typical Paragraph Length |
|-------------|-------------------------|
| Argumentative / analytical essay | 4–8 sentences |
| Narrative / descriptive writing | Variable — can be 1 sentence for dramatic effect |
| Summary | Single connected paragraph |
| Report | Each section is its own paragraph/block |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Clause** | A unit of language containing a subject and a finite verb |
| **Main clause** | A clause that is grammatically complete and can stand alone |
| **Subordinate clause** | A clause that depends on a main clause for its meaning |
| **Relative clause** | A subordinate clause giving more information about a noun |
| **Coordinating conjunction** | A conjunction joining two grammatically equal clauses (FANBOYS) |
| **Subordinating conjunction** | A conjunction introducing a subordinate clause |
| **Active voice** | The subject of the sentence performs the action |
| **Passive voice** | The subject of the sentence receives the action |
| **Comma splice** | Incorrectly joining two independent clauses with only a comma |
| **Sentence fragment** | An incomplete sentence lacking a main clause |
| **Tense** | The form of a verb showing when an action takes place |
| **Modal verb** | A verb expressing possibility, necessity, permission, or ability |
| **Determiner** | A word that specifies or quantifies a noun |
| **Abstract noun** | A noun naming an idea, feeling, or concept |
| **Collective noun** | A noun naming a group of people, animals, or things |
| **Auxiliary verb** | A helping verb used to form tenses or questions |
| **TIPTOP** | Mnemonic for when to begin a new paragraph: Time, Idea, Place, Topic, Opinion, Person |
