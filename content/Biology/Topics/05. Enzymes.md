## What are Enzymes?

> **Enzymes** are **biological catalysts** — they speed up chemical reactions in living organisms without being used up themselves. They are **proteins** with a specific 3D shape.

- Produced by all living cells
- Speed up reactions by lowering the **activation energy**
- **Specific** — each enzyme only works on one substrate (or type of substrate)
- Not used up in the reaction — can be reused
- Sensitive to changes in temperature and pH

---

## The Lock and Key Model

- The enzyme has an **active site** — a specific-shaped region
- The substrate fits into the active site (like a key into a lock)
- Together they form an **enzyme-substrate complex**
- Products are released; enzyme is unchanged

> ⚠️ The shape of the active site is determined by the amino acid sequence → the shape is **specific** to one substrate.

### Induced Fit Model (Extension)
- Active site is flexible and changes shape slightly when substrate binds
- More accurate than lock and key

---

## Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

### 1. Temperature

| Temperature | Effect |
|------------|--------|
| Low | Slow rate — molecules have little kinetic energy, fewer collisions |
| Optimum (~37°C in humans) | Maximum rate |
| Above optimum | Enzyme **denatures** — active site changes shape permanently |

- **Denaturation:** permanent change in enzyme's 3D structure due to breaking of bonds
- At optimum: maximum enzyme-substrate complexes formed

> 💡 Rate increases with temperature up to optimum, then drops sharply to zero above it.

### 2. pH

| pH | Effect |
|----|--------|
| Optimum pH | Maximum rate |
| Too acidic/alkaline | Denaturation — H⁺ or OH⁻ ions break bonds in the active site |

- Each enzyme has a specific optimum pH
- e.g. Pepsin (stomach): optimum pH ~2; Amylase (saliva): optimum pH ~7

### 3. Substrate Concentration

- More substrate → more enzyme-substrate complexes → faster rate
- Up to a maximum (when all active sites are occupied = **saturation point**)
- After saturation: rate plateaus (adding more substrate has no effect)

### 4. Enzyme Concentration

- More enzyme → more active sites available → faster rate (if substrate is not limiting)
- Rate increases proportionally until substrate becomes the limiting factor

## Inhibitors

### Competitive Inhibitors
- Molecule similar shape to substrate
- Binds to **active site** → blocks substrate from binding
- Effect can be **reversed** by increasing substrate concentration
- e.g. Statins (compete with enzyme for cholesterol synthesis)

### Non-competitive Inhibitors
- Binds to a **different site** on the enzyme (allosteric site)
- Changes shape of active site → substrate cannot bind
- **Cannot** be reversed by increasing substrate concentration
- e.g. Heavy metal ions (lead, mercury)

| Feature | Competitive | Non-competitive |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| Binding site | Active site | Allosteric site |
| Reversed by more substrate? | Yes | No |
| Structural similarity to substrate | Yes | No |

## Intracellular vs Extracellular Enzymes

| Type | Location | Examples |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Intracellular** | Inside cells | Enzymes in cell respiration |
| **Extracellular** | Secreted outside cells | Digestive enzymes (amylase, protease, lipase) |

---

## Digestive Enzymes

| Enzyme | Substrate | Products | Where produced | Where acts |
|--------|-----------|---------|----------------|-----------|
| **Salivary amylase** | Starch | Maltose | Salivary glands | Mouth |
| **Pancreatic amylase** | Starch | Maltose | Pancreas | Small intestine |
| **Maltase** | Maltose | Glucose | Small intestine | Small intestine |
| **Pepsin** | Proteins | Peptides | Stomach | Stomach (pH 2) |
| **Trypsin** | Proteins/peptides | Peptides | Pancreas | Small intestine |
| **Peptidase** | Peptides | Amino acids | Small intestine | Small intestine |
| **Pancreatic lipase** | Lipids | Fatty acids + glycerol | Pancreas | Small intestine |

## Industrial Uses of Enzymes

| Enzyme | Use |
|--------|-----|
| **Amylase** | Making glucose syrup from starch (food industry) |
| **Protease** | Tenderising meat; biological washing powders |
| **Lipase** | Biological washing powders (remove fat stains) |
| **Isomerase** | Converting glucose to fructose (sweeter, used in diet foods) |
| **Lactase** | Producing lactose-free milk for lactose-intolerant people |
| **Pectinase** | Clearing fruit juices |
| **Rennet (chymosin)** | Clotting milk in cheese making |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Catalyst** | Substance that speeds up a reaction without being used up |
| **Active site** | Region of enzyme where substrate binds |
| **Enzyme-substrate complex** | Temporary structure formed when substrate binds to active site |
| **Denaturation** | Permanent change in enzyme's tertiary structure; active site changes shape |
| **Optimum** | Conditions at which enzyme activity is at its maximum |
| **Inhibitor** | Molecule that reduces enzyme activity |
