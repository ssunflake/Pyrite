## Biotechnology

> **Biotechnology** is the use of living organisms (or their products) to develop useful products or processes.

### Traditional Biotechnology
- **Fermentation** using microorganisms (yeast, bacteria)
- Brewing beer, making wine
- Baking bread
- Making cheese and yoghurt
- Producing antibiotics (*Penicillium* mould → penicillin)

---

## Fermentation

> **Fermentation** is the anaerobic breakdown of glucose by microorganisms to produce useful products.

### Yeast Fermentation
$$\text{Glucose} \rightarrow \text{Ethanol} + \text{Carbon dioxide}$$

- Used in: **brewing** (alcohol), **baking** (CO₂ makes dough rise)
- At temperatures above ~45°C, yeast enzymes **denature**

### Bacterial Fermentation
- *Lactobacillus* bacteria ferment lactose → **lactic acid**
- Used in: making **yoghurt** and **cheese**

---

## Industrial Fermenters

Large-scale fermenters maintain optimal conditions:

| Feature | Function |
|---------|---------|
| **Temperature control** (water jacket) | Maintains optimal enzyme temperature |
| **pH control** (acid/alkali added) | Maintains optimal enzyme pH |
| **Stirrer** | Mixes contents; even distribution of nutrients and temperature |
| **Sterile conditions** | Prevents contamination by other microorganisms |
| **Nutrient supply** | Carbon source, nitrogen source, vitamins, minerals |
| **Aeration** | O₂ supply for aerobic organisms (or sealed for anaerobes) |
| **Monitoring** | Sensors measure temperature, pH, O₂ |

### Products Made Using Fermenters
| Product | Microorganism | Use |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| **Penicillin** | *Penicillium* (fungus) | Antibiotic |
| **Insulin** | GM *E. coli* | Diabetes treatment |
| **Citric acid** | *Aspergillus* | Food flavouring/preservative |
| **Mycoprotein (Quorn™)** | *Fusarium* (fungus) | Meat substitute |
| **Biogas** | Methane-producing bacteria | Fuel |
| **Enzymes** (amylase, protease, lipase) | Various bacteria/fungi | Food industry, washing powder |

## Mycoprotein (Quorn™)

- Produced from *Fusarium venenatum* (a fungus)
- Grown in fermenters on glucose syrup
- High protein, low fat, high fibre
- Sustainable alternative to meat
- Harvested, heat-treated to kill fungus, processed into food products

### Advantages of Mycoprotein
- Uses less land and water than livestock
- Lower greenhouse gas emissions
- High protein content
- Suitable for vegetarians/vegans

## Genetic Modification (Genetic Engineering)

> **Genetic modification (GM)** is the insertion of a gene from one organism into another organism, giving the recipient organism a new characteristic.

### General Process
1. **Identify** the desired gene in a donor organism
2. **Cut out** the gene using **restriction enzymes** (cut DNA at specific sequences)
3. **Open a vector** (usually a **plasmid** from bacteria) using the same restriction enzyme → creates **sticky ends**
4. **Insert** the gene into the vector using **ligase** (DNA ligase joins sticky ends) → forms **recombinant DNA**
5. **Insert** vector into host organism (e.g. bacterium)
6. Host cell replicates → copies gene → produces desired protein

### Key Tools
| Tool | Function |
|------|---------|
| **Restriction enzymes** | Cut DNA at specific base sequences; create sticky ends |
| **DNA ligase** | Joins sticky ends → seals gene into vector |
| **Plasmid** | Small circular bacterial DNA; used as a vector |
| **Vector** | Carrier molecule (plasmid, virus) that transfers gene into host |
| **Recombinant DNA** | DNA containing genes from two different organisms |

## Examples of Genetic Modification

### 1. Human Insulin Production
- **Problem:** Diabetics need insulin; previously extracted from pigs/cattle (slightly different, costly)
- **Solution:**
  1. Human insulin gene isolated from human DNA
  2. Gene inserted into *E. coli* plasmid using restriction enzymes and ligase
  3. Recombinant bacteria grown in fermenters
  4. Bacteria produce **human insulin** in large quantities
  5. Insulin extracted, purified, and sold

### 2. Golden Rice
- Rice genetically modified to produce **β-carotene** (precursor of vitamin A)
- Genes from daffodil and bacteria inserted into rice genome
- **Purpose:** reduce vitamin A deficiency in developing countries where rice is the main food
- Advantage: cheap, sustainable source of vitamin A for populations that rely on rice

### 3. Bt Crops (pest-resistant plants)
- Gene from *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt) bacteria inserted into crops (maize, cotton)
- Bt gene produces a protein toxic to certain insects
- Reduces need for pesticides
- Advantage: lower pesticide use, higher yield
- Concern: possible harm to non-target insects

### 4. Herbicide-Resistant Crops
- Plants modified to tolerate herbicides (e.g. glyphosate-resistant soy)
- Farmers can apply herbicide to kill weeds without harming crop
- Concern: herbicide-resistant weeds evolving

### 5. Gene Therapy
- Replacing or correcting faulty genes to treat genetic diseases
- **Somatic gene therapy:** treat non-reproductive cells (effects don't pass to offspring)
- Example: SCID (severe combined immunodeficiency) treated by inserting functional immune gene into patient's cells
- **Germ-line gene therapy:** modify gametes/embryos (effects heritable) — currently banned in most countries

## Cloning

### Natural Clones
- Identical twins — from same fertilised egg splitting
- Asexual reproduction in plants (runners, tubers)

### Artificial Cloning — Plants
- **Tissue culture (micropropagation):**
  1. Small sample of plant tissue taken
  2. Grown on sterile nutrient medium with growth hormones
  3. Each cell divides → produces small plantlets
  4. Plantlets transferred to compost → grow into full plants
  5. All genetically identical to parent

### Artificial Cloning — Animals
#### Embryo Splitting
1. Embryo formed by IVF
2. Cells separated at early stage (2–8 cells)
3. Each cell can develop into a full embryo
4. Implanted into surrogate mothers
5. Genetically identical animals born

#### Nuclear Transfer (Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer — SCNT)
1. **Enucleated egg** (nucleus removed) from donor
2. **Nucleus** from somatic (body) cell of animal to be cloned inserted
3. Electric shock fuses nucleus and egg cell → stimulates division
4. Embryo develops → implanted into surrogate
5. Result: genetically identical to nucleus donor
- Famous example: **Dolly the sheep** (1996) — first cloned mammal

## Issues and Ethics of GM and Cloning

### Arguments FOR Genetic Modification
- Produces medicines (insulin, vaccines) cheaply and in large amounts
- Increases crop yields → helps feed growing world population
- Can reduce pesticide/herbicide use
- Could cure genetic diseases (gene therapy)

### Arguments AGAINST Genetic Modification
- **Environmental risks:** GM genes could spread to wild plants → "superweeds"
- **Biodiversity:** GM monocultures reduce crop genetic diversity
- **Unknown long-term effects:** on human health, ecosystems
- **Ethical concerns:** "playing God"; corporate control of food supply
- **Labelling and consumer choice:** people may not know they're eating GM food

### Issues with Cloning
- Very low success rate (SCNT)
- Cloned animals often have health problems
- Reduces genetic diversity (all clones susceptible to same diseases)
- Ethical: concerns about cloning humans

## Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

> PCR amplifies (makes millions of copies of) a specific DNA sequence from a tiny sample.

**Steps:**
1. DNA **denatured** (heated to ~95°C) — strands separate
2. **Primers** (short DNA sequences) bind to target sequence (annealing, ~55°C)
3. **DNA polymerase** (Taq polymerase) copies DNA (extension, ~72°C)
4. Cycle repeated 30–40 times → billions of copies

**Uses of PCR:**
- Forensic science (DNA fingerprinting)
- Medical diagnosis (detect viruses, e.g. COVID-19 PCR test)
- Ancient DNA analysis
- Paternity testing

---

## DNA Profiling (Genetic Fingerprinting)

1. DNA sample obtained (blood, hair, saliva)
2. PCR amplifies specific regions
3. DNA cut with restriction enzymes
4. **Gel electrophoresis** separates fragments by size
5. Pattern of bands = unique DNA profile (except identical twins)
6. Profiles compared to identify individuals

**Uses:**
- Criminal investigations
- Paternity testing
- Immigration disputes
- Identifying human remains
