## Ecological Terms

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Habitat** | Place where an organism lives | Pond, oak woodland, coral reef |
| **Population** | All individuals of one species in one area | All rabbits in a field |
| **Community** | All populations of different species living together in one area | All organisms in a pond |
| **Ecosystem** | Community of organisms and their non-living (abiotic) environment | A forest ecosystem |
| **Biosphere** | All ecosystems on Earth | All life on Earth |
| **Niche** | Role of a species in its ecosystem (what it eats, where it lives, etc.) | Owl = nocturnal predator of small mammals |
| **Producer** | Organism that makes its own food via photosynthesis | Green plants, algae |
| **Consumer** | Organism that feeds on other organisms | Animals |
| **Decomposer** | Organism that breaks down dead organic matter | Bacteria, fungi |

### Food Chains
- Show direction of energy flow
- Arrow means "is eaten by" / "energy flows to"

```
Grass → Rabbit → Fox → Decomposers
(producer) (primary consumer) (secondary consumer)
```

### Trophic Levels
| Level | Name | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1 | **Producer** | Makes own food (photosynthesis) |
| 2 | **Primary consumer** | Eats producer (herbivore) |
| 3 | **Secondary consumer** | Eats primary consumer (carnivore/omnivore) |
| 4 | **Tertiary consumer** | Eats secondary consumer |

### Food Webs
- Multiple food chains linked together
- More realistic than simple food chains
- Show interconnected feeding relationships

## Energy Flow through Ecosystems

### Why Energy is Lost Between Trophic Levels

Only about **10%** of energy passes from one trophic level to the next. The rest (~90%) is lost as:

| Loss | How |
|------|-----|
| **Heat** | Respiration (most energy) |
| **Faeces** | Undigested food not absorbed |
| **Urine** | Metabolic waste |
| **Movement** | Kinetic energy |
| **Dead organic matter** | Not eaten |

### Efficiency of Energy Transfer
$$\text{Efficiency (\%)} = \frac{\text{Energy at next trophic level}}{\text{Energy at current level}} \times 100$$

- Because of energy loss, food chains rarely have more than 4–5 levels
- More efficient for humans to eat plants directly than eat animals that ate plants

### Pyramids of Numbers, Biomass and Energy

| Pyramid | What it shows | Limitations |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **Numbers** | Number of organisms at each level | Can be inverted (e.g. one tree → many insects) |
| **Biomass** | Dry mass of organisms at each level | Usually pyramid shape; seasonal variation |
| **Energy** | Energy at each level | Always a true pyramid (most accurate) |

## Nutrient Cycling

### The Carbon Cycle

**Carbon enters the atmosphere as CO₂:**
- **Respiration** — all organisms release CO₂
- **Combustion** — burning fossil fuels and wood
- **Decomposition** — decomposers break down dead matter → CO₂

**Carbon leaves the atmosphere:**
- **Photosynthesis** — plants absorb CO₂

**Carbon in living organisms:**
- Plants fix CO₂ into glucose (photosynthesis)
- Animals eat plants → carbon transferred
- Organisms die → decomposers → CO₂ returned

**Long-term carbon stores:**
- **Fossil fuels** — formed from dead organisms over millions of years
- **Limestone** — formed from shells/bones of marine organisms
- **Peat** — partially decomposed plant matter in waterlogged conditions

---

### The Nitrogen Cycle

Plants need nitrogen for proteins and DNA (as nitrates $NO_3^-$)

| Process | Organisms involved | What happens |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Nitrogen fixation** | *Rhizobium* (in root nodules); lightning | N₂ gas → ammonium (NH₄⁺) or nitrate |
| **Nitrification** | *Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter* (nitrifying bacteria) | NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (nitrates) |
| **Decomposition** | Fungi, bacteria (decomposers/saprotrophs) | Proteins → amino acids → ammonium (NH₄⁺) |
| **Denitrification** | *Pseudomonas* (denitrifying bacteria) | NO₃⁻ → N₂ gas (reduces soil nitrogen) |
| **Absorption** | Plant roots | Nitrates absorbed by active transport |
| **Feeding** | Animals | Animals eat plants → nitrogen in proteins |
| **Excretion** | Animals | Urea → decomposed to ammonium |

> 💡 Key: Nitrogen gas (N₂) makes up 78% of air but is useless to most organisms — nitrogen fixation is essential!

## Abiotic and Biotic Factors

### Abiotic Factors (non-living)
| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Temperature | Affects enzyme activity, metabolic rate |
| Light intensity | Affects photosynthesis rate |
| Water availability | Affects plant growth, osmosis |
| pH | Affects enzyme activity |
| Humidity | Affects transpiration |
| Wind speed | Affects transpiration, seed dispersal |
| Salinity | Affects osmosis, distribution of organisms |

### Biotic Factors (living)
| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Predation | Reduces prey population |
| Competition | Reduces growth and survival |
| Disease | Reduces population |
| Food supply | Limits population growth |
| Parasitism | Harms host |
| Mutualism | Benefits both species |

## Population Growth

### Types of Growth
| Phase | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Lag phase** | Slow initial growth; organisms adapting |
| **Exponential (log) phase** | Rapid growth; resources plentiful |
| **Stationary phase** | Birth rate = death rate; carrying capacity reached |
| **Death phase** | Resources exhausted; population declines |

### Limiting Factors for Population Size
- Food/nutrient availability
- Space/territory
- Predation
- Disease
- Water
- Accumulation of toxic waste

### Predator-Prey Relationships
- Prey population increases → predator population increases (more food)
- Predator population increases → prey decreases (more eaten)
- Prey decreases → predators decrease (less food)
- Predators decrease → prey recovers
- **Cyclical fluctuations** in both populations (out of phase with each other)

## Ecological Sampling Techniques

### Quadrats
- Square frame placed randomly in habitat
- Count/estimate organisms within it
- Calculate: **% cover** or **population size = (mean per m²) × total area**
- Best for: slow-moving or stationary organisms (plants, limpets)

### Transects
- Line laid across a habitat
- Record organisms at regular intervals
- Show how communities change across an environmental gradient (e.g. shore → inland)
- **Belt transect:** quadrats placed along the line

### Mark-Release-Recapture (Lincoln Index)
- Used for mobile animals
$$N = \frac{n_1 \times n_2}{n_r}$$

Where:
- $N$ = estimated population size
- $n_1$ = number caught and marked first time
- $n_2$ = number caught second time
- $n_r$ = number of marked individuals in second catch

**Assumptions:**
- No births or deaths between captures
- No immigration or emigration
- Marks don't affect survival or behaviour
- Marks don't fall off
