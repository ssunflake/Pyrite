## Diffusion

> **Diffusion** is the net movement of molecules from a region of **higher concentration** to a region of **lower concentration**, down a concentration gradient, as a result of their random movement.

- **Passive process** — no energy (ATP) required
- Continues until equilibrium is reached
- Molecules move in both directions, but net movement is down the gradient

### Factors Affecting the Rate of Diffusion

| Factor | Effect on rate |
|--------|---------------|
| **Concentration gradient** | Steeper gradient → faster rate |
| **Temperature** | Higher temperature → more kinetic energy → faster rate |
| **Surface area** | Larger surface area → faster rate |
| **Thickness of membrane** | Thinner membrane → faster rate |
| **Size of molecule** | Smaller molecules → diffuse faster |

### Fick's Law
$$\text{Rate of diffusion} \propto \frac{\text{surface area} \times \text{concentration difference}}{\text{thickness of membrane}}$$

### Examples of Diffusion in Biology
- O₂ diffuses from alveoli into blood
- CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli
- Glucose diffuses from small intestine into blood
- O₂ diffuses from blood into respiring cells

## Osmosis

> **Osmosis** is the net movement of water molecules from a region of **higher water potential** to a region of **lower water potential**, through a **partially permeable membrane**.

- Water potential: measure of tendency of water to move
- Pure water has the **highest** water potential (0 kPa)
- Adding solutes **lowers** water potential (more negative value)
- Also a **passive process** — no energy required

### Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Partially permeable membrane** | Allows water through but not large solute molecules |
| **Water potential (Ψ)** | Tendency of water to move; pure water = 0 |
| **Solute potential** | Reduction in water potential caused by dissolved solutes |
| **Turgid** | Plant cell fully inflated with water; cell wall prevents bursting |
| **Plasmolysed** | Plant cell that has lost so much water the cell membrane pulls away from cell wall |
| **Flaccid** | Plant cell with no turgor pressure (neither turgid nor plasmolysed) |
| **Haemolysis** | Animal cell bursting due to excess water intake |
| **Crenation** | Animal cell shrinking due to water loss |

### Effects of Osmosis on Cells

**Plant cells:**
| Surrounding solution | Water movement | Cell state |
|---------------------|----------------|------------|
| Hypotonic (dilute) | Water enters cell | **Turgid** (firm) |
| Isotonic (same conc.) | No net movement | **Flaccid** |
| Hypertonic (concentrated) | Water leaves cell | **Plasmolysed** |

**Animal cells (e.g. red blood cells):**
| Surrounding solution | Water movement | Cell state |
|---------------------|----------------|------------|
| Hypotonic | Water enters | **Haemolysis** (bursts) |
| Isotonic | No net movement | Normal shape |
| Hypertonic | Water leaves | **Crenation** (shrinks) |

### Turgor Pressure in Plants
- Turgid cells push against each other → supports the plant
- Loss of turgor → **wilting**
- Cell wall prevents over-expansion of plant cells (unlike animal cells which have no wall)

## Active Transport

> **Active transport** is the movement of molecules or ions from a region of **lower concentration** to a region of **higher concentration** (against the concentration gradient), using **energy (ATP)** and **carrier proteins**.

- Requires metabolic energy (from respiration)
- Uses **carrier proteins** in the membrane
- Can move substances AGAINST the concentration gradient

### Differences from Diffusion and Osmosis

| Feature | Diffusion | Osmosis | Active Transport |
|---------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| Energy required? | No | No | **Yes (ATP)** |
| Direction | Down gradient | Down water potential | **Against gradient** |
| Carrier proteins? | Sometimes | No | **Yes** |
| Specificity | Low | Water only | **High (specific ions)** |

### Examples of Active Transport
- Absorption of glucose from gut into blood (especially when concentration in blood is already high)
- Absorption of mineral ions (e.g. nitrates, potassium) from soil into root hair cells
- Sodium-potassium pump in nerve cells
- Reabsorption of glucose in kidney tubules

## Exchange Surfaces

For efficient exchange, surfaces need:
- **Large surface area**
- **Thin membrane** (short diffusion distance)
- **Maintained concentration gradient** (e.g. blood flow, ventilation)
- **Moist surface** (for gas exchange)

### Examples of Exchange Surfaces

| Surface | What is exchanged | Adaptations |
|---------|------------------|------------|
| **Alveoli** (lungs) | O₂ and CO₂ | Folded, thin walls, rich blood supply |
| **Small intestine villi** | Glucose, amino acids | Finger-like projections, thin walls, blood supply, microvilli |
| **Root hair cells** | Water, mineral ions | Long extensions, large SA |
| **Leaf** (stomata) | CO₂, O₂, water vapour | Many stomata, air spaces, thin |
| **Fish gills** | O₂ and CO₂ | Many lamellae, counter-current flow |

## Key Definitions Summary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Diffusion** | Net movement down concentration gradient; no energy |
| **Osmosis** | Net movement of water down water potential gradient through partially permeable membrane |
| **Active transport** | Movement against gradient using ATP and carrier proteins |
| **Concentration gradient** | Difference in concentration between two regions |
| **Partially permeable** | Allows small molecules (water) through, not large ones |
