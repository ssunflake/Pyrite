## Transport Systems in Plants

Plants have two main transport tissues, both found in **vascular bundles**:

| Tissue | What it transports | Direction | Cells | Driving force |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|-------|--------------|
| **Xylem** | Water and mineral ions | Roots → stems → leaves (upward) | Dead, hollow, lignified | Transpiration pull |
| **Phloem** | Sugars (sucrose) and amino acids | Both directions (up and down) | Living, with sieve plates | Active loading (requires ATP) |

---

## Xylem

### Structure
- Made of **dead cells** with no cytoplasm
- **Hollow tubes** for unimpeded water flow
- Walls strengthened with **lignin** — provides support
- **No end walls** — continuous tube from root to leaf
- **Pits** in walls allow water to move sideways between cells

### Pathway of Water
**Soil water → root hair cell → cortex cells → xylem in root → xylem in stem → xylem in leaf → mesophyll cells → evaporates → exits through stomata**

### Water Uptake in Roots
1. Water enters root hair cells by **osmosis** (soil water has higher water potential)
2. Water moves from cell to cell across cortex by osmosis (down water potential gradient)
3. Water enters xylem — pulled up by transpiration pull

---

## Transpiration

> **Transpiration** is the loss of water vapour from the aerial parts of a plant, mainly through the stomata of leaves.

### The Transpiration Stream
- Water evaporates from mesophyll cells → lowers water potential in leaf
- Water drawn from xylem by osmosis
- Cohesion between water molecules → continuous column pulled up
- Creates **tension** that pulls water up from roots (**transpiration pull / cohesion-tension theory**)

### Factors Affecting Transpiration Rate

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Light intensity** ↑ | Stomata open wider → more water loss |
| **Temperature** ↑ | More kinetic energy → water evaporates faster |
| **Humidity** ↑ | Reduced concentration gradient → less diffusion → less water loss |
| **Wind speed** ↑ | Removes water vapour from around leaf → steeper gradient → more loss |

### Measuring Transpiration — Potometer
- Measures the **rate of water uptake** (approximately = transpiration rate)
- Air bubble moves along capillary tube
- Rate = distance moved / time

### Xerophytes — Plants Adapted to Dry Conditions
| Adaptation | How it reduces water loss |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Thick waxy cuticle | Reduces evaporation from surface |
| Sunken stomata | Humid air trapped near stomata → reduces gradient |
| Rolled leaves | Traps humid air, reduces air movement |
| Reduced leaf surface area (needles/spines) | Less area for evaporation |
| Fewer stomata | Fewer pores for water to escape |
| CAM photosynthesis | Stomata open at night (cooler, less evaporation) |

## Phloem and Translocation

> **Translocation** is the transport of dissolved organic substances (mainly sucrose) in the phloem, from **source** (where made/stored) to **sink** (where used/stored).

### Phloem Structure
- **Sieve tube elements** — living cells with perforated end walls (sieve plates)
- **Companion cells** — support sieve tubes; involved in active loading of sugars
- No nucleus in sieve tubes (companion cells carry out their functions)

### Source and Sink
| Source (produces sugar) | Sink (uses/stores sugar) |
|------------------------|-------------------------|
| Leaves (photosynthesis) | Growing roots and shoots |
| Storage organs (spring) | Fruits and seeds |
| | Storage organs (autumn) |

### Mass Flow Theory (how translocation works)
1. **Active loading** — sucrose actively loaded into phloem at source
2. This lowers water potential → water enters phloem by osmosis
3. Increased pressure → sugar solution moves toward sink
4. Sucrose removed at sink (used in respiration or converted to starch)
5. Water exits → pressure reduced at sink end

---

## Root Structure

- **Root hair cells** — long extensions increasing surface area for water/mineral absorption
- **Endodermis** — controls entry to xylem (Casparian strip forces water through cells)
- **Cortex** — stores starch; water moves through here to xylem

### Mineral Uptake
- Mineral ions absorbed by **active transport** (against concentration gradient)
- Requires ATP from root hair cells
- E.g. nitrate, potassium, magnesium, phosphate

---

## Comparing Xylem and Phloem

| Feature | Xylem | Phloem |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Cells alive? | Dead | Living |
| Direction | Upward only | Both directions |
| Contents | Water + minerals | Sucrose + amino acids |
| Cell walls | Thick, lignified | Thin |
| Energy required? | No (passive) | Yes (active loading) |
| End walls | None (open tubes) | Sieve plates |

---

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Transpiration** | Loss of water vapour from plant surfaces, mainly through stomata |
| **Translocation** | Movement of sugars in phloem from source to sink |
| **Cohesion-tension theory** | Water molecules stick together (cohesion), pulled up by evaporation (tension) |
| **Source** | Part of plant that produces or releases sugars |
| **Sink** | Part of plant that uses or stores sugars |
