## Variation

> **Variation** is the differences that exist between individuals of the same species.

### Types of Variation

| Type | Cause | Examples | Distribution |
|------|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Continuous** | Many genes + environment | Height, mass, skin colour, IQ | Normal distribution curve |
| **Discontinuous** | Usually one or few genes | Blood group, tongue rolling, attached/free earlobes, sex | Distinct categories, no intermediates |

### Causes of Variation

| Cause | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Genetic** | Different alleles inherited from parents; mutations; sexual reproduction produces new combinations |
| **Environmental** | Diet, climate, exercise, disease, accidents |
| **Both** | Most characteristics — e.g. height is genetic but affected by nutrition |

### Why Sexual Reproduction Produces Variation
- **Meiosis** creates gametes with unique combinations of alleles (crossing over, independent assortment)
- **Random fertilisation** — any sperm can fertilise any egg
- **Mutation** — rare changes to DNA

## Natural Selection

> **Natural selection** is the process by which organisms with advantageous characteristics survive and reproduce more successfully, passing on those characteristics to offspring.

### Darwin's Theory — Key Points
1. Organisms **overproduce** — more offspring than the environment can support
2. **Variation** exists within populations
3. **Competition** — for food, space, mates
4. **Survival of the fittest** — individuals with advantageous characteristics survive longer and reproduce more
5. **Inheritance** — advantageous traits passed to offspring
6. Over many generations → **evolution** — change in allele frequency in population

### Process of Natural Selection
```
Random mutation → new allele
↓
If advantageous → individual survives and reproduces more
↓
More offspring inherit the allele
↓
Allele frequency increases in population
↓
Over many generations → population changes (evolution)
```

### Examples of Natural Selection

#### Antibiotic Resistance in Bacteria
1. Random mutation → some bacteria resistant to antibiotic
2. Antibiotic kills non-resistant bacteria
3. Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce
4. Resistance allele passes to all offspring
5. Population becomes resistant

#### Peppered Moth (*Biston betularia*)
- Before industrial revolution: pale moths camouflaged on light tree bark → dark moths eaten by birds
- After industrial revolution: soot darkened trees → dark moths better camouflaged → pale moths eaten
- Dark moths increased in frequency in polluted areas
- After clean air acts: pale moths increased again

#### Sickle Cell Allele in Malaria Regions
- Heterozygous carriers (HbA/HbS) have some protection against malaria
- In malaria-endemic areas: carriers have higher survival → allele maintained at high frequency
- In non-malaria areas: sickle cell allele is disadvantageous → remains rare

## Adaptations

> An **adaptation** is a feature that makes an organism well-suited to its environment.

### Types of Adaptations
| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Structural** | Physical features | Thick fur (polar bear), long neck (giraffe), streamlined body (fish) |
| **Physiological** | Internal processes | Antifreeze proteins (fish), camel's ability to tolerate dehydration |
| **Behavioural** | Actions | Migration, hibernation, warning dances |

### Examples

**Polar bear:**
- Thick white fur — insulation and camouflage
- Fat layer — insulation and energy store
- Large paws — spread weight on ice, good for swimming
- Small surface area:volume ratio — reduces heat loss

**Cactus (xerophyte):**
- Spines instead of leaves — reduce SA, reduce water loss
- Thick waxy cuticle — reduces evaporation
- Swollen stem — water storage
- Shallow, wide roots — catch rainwater quickly
- CAM photosynthesis — stomata open only at night

**Deep sea fish:**
- Large eyes or bioluminescence — detecting light in darkness
- No swim bladder — no need at constant depth
- Flexible body — copes with high pressure

## Artificial Selection (Selective Breeding)

> **Selective breeding (artificial selection)** is the process by which humans select individuals with desirable characteristics to breed, over many generations.

### How it Works
1. Identify individuals with desired trait
2. Breed these together
3. Select offspring with best expression of trait
4. Repeat over many generations

### Examples
| Organism | Trait selected for | Result |
|---------|------------------|--------|
| **Dog** | Various traits | Hundreds of breeds — racing greyhounds, herding collies, etc. |
| **Cattle** | High milk yield OR muscle mass | Holstein (dairy), Hereford (beef) |
| **Wheat** | High yield, disease resistance, short stem | Modern high-yield dwarf varieties |
| **Maize** | Large cobs, pest resistance | Modern crop strains |
| **Roses** | Large flowers, colour, scent | Many decorative varieties |

### Disadvantages of Selective Breeding
- **Reduced genetic diversity** — all similar genetically
- **Inbreeding** — can expose harmful recessive alleles
- Vulnerable to new diseases (all individuals similarly susceptible)
- May involve selecting traits that harm animal welfare (e.g. breathing problems in bulldogs)

## Evolution and Speciation

### Evolution
> Gradual change in the inherited characteristics of a population over many generations.

- Driven by natural selection
- Results in changes in allele frequency in the gene pool
- Can lead to new species over long timescales

### Speciation
> **Speciation** is the formation of new species.

Occurs when:
1. **Population is separated** (geographical isolation — e.g. mountain range, sea)
2. Different environments → different selection pressures
3. Different mutations accumulate in each population
4. After long time → populations so different they **cannot interbreed**
5. Two distinct species have formed

### Evidence for Evolution
| Evidence | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Fossil record** | Shows gradual changes in organisms over time |
| **Comparative anatomy** | Homologous structures in related species (e.g. pentadactyl limb) |
| **Molecular biology** | Similar DNA sequences in related organisms |
| **Antibiotic resistance** | Observable natural selection happening now |
| **Selective breeding** | Shows how traits can change rapidly under selection |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Variation** | Differences between individuals of the same species |
| **Continuous variation** | Variation that shows a range of values with no distinct categories |
| **Discontinuous variation** | Variation that falls into distinct categories with no intermediates |
| **Natural selection** | Process by which organisms better adapted to environment survive and reproduce more |
| **Adaptation** | Feature that makes an organism better suited to its environment |
| **Evolution** | Change in inherited characteristics of a population over generations |
| **Speciation** | Formation of new species through isolation and divergence |
| **Selective breeding** | Humans selecting individuals with desired traits to breed |
