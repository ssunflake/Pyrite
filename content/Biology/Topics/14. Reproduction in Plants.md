## Types of Reproduction

| Type | Genetic material | Offspring | Examples |
|------|-----------------|-----------|---------|
| **Sexual** | Two parents; meiosis; fertilisation | Genetically different | Flowers, seeds |
| **Asexual** | One parent; mitosis | Genetically identical (clones) | Runners, bulbs, cuttings |

---

## Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants

### Structure of a Flower

| Part | Function |
|------|---------|
| **Petals** | Attract pollinators (colour, scent, nectar) |
| **Sepals** | Protect flower bud |
| **Stamens** (anther + filament) | Male reproductive organ; produce pollen |
| **Anther** | Where pollen grains (containing male gametes) are made |
| **Carpel** (stigma + style + ovary) | Female reproductive organ |
| **Stigma** | Sticky surface where pollen lands |
| **Style** | Connects stigma to ovary |
| **Ovary** | Contains ovules (female gametes inside) |
| **Ovule** | Contains egg cell |
| **Nectaries** | Produce nectar to attract insects |
| **Receptacle** | Base of flower |

---

## Pollination

> **Pollination** is the transfer of pollen from the **anther** of one flower to the **stigma** of a flower of the **same species**.

| Type | Agent | Self vs cross |
|------|-------|--------------|
| **Self-pollination** | Wind or own pollen | Same flower or same plant |
| **Cross-pollination** | Wind, insects, animals, water | Different plant of same species |

### Insect vs Wind Pollination

| Feature | Insect-pollinated | Wind-pollinated |
|---------|------------------|----------------|
| Petals | Large, colourful, scented | Small, dull, no scent |
| Nectaries | Present | Absent |
| Pollen | Sticky, spiky, small amount | Smooth, light, abundant |
| Stigma | Sticky, inside flower | Feathery, hangs outside |
| Anthers | Inside flower | Hang outside (exposed to wind) |
| Examples | Rose, sunflower, buttercup | Grass, wheat, maize |

## Fertilisation

1. Pollen grain lands on stigma → germinates
2. **Pollen tube** grows down the style
3. Tube enters ovule through **micropyle**
4. **Male nucleus** travels down tube → fuses with **egg cell nucleus**
5. **Fertilisation** — forms **zygote**
6. Zygote develops into **embryo**
7. Ovule develops into **seed**
8. Ovary wall develops into **fruit**

---

## Seed and Fruit Formation

| Structure after fertilisation | Develops into |
|------------------------------|--------------|
| Zygote | Embryo (radicle, plumule, cotyledon) |
| Ovule | Seed |
| Ovary wall | Fruit |

### Seed Structure
| Part | Function |
|------|---------|
| **Testa** (seed coat) | Protective covering |
| **Cotyledon(s)** | Food store (monocot: 1; dicot: 2) |
| **Embryo** | Radicle (becomes root) + plumule (becomes shoot) |

---

## Seed Dispersal

Seeds need to be dispersed (moved away from parent plant) to:
- Reduce competition for light, water, minerals
- Allow colonisation of new areas

| Method | Features of seed/fruit | Examples |
|--------|----------------------|---------|
| **Wind** | Light, wings, parachutes | Dandelion, sycamore, ash |
| **Animals (external)** | Hooks, spines | Burdock, goosegrass |
| **Animals (eating)** | Fleshy fruit, hard seed coat | Apple, cherry, tomato |
| **Water** | Waterproof, air pockets | Coconut |
| **Self-dispersal (explosive)** | Pod that dries and twists | Pea, lupin, squirting cucumber |

## Germination

> **Germination** is the process by which a seed develops into a seedling.

### Conditions Needed
- **Water** — activates enzymes; swells seed coat
- **Warmth** — needed for enzyme activity
- **Oxygen** — for aerobic respiration (energy for growth)
- (Light is NOT required for germination)

### Process
1. Seed absorbs water → testa splits
2. Radicle (embryo root) emerges first → anchors plant
3. Plumule (embryo shoot) emerges → grows upward
4. Food reserves (cotyledons) provide energy until photosynthesis begins
5. First true leaves develop → photosynthesis begins

## Asexual Reproduction in Plants

> **Asexual reproduction** produces genetically identical offspring (clones) from one parent.

### Natural Methods
| Method | Description | Examples |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Runners (stolons)** | Horizontal stem grows along ground → new plant at tip | Strawberry, spider plant |
| **Rhizomes** | Underground horizontal stems → new shoots | Iris, ginger |
| **Bulbs** | Underground food store with growing point | Daffodil, tulip, onion |
| **Tubers** | Swollen underground stems | Potato |
| **Corms** | Swollen stem base | Crocus |
| **Vegetative propagation** | Leaves, stems, roots develop into new plants | Bryophyllum (leaf plantlets) |

### Artificial Methods
| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Cuttings** | Take stem cutting; dip in rooting hormone; plant in compost |
| **Grafting** | Join shoot of one plant onto rootstock of another |
| **Tissue culture (micropropagation)** | Grow plant from small tissue sample in sterile nutrient medium |

### Advantages and Disadvantages of Asexual Reproduction
| Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-----------|--------------|
| Rapid multiplication | No genetic variation (all clones) |
| All offspring have desirable traits | Vulnerable to same disease (no resistant variants) |
| No need for pollination/fertilisation | Cannot adapt to changing environments |
| Cheaper for farmers | |

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Pollination** | Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma of same species |
| **Fertilisation** | Fusion of male and female gamete nuclei |
| **Germination** | Development of seedling from seed |
| **Dispersal** | Spreading of seeds away from parent plant |
| **Asexual reproduction** | Reproduction from one parent, producing genetically identical offspring |
